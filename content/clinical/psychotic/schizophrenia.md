# Overview

A 22-year-old former university student presents with an 18-month decline in functioning, characterized by auditory hallucinations, multiple delusions, disorganized speech, grossly disorganized behavior, and negative symptoms. The case highlights the distinction between schizophrenia and bipolar disorder with psychotic features, delusional disorder, and substance-induced psychotic disorder.

---

# Learning Objectives

- Identify the characteristic symptoms of schizophrenia and their minimum duration requirements
- Distinguish schizophrenia from bipolar disorder with psychotic features based on mood symptoms
- Differentiate schizophrenia from delusional disorder based on the presence of other psychotic features
- Understand the significance of Duration of Untreated Psychosis (DUP) in prognosis

# Presenting Complaint

Mr. and Mrs. Kumar brought their son Vikram to the emergency psychiatric clinic after he refused to eat for three days, claiming his mother was poisoning his food to "shut down the transmitter." Over the past eight months, Vikram had transformed from a promising engineering student into a withdrawn, suspicious young man who barely left his room.

The decline began subtly. Last year, Vikram started missing classes, telling his parents he needed to "decode the patterns" in his lecture notes. He spent hours staring at the ceiling, occasionally laughing to himself. When asked what was funny, he would say "the commentators" without elaborating. His hygiene deteriorated --- he showered once a week at most and wore the same clothes for days, claiming "they can't track me if I smell like everyone else."

Three months ago, Vikram became convinced that his neighbors were spies working for a foreign government. He covered his windows with aluminum foil and removed all electronic devices from his room except an old radio, which he said was the only "clean" communication channel. He accused his father of being a "double agent" after his father suggested he see a doctor. He stopped speaking to his younger sister entirely, believing she had been replaced by an impostor.

His sleep pattern became erratic --- awake all night, pacing and muttering, then sleeping fitfully during the day. He lost approximately 8 kg over four months because he believed the food supply was contaminated. His parents found notebooks filled with elaborate diagrams, random words circled repeatedly, and what appeared to be coded messages. He had written "THEY KNOW" on every page margin.

The breaking point came when Vikram smashed the family television, screaming that it was "broadcasting instructions into my brain." His father restrained him while his mother called for an ambulance. In the emergency department, Vikram was agitated, verbally hostile toward staff, and refused all blood draws, claiming they would "extract my DNA for the database."

---

# History of Present Illness

Vikram's illness began insidiously approximately 18 months ago, during his second year of university. Initially, he reported feeling "off" --- difficulty concentrating, vague sense of being watched, and increasing social withdrawal. He stopped attending group study sessions, telling friends he "needed to focus" but actually spending hours alone in his dorm room.

The prodromal phase lasted approximately 10 months. During this time, he experienced subtle perceptual disturbances --- colors seemed too bright, sounds seemed to have hidden meanings, and he felt increasingly disconnected from his surroundings. He described a sense that his thoughts were "not entirely my own" and that strangers on the street were looking at him with knowing expressions.

The first clear psychotic episode occurred 8 months ago when Vikram began hearing voices. Initially, he heard a single male voice commenting on his actions --- "He's opening the fridge now." Over time, multiple voices emerged: some criticized him ("You're pathetic, everyone knows"), others gave commands ("Don't eat that, it's poisoned"), and some engaged in conversations with each other about him as if he were not present. The voices were audible, clear, and perceived as coming from outside his head --- often from the walls or electrical outlets.

Delusions crystallized progressively. He developed persecutory delusions (neighbors spying on him, food being poisoned), referential delusions (believing TV news anchors were sending him personal messages), and delusions of control (believing his thoughts were being broadcast and manipulated via Wi-Fi). He also experienced thought insertion --- the belief that thoughts were being placed in his mind by external forces --- and thought broadcasting --- the belief that his thoughts were accessible to others.

His emotional expression flattened over time. Where he had once been animated and humorous, he became emotionally blunted, showing little reaction even when his mother cried. His motivation evaporated; he stopped pursuing hobbies, abandoned his academic goals, and spent most days in bed or pacing his room. His speech became increasingly disorganized --- he would jump between unrelated topics, use neologisms ("thoughtscramblers" for people he believed could read minds), and occasionally become completely incoherent during periods of high agitation.

He has shown no insight into his illness. When confronted with the irrationality of his beliefs, he becomes angry and accuses others of being part of the conspiracy. He has refused all previous suggestions for psychiatric evaluation.

# Differential Diagnosis

## Bipolar I Disorder, Current Episode Manic with Psychotic Features

Why Consider: Vikram shows psychotic symptoms and agitation.

Why Rule Out: There is no evidence of a manic or depressive episode. Vikram does not show elevated mood, increased energy, decreased need for sleep, pressured speech, or other manic symptoms. His psychosis is not mood-congruent, and there is no history of mood episodes. The psychotic symptoms persist continuously rather than occurring exclusively during mood episodes.

---

## Delusional Disorder

Why Consider: Vikram shows prominent delusions.

Why Rule Out: Vikram's delusions are not non-bizarre and fixed in isolation. He has multiple types of delusions (persecutory, referential, control), prominent hallucinations, disorganized speech, grossly disorganized behavior, and negative symptoms. Delusional disorder requires the absence of the other characteristic symptoms of schizophrenia, which are clearly present here.

---

## Substance-Induced Psychotic Disorder

Why Consider: Vikram shows psychotic symptoms in a young adult.

Why Rule Out: Vikram has no history of substance use. There is no temporal relationship between substance use and symptom onset. His family confirms he has never used alcohol, cannabis, or other substances. The chronic, progressive course is inconsistent with substance-induced psychosis, which typically resolves with abstinence.

---

## Schizoaffective Disorder

Why Consider: Vikram shows psychotic symptoms and some affective changes.

Why Rule Out: Schizoaffective disorder would require a major mood episode concurrent with the psychotic symptoms and mood symptoms present for a majority of the illness duration. Vikram has no mood episodes; his affective disturbance is negative symptoms (flat affect), not depression or mania.

# Correct Diagnosis

## Schizophrenia

### DSM-5-TR Explanation

The correct diagnosis is Schizophrenia because Vikram demonstrates two or more characteristic symptoms (hallucinations, delusions, disorganized speech, grossly disorganized behavior, negative symptoms) present for a significant portion of time during a 1-month period, with continuous signs of disturbance persisting for at least 6 months. The level of functional impairment is profound --- he has dropped out of university, severed social relationships, and is unable to care for himself. The symptoms are not attributable to substance use or another medical condition.

### Diagnostic Reasoning

Vikram meets DSM-5-TR criteria for Schizophrenia:

1. **Criterion A: Two or more of the following, each present for a significant portion of time during a 1-month period:**
   - Delusions (persecutory, referential, thought broadcasting, thought insertion)
   - Hallucinations (auditory hallucinations --- multiple voices commenting, criticizing, commanding)
   - Disorganized speech (tangentiality, neologisms, word salad)
   - Grossly disorganized or catatonic behavior (foil on windows, smashing TV, refusing food)
   - Negative symptoms (flat affect, avolition/apathy)

2. **Criterion B: Level of functioning in one or more major areas is markedly below the level achieved prior to the onset** (dropped out of university, unemployed, unable to care for himself)

3. **Criterion C: Continuous signs of the disturbance persist for at least 6 months** (18-month total duration with 8 months of active psychotic symptoms)

4. **Criterion D: Not attributable to schizoaffective disorder or depressive/bipolar disorder with psychotic features** (no mood episodes)

5. **Criterion E: Not attributable to substance use or another medical condition** (no substance use, no medical illness)

### Required Criteria

- Criterion A: Two or more characteristic symptoms (at least one must be delusions, hallucinations, or disorganized speech)
- Criterion B: Functional decline
- Criterion C: Continuous disturbance for at least 6 months
- Criterion D: Not schizoaffective or mood disorder with psychotic features
- Criterion E: Not substance-induced or due to medical condition

### Criteria Met

All criteria met. Vikram demonstrates at least two characteristic symptoms (delusions, hallucinations, disorganized speech, grossly disorganized behavior, negative symptoms) with at least one being delusions, hallucinations, or disorganized speech. Marked functional decline, continuous disturbance for 18 months, no mood episodes, no substance use.

### Criteria Not Met

None. All required criteria are met.

# Treatment Plan

## Psychotherapy

- Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Psychosis (CBTp) once acute symptoms stabilize
- Family therapy to reduce expressed emotion
- Social skills training
- Supported employment/education programs

## Medication

- Antipsychotic medication (second-generation antipsychotics such as risperidone, olanzapine, or aripiprazole are commonly first-line)
- Psychoeducation for family
- Assertive community treatment or case management for ongoing support

## Lifestyle

- Structured daily routine
- Supervised living if independent functioning is impaired
- Substance use prevention
- Regular physical health monitoring (metabolic side effects of antipsychotics)
- Social engagement through structured, low-stress activities

## Prognosis

Variable. Early intervention improves outcomes. Approximately 20--25% achieve full recovery; 50% have recurrent episodes with partial recovery; 20--30% have chronic, severe impairment. Negative symptoms and cognitive deficits are the strongest predictors of poor functional outcome. Family history of schizophrenia and longer duration of untreated psychosis are associated with worse prognosis.

# Common Student Mistakes

- Confusing the negative symptoms of schizophrenia (flat affect, avolition, alogia, anhedonia, asociality) with depression. The key distinction is that negative symptoms are stable, pervasive deficits in normal functioning without the dysphoria, guilt, or hopelessness of depression. A patient with negative symptoms may not feel sad --- they simply lack the capacity for emotional expression and motivation. Depression is a mood disorder; negative symptoms are deficit states.
- Confusing schizophrenia with dissociative identity disorder (formerly multiple personality disorder). The term "schizophrenia" comes from Greek roots meaning "splitting of the mind," but this refers to the splitting of psychic functions, not a split personality. These are entirely different conditions.

- **DUP = Duration of Untreated Psychosis**: Every month counts. Early intervention is critical for better functional recovery.

# Exam Pearls

- Duration of Untreated Psychosis (DUP) --- the time between onset of psychotic symptoms and initiation of adequate treatment --- is one of the strongest predictors of outcome in schizophrenia. Every month of untreated psychosis increases the risk of poorer functional recovery. This is why early intervention programs and reducing stigma to encourage help-seeking are critical public health priorities.
- The term "schizophrenia" was coined by Swiss psychiatrist Eugen Bleuler in 1908, replacing Emil Kraepelin's earlier term "dementia praecox." Bleuler chose the Greek roots schizein ("to split") and phrēn ("mind") to describe the splitting of psychic functions --- not a split personality, which is a common misconception. Dissociative Identity Disorder (formerly Multiple Personality Disorder) is an entirely different condition.
- This case is rated Advanced because schizophrenia has numerous differential diagnoses, and students must carefully evaluate the presence of mood episodes, substance use, and the specific quality of psychotic symptoms. The distinction from schizoaffective disorder, delusional disorder, and bipolar disorder with psychotic features requires systematic application of DSM criteria.

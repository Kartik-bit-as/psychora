# Overview

Ms. Rachel Kim is a 29-year-old registered nurse in the ICU who presents with a predictable, devastating pattern tied to her menstrual cycle. For approximately 7--10 days before her period begins, she experiences a profound transformation: severe irritability and rage, tearfulness, binge eating, "brain fog," and passive suicidal thoughts. She brought a meticulously kept calendar tracking 8 months of symptoms. The day her period starts, it is "like someone flips a switch" — the fog lifts, and she has two good weeks before the countdown begins again. Her husband has threatened to leave twice, and she made a medication error at work during her symptomatic phase. She has never experienced symptoms outside the luteal phase and is completely euthymic during the follicular phase.

---

# Learning Objectives

- Identify the diagnostic criteria for Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder (PMDD), including the cyclical nature and symptom domains
- Differentiate PMDD from Major Depressive Disorder, Generalized Anxiety Disorder, and Borderline Personality Disorder
- Recognize the importance of prospective symptom tracking across at least two cycles for diagnosis
- Understand that PMDD symptoms completely remit after menses onset, distinguishing it from chronic mood disorders
- Appreciate the functional impairment in PMDD and why it is not "just PMS"

# Presenting Complaint

Ms. Rachel Kim arrived composed and articulate, but her hands trembled slightly as she unfolded a meticulously kept calendar. She had tracked her symptoms for the past 8 months.

"I need you to understand --- I'm not like this normally," she said, pointing to the calendar. "See these two weeks? I'm fine. I'm a good nurse, a good wife, a functional human being. But these two weeks..." She traced her finger across the red-marked days. "I become destructive. To myself, to my marriage, to my job."

Rachel described a predictable, devastating pattern that has worsened since she stopped oral contraceptives 18 months ago (they had masked the symptoms). For approximately 7--10 days before her period begins, she experiences a profound transformation:

"Irritability doesn't cover it. I feel rage --- at my husband for breathing too loud, at a patient for needing too much, at myself for existing. I cry in the supply closet at work. I binge eat until I'm sick. I have thoughts that scare me --- like, 'everyone would be better off if I disappeared.'"

She emphasized the cyclical nature: "The day my period starts, it's like someone flips a switch. The fog lifts. I can think clearly again. I feel remorse for how I acted, but also relief --- like I've been let out of prison. Then I have two good weeks, and the countdown begins again."

Her husband, David, has threatened to leave twice. "He says he can't keep being my punching bag for half of every month. And I don't blame him. But I also can't control it. I've tried everything --- meditation, supplements, cutting out sugar. Nothing touches it."

# History of Present Illness

Rachel's symptoms are tightly linked to the luteal phase of her menstrual cycle.

**Onset:** Began in late teens but was masked by oral contraceptives from age 19 to 27. Symptoms emerged clearly after discontinuing hormonal birth control 18 months ago.

**Duration:** Cyclical --- approximately 7--10 days per cycle, every cycle, for 18 months since stopping OCPs. (Symptoms likely present but masked for 8+ years prior.)

**Progression:** Worsening since OCP discontinuation. Initially mild irritability and fatigue; now includes severe mood symptoms, cognitive impairment, and functional disruption.

**Triggers:** Symptoms emerge predictably during the luteal phase (after ovulation, before menses). No correlation with external stressors --- she can have a "good" month at work and still experience the syndrome.

**Impact on Life:**

- **Occupational:** Has called in sick 3 times during luteal phase (unprecedented in her career). Made a medication error last month during symptomatic phase --- caught by pharmacy, but terrifying. Considering leaving ICU for less demanding unit.

- **Marital:** Severe conflict during luteal phase. Rachel becomes critical, accusatory, and emotionally volatile. David has slept on the couch for 6 of the past 8 months during symptomatic weeks. Intimacy ceases entirely during luteal phase.

- **Social:** Cancels all plans during symptomatic weeks. Has lost two close friendships who felt "rejected."

- **Self-care:** Binge eating (primarily carbohydrates and sweets), alcohol consumption increases (2--3 drinks nightly vs. 1--2 weekly during follicular phase), exercise stops completely.

- **Cognitive:** Describes "brain fog" --- difficulty with calculations, memory lapses, trouble following complex protocols at work.

She has **never experienced symptoms outside the luteal phase**. During follicular phase (post-menses to ovulation), she is euthymic, energetic, and fully functional.

# Diagnostic Clues

- She brought a calendar. She tracked her symptoms for 8 months. The pattern is not random --- it is exquisitely predictable
- Her symptoms are severe enough to cause functional impairment --- marital conflict, occupational risk, social withdrawal --- but they resolve completely within a few days of menstruation onset

- This is not a personality disorder or a chronic mood disorder

- She has 8 months of documented cycles, symptoms in 5+ domains including at least one core affective symptom, and clear functional impairment

# Differential Diagnosis

## Major Depressive Disorder, Recurrent

Why Consider: Rachel has severe depressive symptoms, including suicidal ideation and functional impairment.

Why Rule Out: While Rachel has severe depressive symptoms, they are not present continuously. MDD requires symptoms to be present most of the day, nearly every day, for at least 2 weeks without the cyclical remission seen here. Rachel is completely symptom-free for approximately 2 weeks every month. This is incompatible with MDD. Additionally, her symptoms include prominent irritability, anxiety, and physical symptoms that are characteristic of PMDD's broader symptom profile rather than typical MDD. Treating her with standard antidepressant dosing for MDD would miss the hormonal timing and potentially be less effective.

## Generalized Anxiety Disorder

Why Consider: Rachel does experience anxiety during the luteal phase.

Why Rule Out: Rachel does experience anxiety during the luteal phase, but it is not generalized --- it is cyclical and confined to the premenstrual period. GAD requires excessive anxiety and worry more days than not for at least 6 months about multiple domains. Rachel's anxiety is time-limited, predictable, and tied to hormonal fluctuations. During her follicular phase, she has no anxiety symptoms. The anxiety is a component of PMDD, not a standalone anxiety disorder.

## Borderline Personality Disorder

Why Consider: BPD features affective instability, interpersonal conflict, and self-deprecating thoughts --- all present in Rachel during her luteal phase.

Why Rule Out: BPD symptoms are chronic, pervasive, and not tied to a biological cycle. Rachel's symptoms are exquisitely cyclical, completely remitting during follicular phase. She has no history of self-harm, no chronic emptiness, no identity disturbance, and no frantic efforts to avoid abandonment outside the luteal phase. Her personality structure is stable; her symptoms are state-dependent and hormonally mediated. The cyclicality is the critical discriminator.

## Premenstrual Syndrome (PMS)

Why Consider: Rachel has premenstrual symptoms.

Why Rule Out: PMS involves mild physical and emotional symptoms that do not cause significant functional impairment. Rachel's symptoms are severe, include suicidal ideation, and cause occupational and marital crisis --- this exceeds PMS.

## Dysmenorrhea

Why Consider: Rachel has physical symptoms during her cycle.

Why Rule Out: Rachel has physical symptoms, but they are not the primary complaint. Her dominant symptoms are mood-based.

## Perimenopausal Depression

Why Consider: Hormonal fluctuations can cause mood symptoms.

Why Rule Out: Rachel is 29 with regular cycles; not perimenopausal.

## Thyroid Disorder

Why Consider: Thyroid dysfunction can cause mood symptoms.

Why Rule Out: TSH normal. Symptoms are cyclical, not continuous.

## Anemia

Why Consider: Rachel has iron-deficiency anemia.

Why Rule Out: Iron deficiency present but managed; does not explain cyclical mood pattern.

# Correct Diagnosis

**Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder**

## DSM-5-TR Explanation

Rachel meets full criteria for Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder (PMDD):

## Diagnostic Reasoning

1. **At least 5 symptoms in the final week before menses onset** --- she has 11 symptoms across affective, behavioral, cognitive, and somatic domains.

2. **At least one symptom from core affective group (A1--A4)** --- she has marked affective lability (A1), marked irritability/anger (A2), marked depressed mood (A3), and marked anxiety (A4).

3. **Symptoms improve within a few days after menses onset** --- she describes a "switch flipping" when her period begins.

4. **Minimal symptoms in the week post-menses** --- she is fully euthymic and functional during follicular phase.

5. **Symptoms cause clinically significant distress and impairment** --- marital conflict, occupational risk, social withdrawal, binge eating.

6. **Confirmation across at least two symptomatic cycles** --- she has 8 months of documented cycles.

7. **Not attributable to substance, medical condition, or other disorder** --- while she has PCOS, her symptoms are specifically tied to the luteal phase and not explained by PCOS alone.

## Required Criteria

- Criterion A: In the majority of menstrual cycles, at least five symptoms must be present in the final week before the onset of menses, start to improve within a few days after the onset of menses, and become minimal or absent in the week postmenses
- Criterion A1--A4: At least one of the symptoms must be one of the following: (1) marked affective lability, (2) marked irritability or anger or increased interpersonal conflicts, (3) marked depressed mood or feelings of hopelessness or self-deprecating thoughts, (4) marked anxiety or tension or feelings of being keyed up or on edge
- Criterion A5--A11: Additional symptoms may include: (5) decreased interest in usual activities, (6) subjective difficulty in concentration, (7) lethargy or easy fatigability or marked lack of energy, (8) marked change in appetite or overeating or specific food cravings, (9) hypersomnia or insomnia, (10) a subjective sense of being overwhelmed or out of control, (11) physical symptoms such as breast tenderness or swelling, joint or muscle pain, a sensation of "bloating," or weight gain
- Criterion B: The symptoms are associated with clinically significant distress or interference with work, school, usual social activities, or relationships with others
- Criterion C: The disturbance is not merely an exacerbation of the symptoms of another disorder
- Criterion D: Criterion A should be confirmed by prospective daily ratings during at least two symptomatic cycles
- Criterion E: The symptoms are not attributable to the physiological effects of a substance or another medical condition

## Criteria Met

- Criterion A: 11/11 symptoms present in final week before menses (minimum 5 required)
- Criterion A1--A4: All four core affective symptoms met (affective lability, irritability/anger, depressed mood, anxiety)
- Criterion A5--A11: All additional symptoms present
- Criterion B: Significant distress and impairment (marital conflict, medication error at work, social withdrawal, considering job change)
- Criterion C: Not an exacerbation of another disorder (symptoms completely remit post-menses)
- Criterion D: Confirmed by 8 months of prospective daily ratings
- Criterion E: Not attributable to substance or medical condition (PCOS does not explain cyclical pattern)

## Criteria Not Met

- None. All criteria for Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder are met.

# Treatment Plan

## Psychotherapy

- **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT):** Specifically adapted for PMDD. Targets catastrophic thinking during luteal phase, helps develop coping strategies for symptom management, and addresses interpersonal conflict patterns.

- **Interpersonal Therapy (IPT):** Addresses role transitions (considering pregnancy, job change) and relationship strain.

## Medication

- **SSRIs** are first-line and can be administered either continuously or luteal-phase only (days 14--28 of cycle). Sertraline, fluoxetine, and escitalopram have the strongest evidence. Luteal-phase dosing is often preferred to minimize side effects.

- **Hormonal interventions:** Combined oral contraceptives (especially those containing drospirenone) can suppress ovulation and reduce symptoms. GnRH agonists or surgical options reserved for severe, treatment-resistant cases.

- **Calcium, magnesium, vitamin B6:** Modest evidence; can be adjunctive.

- *All medication decisions by prescribing clinician only.*

## Lifestyle

- Regular aerobic exercise (especially during follicular phase to build resilience)
- Reduced caffeine and alcohol during luteal phase
- Complex carbohydrate-rich diet to stabilize mood and reduce cravings
- Sleep hygiene
- Stress reduction techniques (yoga, meditation) --- most effective when practiced consistently, not just during symptomatic phase
- Symptom tracking continues to guide treatment and validate experience

## Prognosis

Good to excellent with appropriate treatment. PMDD is highly responsive to SSRIs and hormonal interventions. Rachel's insight, meticulous tracking, and motivation are strong protective factors. However, she faces decisions about pregnancy (SSRIs generally compatible with pregnancy but require discussion) and career (ICU may be too high-stakes during symptomatic weeks). Long-term management is typically necessary as PMDD tends to persist until menopause.

# Common Student Mistakes

- Diagnosing MDD because the depressive symptoms are severe, while ignoring the cyclical pattern. Students see suicidal ideation, binge eating, and functional impairment and reflexively select MDD. However, timing is everything in PMDD. If symptoms completely remit after menses and the patient is euthymic for 1--2 weeks, MDD is incorrect.
- Dismissing PMDD as "just PMS" and underestimating its functional impact.
- Failing to ask about menstrual cycle timing or request prospective symptom tracking.
- Attributing symptoms to a personality disorder without recognizing the biological cyclicality.

---

# Memory Tricks

- **"The Two-Week Switch"**: PMDD patients are two different people --- one for 2 good weeks, one for 2 bad weeks. The switch flips when menses begins.
- **"5 in the final week, gone in a few days"**: 5+ symptoms in the week before menses, improve within days of onset, minimal post-menses.
- **"Track it to crack it"**: Prospective daily ratings for at least 2 cycles are required for diagnosis. A calendar is worth more than a thousand words.
- **Think of a tide**: PMDD is like a tide that comes in predictably every month and recedes just as predictably --- unlike chronic disorders where the water stays high.

---

# Exam Pearls

- PMDD is one of the most underdiagnosed and dismissed conditions in women's mental health. Patients like Rachel are often told "it's just PMS" or "all women get moody before their period" for years before receiving appropriate treatment.
- The functional impairment in PMDD is real and severe --- studies show it is comparable to MDD in terms of disability.
- The diagnostic key is prospective symptom tracking across at least two cycles. Do not rely on retrospective recall, which is unreliable.
- Never dismiss a patient who says "something is wrong with my cycle" --- they are usually right.
- SSRIs can be given luteal-phase only, which minimizes side effects and is often preferred by patients.

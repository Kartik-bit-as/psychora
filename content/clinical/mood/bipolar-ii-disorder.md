# Overview

He describes periods of intense creative productivity, reduced need for sleep, and elevated mood lasting one to two weeks, followed by crashes into "a dark pit" lasting three to four weeks. He has never connected these cycles as part of a single condition, instead viewing the highs as his "true self" and the lows as personal failures he needed to push through. # Presenting Complaint

Mr. Julian Reyes scheduled the appointment after a tense weekend argument with his partner, Maya. He arrived ten minutes late, slightly breathless, wearing a paint-splattered denim jacket over a wrinkled button-down. He talked rapidly during intake, occasionally interrupting himself with tangential observations about the clinic's color scheme.

"Okay, so," he began, "Maya says I need to talk to someone because she's worried I'm going to ruin my life. And honestly? Maybe I am. But the thing is, when I'm up, it doesn't feel like ruining anything. It feels like finally being who I'm supposed to be."

Julian described a pattern stretching back to his early twenties --- periods of intense creative productivity, reduced need for sleep, and elevated mood that would last one to two weeks, followed by crashes into "a dark pit" lasting three to four weeks. He had never connected these cycles as part of a single condition, instead viewing the highs as his "true self" and the lows as personal failures he needed to push through.

The current concern, he explained, stemmed from a recent hypomanic period three weeks ago during which he accepted six new client projects simultaneously, spent $4,200 on photography equipment he couldn't afford, and stayed awake for three consecutive nights convinced he was on the verge of a breakthrough artistic vision. "I felt like I had tapped into something cosmic," he said. "Like the universe was feeding me ideas directly."

That episode ended abruptly. For the past two weeks, he has been unable to complete any of the projects he enthusiastically accepted. He sleeps 10--12 hours daily, has stopped showering, and told Maya he "wishes he could just hibernate until spring." He missed a client deadline last week for the first time in his career.

"I keep promising Maya I'll get it together," he said quietly, the energy draining from his voice. "But I'm starting to think there's something actually wrong with my brain. Not just laziness or whatever."

# History of Present Illness

Julian's mood cycles have occurred with increasing frequency over the past six years, though he had previously attributed them to creative temperament or seasonal variation.

**Onset:** First noticed at age 22 during his senior year of art school. A two-week period of intense painting, minimal sleep, and social expansiveness was followed by a month of withdrawal, self-doubt, and academic probation risk. He assumed it was stress.

**Duration:** Cycles have shortened from approximately annual to every 6--10 weeks over the past two years. Hypomanic episodes last 4--10 days; depressive episodes last 2--5 weeks.

**Progression:** Increasing functional impact. Early episodes were disruptive but contained within academic or artistic contexts. Recent episodes have affected finances, relationships, and professional reputation. During the most recent hypomanic period, he made impulsive romantic advances toward a client (rejected, but professionally embarrassing) and drove to a neighboring state at 2:00 AM to photograph a "perfectly lit abandoned factory."

**Triggers:** Stressful deadlines, creative breakthroughs, and relationship conflicts often precede hypomanic periods. Depressive episodes often follow the collapse of hypomanic projects or social consequences of impulsive behavior.

**Impact on Life:**

- **Occupational:** Unpredictable productivity. Highly praised work during hypomanic periods; missed deadlines, withdrawn communication, and client complaints during depressive periods. Income is erratic.

- **Financial:** Significant credit card debt from impulsive purchases during elevated periods. Currently owes $8,400 across three cards.

- **Relational:** Maya has threatened to end the relationship twice. She describes feeling like she is dating "two different people" and cannot predict which Julian will wake up each morning.

- **Social:** Expansive and charismatic during hypomania --- makes many new acquaintances, joins groups, plans elaborate events. Withdraws completely during depression, ghosting new friends who then feel rejected.

- **Self-care:** Neglected during both poles --- hyperfocused on projects during hypomania (forgetting meals, sleep); apathetic during depression.

He has never experienced psychotic symptoms, required hospitalization, or had symptoms severe enough to warrant emergency intervention.

# Differential Diagnosis

## Cyclothymic Disorder

Why Consider: Julian has recurrent mood cycling with both elevated and depressive periods.

Why Rule Out: Cyclothymic Disorder requires numerous periods of hypomanic symptoms and numerous periods of depressive symptoms for at least 2 years with no more than 2 months symptom-free, and no full major depressive episode or full hypomanic episode during the first 2 years. Julian's depressive episodes meet full criteria for major depressive episodes, and his hypomanic episodes meet full hypomanic criteria. Cyclothymia is a diagnosis of subthreshold symptoms over long duration --- Julian has crossed the threshold into full syndromal episodes.

## Bipolar I Disorder, Current Episode Depressed

Why Consider: Julian has a clear bipolar pattern with recurrent mood episodes.

Why Rule Out: Bipolar I requires at least one manic episode --- a distinct period of abnormally elevated, expansive, or irritable mood lasting at least 1 week (or any duration if hospitalized), with marked impairment in functioning, need for hospitalization, or psychotic features. Julian's elevated periods, while impactful, have never reached manic severity. He has not been hospitalized, has not had psychotic symptoms, and has retained some level of functioning even during his most elevated states. His episodes are hypomanic, not manic --- the critical distinction.

## Major Depressive Disorder, Recurrent, with Atypical Features

Why Consider: Julian's depressive episodes meet MDD criteria, and his current presentation is depressive.

Why Rule Out: While Julian's depressive episodes meet MDD criteria, the diagnosis of MDD cannot be made if there is a history of hypomanic or manic episodes. The presence of hypomania shifts the diagnosis to the bipolar spectrum. Additionally, Julian's poor response to sertraline (agitation, mixed features) is a classic red flag for bipolarity that is often missed in patients incorrectly diagnosed with unipolar depression. Treating bipolar depression with antidepressant monotherapy can induce mood elevation or mixed states.

## ADHD

Why Consider: Julian is creative and distractible, with inconsistent productivity.

Why Rule Out: His symptoms are episodic and mood-based rather than chronic and attention-based. No childhood history of ADHD symptoms. The pattern of distinct hypomanic and depressive episodes is inconsistent with ADHD.

## Borderline Personality Disorder

Why Consider: Mood lability is present, and relationship instability is reported.

Why Rule Out: BPD mood shifts are typically rapid (hours), reactive to interpersonal stressors, and accompanied by chronic emptiness, identity disturbance, and self-harm/impulsivity across domains. Julian's mood shifts are episodic and sustained (days to weeks) rather than rapid and reactive. His self-image is relatively stable outside episodes.

## Substance-Induced Mood Disorder

Why Consider: Cannabis and alcohol use are present.

Why Rule Out: Cannabis use is minimal (2--3 times monthly) and alcohol use is primarily during depressive episodes. Neither is temporally linked to the onset or maintenance of mood episodes. The cyclical pattern predates and exceeds substance use.

## Thyroid Disorder

Why Consider: Mood disorders can be secondary to thyroid dysfunction.

Why Rule Out: No thyroid dysfunction reported. Mood cycles are not consistent with thyroid dysfunction patterns.

# Correct Diagnosis

**Bipolar II Disorder, Current Episode Depressed**

## DSM-5-TR Explanation

Julian meets full criteria for Bipolar II Disorder. The diagnosis requires:

## Diagnostic Reasoning

1. **At least one hypomanic episode** --- Julian has had numerous episodes lasting 4--10 days with elevated mood, decreased need for sleep, increased talkativeness, flight of ideas, distractibility, increased goal-directed activity, and excessive involvement in pleasurable/risky activities. These episodes are not severe enough to cause marked impairment requiring hospitalization and no psychotic features are present --- distinguishing them from manic episodes.

2. **At least one major depressive episode** --- Julian has recurrent depressive episodes lasting 2--5 weeks with depressed mood, anhedonia, fatigue, hypersomnia, worthlessness, and diminished concentration. These meet full MDE criteria.

3. **The hypomanic and depressive episodes cause clinically significant distress and impairment** --- evident in financial debt, relationship instability, and occupational inconsistency.

4. **No history of manic episodes** --- crucial distinguishing feature. His elevated periods, while problematic, have never reached the severity, duration, or functional devastation of mania.

5. **Symptoms not better explained by substance, medical condition, or other disorder** --- cannabis use is minimal; thyroid function normal.

## Required Criteria

- Criterion A: At least one hypomanic episode
- Criterion B: At least one major depressive episode
- Criterion C: Symptoms cause clinically significant distress or impairment
- Criterion D: Not better explained by schizoaffective disorder, schizophrenia, schizophreniform disorder, delusional disorder, or other specified/unspecified schizophrenia spectrum and other psychotic disorder
- Criterion E: Never had a manic episode

## Criteria Met

- Criterion A: Multiple hypomanic episodes (4--10 days, elevated mood, decreased sleep, increased talkativeness, flight of ideas, distractibility, increased goal-directed activity, risky behavior)
- Criterion B: Recurrent major depressive episodes (2--5 weeks, depressed mood, anhedonia, fatigue, hypersomnia, worthlessness, diminished concentration)
- Criterion C: Significant distress and impairment (financial debt $8,400, relationship threats, missed deadlines, occupational inconsistency)
- Criterion D: No psychotic features

# Treatment Plan

## Psychotherapy

- **Interpersonal and Social Rhythm Therapy (IPSRT):** Specifically designed for bipolar disorders. Focuses on stabilizing daily rhythms (sleep, meals, activity) that help prevent mood cycling. Highly relevant given Julian's irregular freelance schedule.

- **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT):** Targets cognitive patterns during depressive episodes and helps identify early warning signs of hypomania. Useful for addressing his romanticization of hypomanic periods.

- **Family-Focused Therapy:** Maya is significantly impacted; involving her in treatment can improve communication, reduce expressed emotion, and build a shared understanding of early intervention.

## Medication

- **Mood stabilizers** --- lamotrigine is particularly effective for bipolar depression prevention and has a favorable side effect profile for creative individuals concerned about cognitive blunting.

- **Second-generation antipsychotics** (e.g., quetiapine, lurasidone) have evidence for bipolar depression.

- **Lithium** remains gold-standard for bipolar disorder with strong anti-suicidal properties, though Julian may resist due to concerns about creativity.

- **Antidepressant monotherapy is contraindicated** --- risk of mood elevation, mixed states, or rapid cycling.

- *Individual medication selection by prescribing clinician only.*

## Lifestyle

- Strict sleep hygiene and regular sleep-wake schedule (sleep deprivation is a known trigger for hypomania)
- Limit caffeine and alcohol
- Regular exercise (moderate intensity, not excessive)
- Mood tracking (daily ratings) to identify prodromal symptoms
- Financial safeguards during elevated periods (Maya as co-signer, spending limits)
- Structured work schedule with realistic project loads

## Prognosis

Guarded to fair. Bipolar II is often underdiagnosed and undertreated. Julian has strong creative talents and a supportive partner, but the progressive worsening of cycling frequency is concerning. Early, appropriate treatment (mood stabilizer + psychotherapy) can significantly improve outcomes. Risk of progression to more rapid cycling or mixed features exists if inappropriately treated with antidepressants alone. Long-term medication adherence may be challenging given his fear of losing creativity.

# Common Student Mistakes

- Diagnosing Bipolar II based on hypomania alone, without confirming a major depressive episode. Some learners see the elevated periods and immediately select Bipolar II, forgetting that the diagnosis requires at least one major depressive episode in addition to hypomania.
- Confusing hypomania with normal creativity or productivity. Hypomania is inherently unstable and always crashes, causing significant impairment.
- Missing the diagnosis because the patient is currently in a depressive episode and the history of hypomania is not elicited.
- Prescribing antidepressant monotherapy without screening for bipolarity, which can worsen the condition.

# Memory Tricks

- **"Two poles, not too high"**: Bipolar II has two poles (depression + hypomania) but never reaches the extreme highs of mania.
- **"Hypo = below"**: Hypomania is "below" mania in severity --- significant but not psychotic, not hospitalized, not fully impaired.
- **Think of a bouncing ball**: In Bipolar II, the ball bounces up to a moderate height (hypomania) and crashes down deep (depression), but never hits the ceiling (mania).

# Exam Pearls

- The "creative hypomania" trap is one of the most dangerous features of Bipolar II. Patients often resist treatment because they associate their elevated periods with productivity, charisma, and artistic identity. However, hypomania is inherently unstable --- it always crashes. The goal of treatment is not to eliminate all elevation but to dampen the amplitude of cycles so that creativity becomes sustainable rather than destructive.
- Always verify both poles when considering Bipolar II. If a patient has hypomania without MDE, the diagnosis is different (or requires further evaluation).
- Poor response to antidepressants (agitation, mixed features, rapid cycling) is a red flag for underlying bipolar spectrum disorder.
- Family history of bipolar disorder in first-degree relatives significantly increases diagnostic confidence.

# Overview

Mr. Harold Chen is a 52-year-old retired police officer who presents with a profound sense of emptiness that has deepened over the past eight months since his disability retirement. He was brought to the appointment by his daughter, Melissa, who reports that he "hasn't been himself since the shooting." Harold describes waking each morning with a sense of dread, lying in bed for hours, and eventually forcing himself up only because his daughter calls to check on him. He has stopped visiting his favorite diner, stopped fishing, and stopped everything that once gave his life structure. He keeps thinking about a 19-year-old bystander who died during an armed robbery response, believing that if he couldn't save that child, he is no good to anyone. He has had passive suicidal ideation but denies active intent, plan, or means. He refuses to keep firearms in the house.

---

# Learning Objectives

- Identify the diagnostic criteria for Major Depressive Disorder, Single Episode, Severe
- Differentiate MDD from PTSD, Persistent Depressive Disorder, and Adjustment Disorder in the context of trauma history
- Recognize that a trauma precipitant does not automatically indicate PTSD as the primary diagnosis
- Understand the concept of severity specifiers in MDD
- Appreciate the presentation of depression in high-stigma populations such as first responders

# Presenting Complaint

Mr. Harold Chen arrived accompanied by his daughter, Melissa, who did most of the talking during the first few minutes. Harold sat slumped in the chair, hands folded in his lap, eyes fixed on the floor. He wore a stained sweatshirt and sweatpants --- clothing his daughter later mentioned he had been wearing for three days.

"Dad hasn't been himself since the shooting," Melissa said, glancing at her father with concern. "He used to be the strongest person I knew. Now I can barely get him to shower."

Harold spoke slowly when prompted, his voice a low monotone. "I was a good cop for 23 years," he said. "I thought I was helping people. Then I watched a 19-year-old kid bleed out because I couldn't get to him in time. And now..." He paused for a long moment. "Now I can't even help myself."

He described a profound sense of emptiness that has deepened over the past eight months since his disability retirement. He wakes each morning with a sense of dread, lies in bed for hours, and eventually forces himself up only because his daughter calls to check on him. He has stopped visiting his favorite diner, stopped fishing, stopped everything that once gave his life structure.

"I keep thinking about that kid," he said. "Every day. I see his face. And I think --- if I couldn't save him, what good am I to anyone?"

# History of Present Illness

Harold's depression began following a traumatic on-duty shooting but has persisted and generalized beyond the trauma context.

**Onset:** Approximately 8 months ago, immediately following a critical incident in which a young bystander died during an armed robbery response. Harold was not physically injured but witnessed the death. He was placed on administrative leave, then disability retirement.

**Duration:** Continuous for 8 months with no significant remission.

**Progression:** Worsening. Initial symptoms were primarily intrusive memories and guilt related to the shooting (suggesting acute stress/trauma response). Over the past 4 months, symptoms have generalized into a pervasive depressive syndrome with prominent anhedonia, psychomotor retardation, and hopelessness.

**Triggers:** The shooting was the precipitant, but symptoms have persisted and worsened despite temporal distance from the trauma. Recent additional stressor: his ex-wife remarried last month, which intensified his sense of isolation.

**Impact on Life:**

- **Occupational:** Completely unable to work. Disability retirement was approved based on psychological impairment.

- **Social:** Near-total isolation. Has not seen friends from the force in 6 months. Declines all invitations. Daughter is his only regular contact.

- **Familial:** Relationship with children strained. Son has stopped calling because Harold "just brings the mood down." Melissa visits weekly but feels helpless.

- **Self-care:** Severely neglected. Showers 1--2 times weekly. Meals irregular --- often skips breakfast and lunch, eats whatever is available without pleasure. Has lost 11 kg.

- **Sleep:** Initial insomnia (difficulty falling asleep due to rumination) has shifted to hypersomnia --- sleeps 10--12 hours, still feels exhausted.

He has had **passive suicidal ideation** ("I wouldn't mind if I didn't wake up") but denies active intent, plan, or means. He refuses to keep firearms in the house (previously owned several; Melissa removed them at his request).

He denies any history of manic or hypomanic episodes.

# Differential Diagnosis

## Posttraumatic Stress Disorder

Why Consider: Harold experienced a Criterion A trauma (witnessed death of a bystander during armed robbery response) and has trauma-related guilt.

Why Rule Out: While Harold experienced a Criterion A trauma and has trauma-related guilt, his current symptom profile does not meet PTSD criteria. PTSD requires intrusion symptoms (nightmares, flashbacks), avoidance of trauma reminders, negative alterations in cognition/mood (trauma-specific), and marked alterations in arousal/reactivity. Harold's presentation is dominated by a pervasive depressive syndrome rather than trauma-specific symptom clusters. His guilt is generalized worthlessness rather than trauma-distorted cognitions. His avoidance is general social withdrawal rather than avoidance of trauma reminders. While PTSD and MDD can co-occur, the primary diagnosis driving his current impairment is MDD.

## Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia)

Why Consider: Harold has chronic depressive symptoms.

Why Rule Out: PDD requires 2+ years of continuous depressed mood. Harold's episode is 8 months --- significant, but not chronic enough for PDD. Additionally, his episode began with an acute precipitant (the shooting) and has a clear onset, which is more consistent with a major depressive episode than the insidious, chronic pattern of dysthymia.

## Adjustment Disorder with Depressed Mood

Why Consider: Harold's symptoms developed after a significant stressor (the shooting).

Why Rule Out: Adjustment Disorder requires symptoms to develop within 3 months of a stressor and to not meet criteria for another mental disorder. Harold's symptoms have persisted for 8 months and meet full criteria for MDD, which supersedes adjustment disorder. The "not meeting criteria for another disorder" exclusion rule applies here.

## Complicated Grief

Why Consider: Harold is grieving a traumatic loss.

Why Rule Out: Harold is not grieving a loss through death in the traditional sense. His guilt is survivor guilt, not bereavement.

## Substance-Induced Depression

Why Consider: Harold uses alcohol occasionally.

Why Rule Out: No substance use changes. Alcohol use minimal (1--2 beers weekly).

## Hypothyroidism

Why Consider: Hypothyroidism can cause depressive symptoms.

Why Rule Out: TSH normal. Mood episode has clear psychological precipitant.

## Bereavement/Normal Grief

Why Consider: Harold experienced a traumatic loss.

Why Rule Out: Not applicable --- no death of a loved one.

# Correct Diagnosis

**Major Depressive Disorder, Single Episode, Severe**

## DSM-5-TR Explanation

Harold meets full criteria for Major Depressive Disorder, Single Episode, Severe:

## Diagnostic Reasoning

1. **Five+ symptoms present during same 2-week period** --- he has 10 symptoms including both core features (depressed mood, anhedonia).

2. **Symptoms cause clinically significant distress and impairment** --- he is unable to work, socially isolated, and severely neglecting self-care.

3. **Not attributable to substance or medical condition** --- no substance use; medical conditions are stable and do not explain the syndrome.

4. **No history of manic or hypomanic episodes** --- rules out bipolar spectrum.

5. **Severity "Severe"** --- he has substantial functional impairment, numerous symptoms exceeding the minimum, and suicidal ideation (passive). He is barely able to maintain basic hygiene and has withdrawn from virtually all life activities.

The specifier "With Melancholic Features" could be considered given the prominent anhedonia, psychomotor retardation, and early morning worsening, but the core diagnosis is MDD Severe.

## Required Criteria

- Criterion A: Five (or more) of the following symptoms have been present during the same 2-week period and represent a change from previous functioning; at least one of the symptoms is either (1) depressed mood or (2) loss of interest or pleasure
- Criterion B: The symptoms cause clinically significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning
- Criterion C: The episode is not attributable to the physiological effects of a substance or another medical condition
- Criterion D: The occurrence of the major depressive episode is not better explained by schizoaffective disorder, schizophrenia, schizophreniform disorder, delusional disorder, or other specified and unspecified schizophrenia spectrum and other psychotic disorders
- Severity Specifier: Severe (with or without psychotic features)

## Criteria Met

- Criterion A: Depressed mood (severe, 8 months), anhedonia (severe, 6 months), weight loss (11 kg, 4 months), insomnia→hypersomnia (8 months), psychomotor retardation (severe, 4 months), fatigue (severe, 8 months), worthlessness (severe, 6 months), excessive guilt (severe, 8 months), diminished concentration (moderate, 4 months), passive suicidal ideation (mild, 2 months)
- Criterion B: Significant impairment (unable to work, total social isolation, severe self-neglect, weight loss, passive SI)
- Criterion C: Not attributable to substance or medical condition
- Criterion D: No psychotic features
- Severity: Severe (substantial functional impairment, numerous symptoms, suicidal ideation)

## Criteria Not Met

- None. All criteria for Major Depressive Disorder, Single Episode, Severe are met.

# Treatment Plan

## Psychotherapy

- **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT):** Targets cognitive distortions (all-or-nothing thinking about the shooting, personalization of outcome, hopelessness about the future). Behavioral activation to reverse anhedonia and withdrawal.

- **Prolonged Exposure (PE) or Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT):** If trauma-specific processing is indicated as adjunct, but MDD should be the primary treatment target initially.

- **Interpersonal Therapy (IPT):** Addresses role transitions (retirement, divorce, empty nest) and complicated grief themes.

## Medication

- **SSRIs** (e.g., sertraline, escitalopram) --- first-line for moderate-severe MDD. Sertraline also has evidence for PTSD if comorbid.

- **SNRIs** (e.g., venlafaxine, duloxetine) --- alternative if SSRI insufficient, particularly given his chronic pain.

- Given severity, **combined psychotherapy and pharmacotherapy** is indicated.

- *Individual medication selection by prescribing clinician only.*

## Lifestyle

- Gradual re-engagement with previously meaningful activities (fishing, diner visits)
- Structured daily routine
- Regular physical activity (walking, given back pain limitations)
- Sleep hygiene
- Social reconnection in graded steps
- Possible peer support group for retired first responders

## Prognosis

Guarded but potentially good. Harold has several protective factors (supportive daughter, insight, no substance use, requested firearm removal) but also significant risk factors (male gender, first responder culture discouraging help-seeking, severe functional impairment, passive SI). Early, intensive intervention is critical. The trauma context adds complexity but does not preclude recovery.

# Common Student Mistakes

- Automatically diagnosing PTSD in anyone with trauma history. Students see "police officer + shooting" and reflexively select PTSD. However, PTSD requires specific symptom clusters (intrusion, avoidance, negative alterations, arousal). If the patient presents with a full depressive syndrome that exceeds trauma-specific symptoms, MDD may be the primary diagnosis.
- Missing the severity specifier. Harold's presentation includes substantial functional impairment, numerous symptoms, and suicidal ideation --- this warrants "Severe" rather than "Moderate."
- Attributing all symptoms to "normal grief" or "burnout" rather than recognizing a full major depressive episode.
- Forgetting to rule out bipolar spectrum disorders before diagnosing MDD.

---

# Memory Tricks

- **"SIG E CAPS"**: Sleep, Interest, Guilt, Energy, Concentration, Appetite, Psychomotor, Suicidality --- Harold has all of these.
- **"Trauma + Depression ≠ PTSD"**: A trauma history does not automatically mean PTSD. Map symptoms to criteria, not just the presence of a trauma narrative.
- **First responder depression**: Think "toughness fatigue" --- they don't say "I'm depressed," they say "I'm useless" or "I'm weak."

---

# Exam Pearls

- In first responders and other high-stigma populations, depression often presents as "toughness fatigue" rather than sadness. Patients like Harold may not say "I'm depressed" --- they say "I'm useless," "I'm weak," or "I can't do my job anymore."
- The emotional constriction and psychomotor slowing can be mistaken for "just being a stoic guy" when they are actually severe melancholic features. Always assess for anhedonia and worthlessness in populations that minimize emotional expression.
- Always map symptoms to criteria rather than relying on the presence of a trauma narrative alone. PTSD requires specific symptom clusters; MDD requires depressive symptoms.
- The specifier "With Melancholic Features" could be considered given prominent anhedonia, psychomotor retardation, and early morning worsening.

# Overview

Ms. Priya Sharma is a 31-year-old marketing coordinator who presents with a chronic pattern of emotional oscillation characterized by periods of feeling "electric" --- brimming with ideas, making ambitious plans, staying up late working on side projects, and feeling like she could conquer anything --- followed by crashes where energy evaporates, she cancels plans, ignores messages, feels profoundly inadequate, and wonders why she ever thought she was capable of anything. She describes never finding a "middle ground" and feeling like she is "always either too much or not enough." She has never been "clinically depressed" by her own assessment, and she maintains basic functioning throughout. Her most recent partner ended their relationship two months ago, citing her unpredictability, which served as the catalyst for seeking help.

---

# Learning Objectives

- Identify the diagnostic criteria for Cyclothymic Disorder, including the requirement for chronic subthreshold mood cycling
- Differentiate Cyclothymic Disorder from Bipolar II Disorder, Borderline Personality Disorder, and Persistent Depressive Disorder
- Recognize that Cyclothymic Disorder requires subthreshold symptoms that do not meet full criteria for hypomanic or major depressive episodes
- Understand the chronicity requirement (≥2 years in adults) and the functional impact of subthreshold mood variability
- Appreciate that Cyclothymic Disorder is not "mild Bipolar II" but a distinct diagnostic entity

# Presenting Complaint

Ms. Priya Sharma arrived precisely on time, dressed professionally but with slightly mismatched accessories --- a statement necklace that clashed with her earrings. She sat down with a sigh that seemed to carry genuine weariness.

"I feel like I'm always either too much or not enough," she said. "There's no in-between. My friends joke that I'm 'extra,' but honestly, it's exhausting. And the crashes afterward are worse."

Priya described a pattern of emotional oscillation that has characterized her adult life. For periods of several days, she feels "electric" --- brimming with ideas, making ambitious plans, staying up late working on side projects, texting friends constantly, feeling like she could conquer anything. Then, inevitably, she crashes. The energy evaporates. She cancels plans, ignores messages, feels profoundly inadequate, and wonders why she ever thought she was capable of anything.

"I've never been 'clinically depressed,' I don't think," she clarified. "I still go to work. I shower. I eat. But inside, I feel hollow. And then a week later, I'm planning a cross-country road trip or applying to grad school or starting a podcast. The cycle just... repeats."

She emphasized that these shifts are not triggered by external events. "It's not like something bad happens and I get sad. It's just... my brain does this. Always has."

Her reason for seeking help now is relational. Her most recent partner ended their relationship two months ago, citing her unpredictability. "He said he never knew which version of me he'd get," she said, her voice cracking slightly. "The fun, energetic Priya or the withdrawn, irritable one. He said it felt like dating two people who hated each other."

# History of Present Illness

Priya's mood variability has been present since late adolescence but has intensified in her twenties.

**Onset:** First noticed around age 17. She remembers being "intense" in high school --- periods of obsessive focus on extracurriculars followed by weeks of apathy. Assumed it was normal teenage drama.

**Duration:** Continuous pattern for approximately 14 years. Hypomanic-like periods last 3--7 days; depressive-like periods last 5--10 days. There are rarely more than 2--3 consecutive "neutral" days.

**Progression:** Relatively stable in pattern but increasing functional consequences. Early adulthood cycles were more contained within academic and social contexts. Now, they affect her professional reliability, romantic stability, and financial planning.

**Triggers:** Minimal external trigger correlation. Some evidence that work stressors can precipitate shifts, but episodes often occur without clear antecedents.

**Impact on Life:**

- **Occupational:** Generally competent but inconsistent. Managers describe her as "brilliant but unreliable." She has been passed over for promotion twice. During elevated periods, she takes on extra projects enthusiastically; during low periods, she misses deadlines and avoids meetings.

- **Social:** Maintains a wide but shallow social network. Makes intense new friendships during elevated periods, then withdraws, leaving others confused and hurt. Has lost several close friends who felt "abandoned."

- **Romantic:** All three serious relationships ended due to her emotional unpredictability. Partners describe her as "exhausting" and "impossible to read."

- **Financial:** Impulsive spending during elevated periods (designer clothes, travel bookings, online courses she never completes). Currently has $6,200 in credit card debt.

- **Self-care:** Sleep varies dramatically (4--5 hours during elevated periods, 9--10 hours during low periods). Appetite fluctuates. Exercise routine abandoned and restarted repeatedly.

She has never experienced a full major depressive episode (no 2-week period with 5+ symptoms including core features) and never experienced a full hypomanic episode (no 4-day period with full syndromal criteria causing clear functional change observable by others). Her symptoms are subthreshold but persistent.

# Diagnostic Clues

- Chronic pattern of emotional oscillation spanning many years (approximately 14 years)
- Patient explicitly states she has never been "clinically depressed" and has always maintained basic functioning
- Symptoms are real and distressing but don't quite reach the threshold of full episodes
- Never had a period that meets full criteria for a major depressive episode (2+ weeks, 5+ symptoms including a core feature) or a full hypomanic episode (4+ days, clear functional change observable by others)
- Subthreshold symptoms are numerous, persistent, and cause significant impairment
- Duration is critical: symptoms have been present for well over 2 years with no more than brief symptom-free periods
- Does not meet criteria for Bipolar II (no full MDE, no full hypomanic episode) or MDD (no full MDE)

# Differential Diagnosis

## Bipolar II Disorder

Why Consider: Priya has mood cycling with both elevated and depressive periods, and there is family history of bipolar II disorder.

Why Rule Out: Bipolar II requires at least one major depressive episode AND at least one hypomanic episode. Priya explicitly states she has never been "clinically depressed," and her depressive periods are subthreshold --- they don't meet the 5-symptom, 2-week threshold for MDE. Her elevated periods are also subthreshold for hypomania --- too brief (3--7 days vs. minimum 4 days) and not severe enough to cause clear functional change observable by others. Without full episodes, Bipolar II cannot be diagnosed.

## Borderline Personality Disorder

Why Consider: BPD features mood lability and unstable relationships, which are present in Priya's presentation.

Why Rule Out: BPD mood shifts are typically rapid (hours), reactive to interpersonal stressors, and accompanied by chronic emptiness, identity disturbance, and self-harm/impulsivity across domains. Priya's mood shifts are sustained (days to over a week), not clearly reactive, and follow a bipolar-like pattern of elevation and depression. She does not report self-harm, chronic emptiness (her "hollow" feeling is episodic), or identity disturbance. Her impulsivity is primarily confined to elevated periods (spending, planning) rather than the pervasive impulsivity of BPD.

## Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia)

Why Consider: Priya has chronic mood symptoms and functional impairment.

Why Rule Out: Dysthymia/PDD requires chronic depressed mood for 2+ years without the hypomanic-like periods. Priya's presentation is biphasic --- she has both elevated and depressive periods. PDD is unipolar; Cyclothymia is biphasic. Additionally, her depressive periods are subthreshold even for PDD, which requires depressed mood for most of the day, more days than not, for 2+ years.

## Bipolar I Disorder

Why Consider: Family history of bipolar disorder and presence of mood cycling.

Why Rule Out: No manic episodes. No full hypomanic or depressive episodes.

## Substance-Induced Mood Disorder

Why Consider: Caffeine use is moderate.

Why Rule Out: Caffeine use is moderate (3--4 cups daily); no substance use pattern temporally linked to mood shifts.

## Personality Disorder (NOS/Other Specified)

Why Consider: Long-standing patterns that could suggest personality pathology.

Why Rule Out: While personality factors contribute, the pattern is better explained by a mood disorder diagnosis. Cyclothymia can coexist with personality traits but should not be overlooked in favor of a personality diagnosis when criteria are met.

## ADHD

Why Consider: Inconsistent focus and impulsivity present.

Why Rule Out: Symptoms are mood-dependent rather than chronic. No childhood ADHD history.

# Correct Diagnosis

**Cyclothymic Disorder**

## DSM-5-TR Explanation

Priya meets criteria for Cyclothymic Disorder:

## Diagnostic Reasoning

1. **Numerous periods with hypomanic symptoms** that do not meet criteria for hypomanic episode --- she has repeated 3--7 day periods of elevated mood, increased energy, decreased sleep, increased talkativeness, and inflated self-esteem, but these are too brief and/or lack the full symptom count or clear functional change required for hypomania.

2. **Numerous periods with depressive symptoms** that do not meet criteria for major depressive episode --- she has repeated 5--10 day periods of depressed mood, anhedonia, fatigue, poor concentration, and inadequacy, but these are subthreshold (not 5+ symptoms for 2+ weeks with core features).

3. **Symptoms present for at least 2 years** (in adults) --- her pattern has been present for approximately 14 years, far exceeding the minimum.

4. **No more than 2 months symptom-free** during the 2-year period --- she describes rarely more than 2--3 consecutive neutral days.

5. **Symptoms cause clinically significant distress and impairment** --- evident in occupational stagnation, relationship failures, financial debt, and subjective exhaustion.

6. **Not better explained by another disorder** --- no full MDE, no full hypomanic episode, no substance use, no medical condition.

## Required Criteria

- Criterion A: For at least 2 years (at least 1 year in children and adolescents) there have been numerous periods with hypomanic symptoms that do not meet criteria for a hypomanic episode and numerous periods with depressive symptoms that do not meet criteria for a major depressive episode
- Criterion B: During the above 2-year period (1 year in children and adolescents), the hypomanic and depressive periods have been present for at least half the time and the individual has not been without the symptoms for more than 2 months at a time
- Criterion C: Criteria for a major depressive, manic, or hypomanic episode have never been met
- Criterion D: The symptoms in Criterion A are not better explained by schizoaffective disorder, schizophrenia, schizophreniform disorder, delusional disorder, or other specified or unspecified schizophrenia spectrum and other psychotic disorder
- Criterion E: The symptoms are not attributable to the physiological effects of a substance or another medical condition
- Criterion F: The symptoms cause clinically significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning

## Criteria Met

- Criterion A: Numerous periods with hypomanic-like symptoms (3--7 days, elevated mood, increased energy, decreased sleep, increased talkativeness, inflated self-esteem) and depressive-like symptoms (5--10 days, depressed mood, anhedonia, fatigue, poor concentration, inadequacy) for 14 years
- Criterion B: Symptoms present for at least half the time with rarely more than 2--3 consecutive neutral days
- Criterion C: Never met full criteria for MDE, manic, or hypomanic episode
- Criterion D: No psychotic disorder
- Criterion E: Not attributable to substance or medical condition
- Criterion F: Significant distress and impairment (occupational stagnation, three failed relationships, $6,200 debt, social isolation)

## Criteria Not Met

- None. All criteria for Cyclothymic Disorder are met.

# Treatment Plan

## Psychotherapy

- **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT):** Targets cognitive patterns during both poles and builds skills for emotional regulation.

- **Interpersonal and Social Rhythm Therapy (IPSRT):** Helps stabilize daily routines and sleep-wake cycles, which can reduce cycle frequency and amplitude.

- **Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy (MBCT):** Useful for developing non-reactive awareness of mood shifts without judgment.

## Medication

- **Mood stabilizers** (e.g., lamotrigine, lithium) may be considered if symptoms are severe or progressive, though evidence is less robust than for Bipolar I/II.

- **Second-generation antipsychotics** at low doses have emerging evidence.

- **Antidepressants should be used with caution** --- may trigger hypomanic-like symptoms or rapid cycling.

- *Individual medication selection by prescribing clinician only.*

## Lifestyle

- Strict sleep hygiene (sleep disruption is a major cycle trigger)
- Regular exercise (moderate, consistent --- not excessive during elevated periods)
- Caffeine reduction
- Mood tracking to identify prodromal patterns
- Financial planning safeguards during elevated periods
- Communication strategies for relationships

## Prognosis

Fair to good. Cyclothymic disorder often goes unrecognized for years, leading to cumulative relational and occupational damage. Early intervention can prevent progression to full Bipolar I or II. However, patients often resist the "bipolar" label, and medication adherence can be challenging. The chronic nature means treatment is long-term.

# Common Student Mistakes

- Diagnosing Bipolar II when the patient has never had full episodes. Students see mood cycling and immediately jump to Bipolar II, forgetting that both poles must meet full episode criteria.
- Viewing Cyclothymia as "mild Bipolar II" rather than a distinct disorder of chronic subthreshold cycling. The diagnostic threshold matters.
- Missing the diagnosis because the patient is "functioning" --- Priya works, showers, and eats, but her quality of life is profoundly impaired.
- Attributing the pattern to personality rather than a treatable mood disorder.

---

# Memory Tricks

- **"Subthreshold but suffering"**: Cyclothymia is subthreshold for full episodes but causes real, significant impairment.
- **"Two years, two poles, too brief"**: Remember the 2-year minimum, the biphasic nature, and the subthreshold ("too brief/too mild") symptoms.
- **Think of a dimmer switch**: Unlike the on/off switch of Bipolar II (full episodes), Cyclothymia is like a dimmer that never fully turns on or off --- always fluctuating but never reaching full intensity.

# Exam Pearls

- Cyclothymic Disorder is the most underdiagnosed mood disorder in clinical practice. Patients like Priya are often dismissed as "dramatic," "difficult," or "just stressed" because they don't meet full criteria for more recognizable disorders.
- The key diagnostic clue is the chronicity of subthreshold biphasic symptoms --- not the severity of any single episode. Ask: "Has this pattern been present for years, even if no single period was 'bad enough' for hospitalization?"
- Cyclothymia can coexist with personality traits but should not be overlooked in favor of a personality diagnosis when criteria are met.
- Patients with Cyclothymia are at elevated risk for progression to Bipolar I or II, making early identification and treatment important.

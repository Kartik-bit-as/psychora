# Overview

Ms. Diane Walsh is a 41-year-old administrative assistant who describes a life lived in what she calls "permanent twilight." She cannot recall a period of more than a few weeks when she felt genuinely happy, energetic, or excited about the future. She functions adequately --- she holds a job, raises her children, maintains her marriage --- but describes everything as "going through the motions." Her persistent low mood has been present since adolescence with no sustained remission. The catalyst for seeking help was her 11-year-old daughter asking, "Mommy, why are you always sad?" which made Diane realize her mood was affecting her children.

---

# Learning Objectives

- Identify the diagnostic criteria for Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia), including the chronicity requirement
- Differentiate PDD from Major Depressive Disorder, recognizing that PDD captures chronic subthreshold depression without discrete full MDEs
- Recognize the "high-functioning, low-wellbeing" presentation of chronic depression
- Understand the specifiers for PDD (early onset, pure dysthymic syndrome)
- Appreciate that functioning does not equal wellness in depressive disorders

# Presenting Complaint

Ms. Diane Walsh spoke in a measured, quiet voice, as if she had rehearsed her words to avoid taking up too much space. She sat with her hands clasped tightly in her lap, occasionally glancing at the door as if expecting someone to interrupt and tell her she didn't belong there.

"I don't really have a crisis," she said. "I just... I saw a commercial about depression, and it said something about people feeling down for years. And I thought --- that's me. That's been me since I was a teenager."

Diane described a life lived in what she called "permanent twilight." She cannot recall a period of more than a few weeks when she felt genuinely happy, energetic, or excited about the future. She functions adequately --- she holds a job, raises her children, maintains her marriage --- but describes everything as "going through the motions."

"I love my kids," she said firmly. "I do. But sometimes I watch them playing and I feel... nothing. Like I'm watching a movie of someone else's life. And then I feel terrible for feeling nothing, which makes it worse."

She has been married to her husband, Tom, for 14 years. She described him as "a good man, patient." He has suggested therapy multiple times over the years, but she always refused, believing her unhappiness was a character flaw rather than an illness. "My mother was the same way," she said. "Grumpy, tired, always complaining. I thought it was just... our family temperament."

The catalyst for seeking help was her daughter, Emma. "Emma said to me last month, 'Mommy, why are you always sad?' And I realized --- she's noticing. She's 11, and she's already learning that mothers are supposed to be unhappy. I don't want that for her."

# History of Present Illness

Diane's low mood has been present since adolescence with no sustained remission.

**Onset:** Began around age 15. She remembers feeling "different" from her peers --- while they were excited about dances and college plans, she felt "flat" and "tired all the time." She assumed it was normal.

**Duration:** Continuous for approximately 26 years. Depressed mood present "more days than not" for the entire duration. No remission longer than 4--6 weeks.

**Progression:** Relatively stable in severity but increasing functional awareness. In her twenties, she was able to attribute her low mood to external circumstances (difficult job, financial stress). In her thirties, as circumstances improved (stable marriage, children, decent income), she could no longer explain her persistent unhappiness. The discrepancy between her "good life" and her internal state has intensified her guilt and self-criticism.

**Triggers:** No clear external triggers for the overall condition. Individual bad days may be triggered by minor stressors, but the baseline mood is consistently low regardless of circumstances.

**Impact on Life:**

- **Occupational:** Has held the same administrative position for 12 years. Competent but never promoted. Described by supervisors as "reliable but uninspired." She has never pursued career advancement because "what's the point?"

- **Parental:** Physically present and responsible but emotionally distant. She attends school events, helps with homework, prepares meals --- but describes parenting as "a job I perform adequately rather than a joy."

- **Marital:** Tom is supportive but frustrated. Intimacy is rare; Diane describes sex as "another task." She worries he will eventually leave her for someone "more alive."

- **Social:** Maintains one close friendship from high school (who also struggles with low mood). Has not made new friends in over a decade. Declines most social invitations.

- **Self-care:** Adequate but joyless. Showers daily, maintains appearance, but describes these as "maintenance, not self-care." Has never had consistent exercise routine. Eats adequately but without pleasure.

She denies any history of manic or hypomanic episodes. She denies suicidal ideation but has had persistent thoughts that "everyone would be better off without me" --- which she recognizes as untrue but cannot fully dismiss.

# Diagnostic Clues

- She describes a lifetime of low mood --- not episodic crashes, but a continuous, unrelenting baseline of unhappiness
- She cannot remember feeling genuinely good for more than brief interludes
- She has never met full criteria for a major depressive episode --- no discrete 2-week period with 5+ symptoms including core features
- Her symptoms are chronic, persistent, and subthreshold, yet they cause significant impairment across decades
- The duration is the critical clue: depressed mood for more days than not, for at least 2 years, with no symptom-free period longer than 2 months
- She has maintained this pattern for 26 years
- While she has some symptoms that overlap with major depression, the chronicity and subthreshold nature point to a specific diagnosis for long-standing, low-grade depression

# Differential Diagnosis

## Major Depressive Disorder, Recurrent, Mild

Why Consider: Diane has depressive symptoms and functional impairment.

Why Rule Out: MDD requires discrete major depressive episodes --- periods of 2+ weeks with 5+ symptoms including a core feature. Diane has never had a discrete episode meeting full MDE criteria. Her symptoms are chronic, continuous, and subthreshold. While she has depressive symptoms, they do not cluster into discrete episodes. PDD is the appropriate diagnosis for chronic subthreshold depression without full MDEs.

## Persistent Depressive Disorder with Intermittent Major Depressive Episodes

Why Consider: This specifier would apply if Diane had at least one full MDE during the 2-year period.

Why Rule Out: This specifier would apply if Diane had at least one full major depressive episode during the 2-year period of dysthymic symptoms. She explicitly denies ever having a period that met full MDE criteria. Her symptoms are consistently subthreshold. Therefore, the "pure dysthymic syndrome" specifier is correct rather than the "intermittent MDE" specifier.

## Personality Disorder (Avoidant or Dependent)

Why Consider: Diane has long-standing patterns that could suggest personality pathology (avoidance, low self-esteem, emotional constriction).

Why Rule Out: While Diane has long-standing patterns that could suggest personality pathology, her presentation is better explained by a mood disorder. The pervasive depressed mood, anhedonia, fatigue, and hopelessness are symptoms of depression, not personality traits. Additionally, her symptoms began in adolescence and have been continuous --- consistent with early-onset dysthymia. Personality disorders can co-occur, but the mood symptoms are primary and meet full criteria for PDD. The "rule out mood disorder first" principle applies.

## Double Depression

Why Consider: This term refers to PDD with superimposed MDE.

Why Rule Out: Since Diane has never had a full MDE, this does not apply.

## Chronic MDD

Why Consider: MDD can be specified as chronic if symptoms persist for 2+ years.

Why Rule Out: MDD can be specified as chronic if symptoms persist for 2+ years, but this requires an initial episode meeting full MDE criteria. Diane has never had such an episode.

## Substance-Induced Depression

Why Consider: Depression can be substance-induced.

Why Rule Out: No substance use.

## Hypothyroidism

Why Consider: Hypothyroidism can cause depressive symptoms.

Why Rule Out: TSH is stable on levothyroxine. Symptoms predate and exceed thyroid dysfunction.

# Correct Diagnosis

**Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia)**

## DSM-5-TR Explanation

Diane meets criteria for Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia):

## Diagnostic Reasoning

1. **Depressed mood for most of the day, more days than not, for at least 2 years** --- she has had persistent depressed mood for 26 years, far exceeding the minimum.

2. **Presence of two or more additional symptoms** --- she has at least 6 additional symptoms (poor appetite, insomnia/hypersomnia, low energy, low self-esteem, poor concentration, hopelessness), exceeding the minimum of 2.

3. **No symptom-free period longer than 2 months during the 2-year period** --- she reports no remission longer than 4--6 weeks in 26 years.

4. **Symptoms cause clinically significant distress and impairment** --- occupational stagnation, emotional distance in marriage and parenting, social isolation, and profound subjective suffering.

5. **Not better explained by another disorder** --- no history of manic/hypomanic episodes (rules out bipolar-related). No discrete major depressive episodes (which would shift to the "with intermittent major depressive episodes" specifier).

6. **Not attributable to substance or medical condition** --- hypothyroidism is controlled; symptoms predate and exceed what would be expected from thyroid dysfunction.

The specifier "Early Onset" (before age 21) applies, as does "With Pure Dysthymic Syndrome" (no full MDE in the past 2 years).

## Required Criteria

- Criterion A: Depressed mood for most of the day, for more days than not, as indicated by either subjective account or observation by others, for at least 2 years
- Criterion B: Presence, while depressed, of two (or more) of the following: poor appetite or overeating, insomnia or hypersomnia, low energy or fatigue, low self-esteem, poor concentration or difficulty making decisions, feelings of hopelessness
- Criterion C: During the 2-year period (1 year for children or adolescents) of the disturbance, the individual has never been without the symptoms in Criteria A and B for more than 2 months at a time
- Criterion D: Criteria for a major depressive disorder may be continuously present for 2 years
- Criterion E: There has never been a manic episode or a hypomanic episode, and criteria have never been met for cyclothymic disorder
- Criterion F: The disturbance is not better explained by a persistent schizoaffective disorder, schizophrenia, delusional disorder, or other specified or unspecified schizophrenia spectrum and other psychotic disorder
- Criterion G: The symptoms are not attributable to the physiological effects of a substance or another medical condition
- Criterion H: The symptoms cause clinically significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning

## Criteria Met

- Criterion A: Depressed mood for 26 years, most days, more days than not
- Criterion B: At least 6 additional symptoms (poor appetite, insomnia/hypersomnia, low energy, low self-esteem, poor concentration, hopelessness)
- Criterion C: No remission longer than 4--6 weeks in 26 years
- Criterion D: No full MDE criteria met
- Criterion E: No manic or hypomanic episodes
- Criterion F: No psychotic disorder
- Criterion G: Hypothyroidism controlled; symptoms predate and exceed medical explanation
- Criterion H: Significant distress and impairment (occupational stagnation, marital distance, social isolation, emotional unavailability in parenting)

## Criteria Not Met

- None. All criteria for Persistent Depressive Disorder are met.

# Treatment Plan

## Psychotherapy

- **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT):** Targets chronic negative cognitive schemas ("I'm fundamentally flawed," "happiness is for other people") and behavioral patterns (avoidance, withdrawal). Long-term approach given chronicity.

- **Interpersonal Therapy (IPT):** Addresses role transitions, grief (for the life she feels she missed), and interpersonal deficits.

- **Behavioral Activation:** Critical for reversing decades of anhedonia and withdrawal. Gradual, structured re-engagement with potentially rewarding activities.

## Medication

- **SSRIs** (e.g., sertraline, fluoxetine, escitalopram) --- first-line for PDD. Given chronicity, medication may need to be continued long-term.

- **SNRIs** (e.g., venlafaxine, duloxetine) --- alternative if SSRI insufficient.

- **Combined psychotherapy and pharmacotherapy** is often most effective for chronic depression.

- *Individual medication selection by prescribing clinician only.*

## Lifestyle

- Structured daily routine with scheduled pleasant activities
- Regular physical activity (even brief walks)
- Sleep hygiene
- Gradual social re-engagement
- Journaling to identify mood patterns and challenge negative thoughts
- Couples therapy to address marital distance

## Prognosis

Guarded. Early-onset PDD with 26-year duration is challenging to treat. Diane has developed deeply entrenched negative schemas and behavioral patterns. However, she has protective factors (stable marriage, employment, no substance use, sought help voluntarily) and her motivation to change for her children is powerful. Treatment will require patience --- improvement may be gradual rather than dramatic. Risk of progression to major depressive episodes exists if untreated.

# Common Student Mistakes

- Missing PDD because the patient is "functioning." Students often assume that if someone is working and parenting, they cannot have a serious depressive disorder. Diane is adequately functioning but profoundly impaired in quality of life.
- Diagnosing MDD because depressive symptoms are present, without recognizing that they never cluster into discrete full episodes.
- Attributing chronic low mood to personality rather than a treatable mood disorder.
- Overlooking PDD in favor of a personality disorder diagnosis when mood symptoms are primary and meet full criteria.

# Memory Tricks

- **"The walking wounded"**: PDD patients hold jobs, raise children, maintain marriages --- all while experiencing profound internal emptiness. They are invisible to everyone except themselves.
- **"2 years, 2 symptoms, 2 months"**: 2 years minimum duration, 2+ additional symptoms, no more than 2 months symptom-free.
- **"Gray, not black"**: Unlike MDD's episodic darkness, PDD is a persistent gray --- always there, never lifting for long.

---

# Exam Pearls

- Persistent Depressive Disorder is often invisible to everyone except the sufferer. Patients like Diane are the "walking wounded" --- they hold jobs, raise children, maintain marriages, all while experiencing profound internal emptiness.
- They are often misdiagnosed as "just stressed" or "just introverted" for decades. The diagnostic key is the chronicity: when a patient says "I've always been this way," dig deeper. Ask about onset, duration, and remission.
- Chronic does not mean untreatable, but it does mean treatment requires persistence and patience.
- Do not equate functioning with wellness. Always assess subjective distress and the gap between external appearance and internal experience.
- PDD specifically captures the "high-functioning, low-wellbeing" presentation.

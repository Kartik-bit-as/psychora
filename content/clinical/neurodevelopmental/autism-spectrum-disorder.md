# Overview

A 7-year-old boy presents with lifelong deficits in social communication, restricted interests, repetitive behaviors, and sensory sensitivities. The case highlights the distinction between autism and social anxiety, intellectual disability, and childhood-onset schizophrenia.

---

# Learning Objectives

- Identify the three core domains of social communication deficits in autism spectrum disorder
- Distinguish autism from social anxiety disorder based on the quality of social difficulties
- Recognize that autism can co-occur with average or above-average intelligence
- Understand the role of restricted interests and repetitive behaviors in differential diagnosis

# Presenting Complaint

Mrs. Sharma brought her son Aarav to the child psychology clinic after receiving a third concerned note from his class teacher this semester. The notes described Aarav as "bright but isolated," noting that he could solve complex math puzzles meant for older children yet refused to participate in group activities during recess. His teacher observed that when other children invited him to play cricket, Aarav would walk away without responding or sometimes simply stand at the edge of the field watching the grass.

At home, Aarav's parents had noticed peculiar behaviors since he was a toddler but had attributed them to his "unique personality." He would line up his toy cars in perfect color order for hours and become extremely distressed if anyone moved them. He spoke in full sentences by age two but only to request things --- never to share experiences or initiate conversation. Family gatherings were overwhelming for him; he would cover his ears and retreat to a corner when relatives spoke loudly or music played.

The breaking point came last month when Aarav had a severe meltdown at his cousin's birthday party. The combination of balloons popping, children shouting, and the unfamiliar venue caused him to scream, hit his head against the wall, and refuse to speak for the rest of the day. His parents realized this was not mere shyness.

---

# History of Present Illness

Aarav's difficulties became apparent around 18 months of age when his parents noticed he was not pointing at objects or bringing toys to show them --- behaviors his younger sister displayed naturally. He made poor eye contact and seemed to look "through" people rather than at them. By age 3, he developed an intense fascination with ceiling fans, watching them spin for hours and attempting to draw their blades from memory with remarkable accuracy.

His language development was atypical. While he had a large vocabulary and could name every dinosaur species by age 4, he used language instrumentally rather than socially. He would say "I want juice" but never "Look at the bird." He repeated phrases from his favorite cartoons verbatim, often out of context. When upset, he would recite entire episodes of a nature documentary in a monotone voice.

Socially, Aarav has never had a friend. He does not understand turn-taking in conversations and will abruptly walk away mid-sentence if he loses interest. He takes instructions literally --- when his teacher said "pull your socks up," he spent ten minutes physically adjusting his socks while the class moved on to the next activity.

His routines are rigid. He eats the same lunch every day (white rice, plain yogurt, one banana), sits in the exact same spot on the school bus, and becomes distressed if there is any deviation. Last week, when road construction forced a detour, he cried for two hours and refused to enter the school building.

Sensory sensitivities are pronounced. He refuses to wear clothing with tags, covers his ears at the sound of a vacuum cleaner, and gags at the texture of certain foods. Conversely, he seems insensitive to pain --- he once fell and scraped his knee badly but continued playing without noticing until his mother pointed it out.

# Diagnostic Clues

- The patient has an extraordinary memory for facts in his areas of interest, but his teacher describes him as "socially invisible" in the classroom.
- He speaks in full sentences with advanced vocabulary, yet he has never asked a "why" question about another person's behavior or initiated a conversation about shared experiences.
- His distress is not about being away from his parents --- it is about unpredictable environments, sensory overload, and changes to his routines. He does not seek comfort from his mother when distressed; he wants the environment to return to its expected state.
- The presence of restricted interests and repetitive behaviors distinguishes autism from pure social communication disorders.

# Differential Diagnosis

## Social Anxiety Disorder

Why Consider: Aarav shows social withdrawal and avoidance of social situations.

Why Rule Out: Aarav's social difficulties are not driven by fear of negative evaluation or embarrassment. He does not avoid social interaction due to anxiety; rather, he lacks the intrinsic motivation and skills for social engagement. He does not experience distress about being judged --- he simply does not seek social connection in the way expected for his developmental level.

---

## Childhood-Onset Schizophrenia

Why Consider: Aarav shows restricted affect and social withdrawal.

Why Rule Out: There is no evidence of hallucinations, delusions, disorganized speech, or grossly disorganized behavior. Aarav's thought process is coherent and logical within his areas of interest. His restricted affect and social withdrawal are developmental and stable, not episodic or accompanied by positive psychotic symptoms.

---

## Intellectual Developmental Disorder

Why Consider: Aarav shows developmental differences and social difficulties.

Why Rule Out: Aarav's intellectual functioning is at least average, with particular strengths in pattern recognition and factual memory. His difficulties are specific to social communication and behavioral flexibility, not global cognitive impairment. While intellectual disability can co-occur with autism, it does not explain the full clinical picture here.

---

## Social (Pragmatic) Communication Disorder

Why Consider: Aarav shows deficits in social communication.

Why Rule Out: The presence of restricted interests and repetitive behaviors (lining up toys, intense fascination with ceiling fans, rigid routines, sensory sensitivities) distinguishes autism from pure social communication disorders. A child who is socially withdrawn but has no repetitive behaviors or restricted interests warrants consideration of Social (Pragmatic) Communication Disorder instead.

---

# Correct Diagnosis

## Autism Spectrum Disorder

### DSM-5-TR Explanation

The correct diagnosis is Autism Spectrum Disorder because Aarav demonstrates persistent deficits in all three core social communication domains --- social-emotional reciprocity, nonverbal communication, and relationship maintenance --- alongside restricted, repetitive behaviors and interests. These symptoms have been present since early childhood and cause significant impairment in multiple settings.

### Diagnostic Reasoning

Aarav meets DSM-5-TR criteria for Autism Spectrum Disorder:

1. **Criterion A: Persistent deficits in social communication and social interaction across multiple contexts:**
   - A1: Deficits in social-emotional reciprocity (never initiates conversation, no shared experiences, walks away mid-sentence)
   - A2: Deficits in nonverbal communicative behaviors (poor eye contact, minimal gestures)
   - A3: Deficits in developing, maintaining, and understanding relationships (no friends, ignores peers, cannot work in groups)

2. **Criterion B: Restricted, repetitive patterns of behavior, interests, or activities:**
   - B1: Stereotyped or repetitive motor movements (lining up toys, echolalia)
   - B2: Insistence on sameness, inflexible adherence to routines (same lunch, same bus seat, meltdowns with changes)
   - B3: Highly restricted, fixated interests (ceiling fans, dinosaurs, nature documentaries)
   - B4: Hyper- or hyporeactivity to sensory input (sound, texture, pain)

3. **Criterion C: Symptoms present in the early developmental period** (symptoms evident by 18 months)

4. **Criterion D: Clinically significant impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of current functioning** (academic, social, family)

5. **Criterion E: Not better explained by intellectual disability or global developmental delay** (intellectually above average)

### Required Criteria

- Criterion A: Deficits in social communication and social interaction
- Criterion B: Restricted, repetitive patterns of behavior, interests, or activities
- Criterion C: Early developmental period onset
- Criterion D: Clinically significant impairment
- Criterion E: Not better explained by intellectual disability

### Criteria Met

All criteria met. Aarav demonstrates deficits in all three social communication domains (A1, A2, A3) and at least two restricted/repetitive behavior domains (B1, B2, B3, B4). Symptoms present since early childhood with significant impairment.

### Criteria Not Met

None. All required criteria are met.

# Treatment Plan

## Psychotherapy

- Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) or Early Start Denver Model (ESDM) for structured skill building
- Social Skills Training to teach explicit rules of social interaction
- Speech-Language Therapy targeting pragmatic language

## Medication

- No medication specifically treats core autism symptoms
- Consider atypical antipsychotics (e.g., risperidone) only for severe irritability or self-injury if present

## Lifestyle

- Structured daily routines with visual schedules
- Sensory-friendly environment modifications
- Special interests used as motivational tools for learning
- Parent training in behavioral strategies

## Prognosis

Highly variable; early intensive intervention improves outcomes significantly. Many individuals develop compensatory strategies and lead independent, fulfilling lives. Co-occurring conditions (anxiety, ADHD, depression) may emerge in adolescence and require ongoing monitoring.

# Common Student Mistakes

- Confusing autism with intellectual disability because both can involve developmental delays. Remember: autism is defined by social-communication deficits and restricted/repetitive behaviors, not by IQ. Many autistic individuals have average or above-average intelligence, and intellectual disability is a separate comorbid condition, not a defining feature.
- Confusing autism with social anxiety disorder. The key distinction is the quality of social difficulties: in autism, it is a skill deficit and lack of social motivation; in social anxiety, it is fear-driven avoidance with intact social skills.
- Overlooking autism in children with fluent language and average intelligence. The absence of intellectual disability does not rule out autism.

# Exam Pearls

- The presence of restricted interests and repetitive behaviors distinguishes autism from pure social communication disorders. A child who is socially withdrawn but has no repetitive behaviors or restricted interests warrants consideration of Social (Pragmatic) Communication Disorder instead.
- Temple Grandin, a professor of animal science and prominent autism advocate, has described her thinking as "thinking in pictures" --- a visual processing style that is common among autistic individuals and contributed to her revolutionary designs in livestock handling facilities.
- This case is rated Intermediate because the social deficits are clear but the absence of intellectual disability and the presence of fluent language can lead students to overlook autism. The distinction from social anxiety requires careful attention to the quality of social difficulties (skill deficit vs. fear-driven avoidance), which is a common exam trap.

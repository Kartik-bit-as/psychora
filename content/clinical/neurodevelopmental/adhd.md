# Overview

An 11-year-old boy presents with a persistent pattern of inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity that interferes with functioning and development, present since before age 12 and observable across multiple settings. The case highlights the distinction between ADHD and oppositional defiant disorder, autism spectrum disorder, and specific learning disorder.

- Distinguish ADHD from oppositional behavior and learning disorders
- Recognize the heritability of ADHD and the importance of family history
- Understand that ADHD involves dysregulation of attention, not a complete absence of attention

# Presenting Complaint

Mrs. Patel sat in the clinic looking exhausted. Her son, Rohan, had been sent to the principal's office three times this month alone --- twice for talking during class and once for climbing out of a first-floor window during math lesson because he "wanted to see if he could jump to the tree branch." His grades had plummeted from B's in Grade 4 to D's and F's in Grade 6, despite testing in the 85th percentile on standardized intelligence tests.

Rohan's current teacher described him as "a tornado in a child's body." He constantly fidgeted, tapped his pencil, and drummed his fingers. He blurted out answers before questions were finished, interrupted classmates, and could not wait his turn during group activities. Assignments that should take 20 minutes stretched into two hours because he kept getting distracted --- by a bird outside, by a classmate's new shoes, by a thought about his video game.

At home, the situation was equally chaotic. Rohan could not sit through a family dinner without leaving the table multiple times. He lost his school bag, water bottle, and homework at least twice a week. His mother had to remind him ten times to brush his teeth, and even then he often forgot. He started projects with enthusiasm --- building a model rocket, learning guitar --- but abandoned them within days when the novelty wore off.

His mother noted that Rohan had always been "on the go" since he was a toddler. He learned to walk early and was climbing furniture before his first birthday. Sleep was a nightly battle; he resisted bedtime, tossed and turned for hours, and woke up groggy and irritable. His biological father reportedly had similar difficulties as a child and dropped out of school in Grade 10.

---

# History of Present Illness

Rohan's symptoms have been present since early childhood, though they have become more problematic with increasing academic demands. In preschool, teachers described him as "a delightful but exhausting child" who never stopped moving and had difficulty following multi-step instructions. He would start putting away toys, get distracted by a block tower, and forget the original task entirely.

By Grade 1, academic struggles emerged. He could read above grade level when focused but often skipped words, lost his place, or abandoned books mid-chapter. Math was particularly challenging --- not because he lacked ability, but because he made careless errors (adding instead of subtracting, skipping problems) and could not show his work step-by-step. His handwriting was messy and poorly spaced, and he frequently reversed letters.

Socially, Rohan is well-liked but impulsive. He has trouble reading social cues and often interrupts or speaks too loudly. He plays roughly, not out of aggression, but because he misjudges his own strength and gets overexcited. He has been rejected from several playgroups because other parents find him "too much." He desperately wants friends but struggles to maintain friendships because he forgets plans, talks over people, and acts without thinking.

Emotionally, Rohan is sensitive and easily frustrated. When tasks are difficult or boring, he gives up quickly and may throw tantrums. He describes his own mind as "a TV with someone else holding the remote --- channels keep changing and I can't stop it." He often feels "stupid" despite his intelligence, and his self-esteem has deteriorated as academic demands have increased.

Sleep is consistently poor. He takes 1--2 hours to fall asleep because his mind races with thoughts. He wakes frequently and has difficulty getting up in the morning. His mother suspects he gets only 6--7 hours of sleep on school nights, far less than recommended for his age.

# Diagnostic Clues

- The patient's intelligence testing places him in the 85th percentile, yet his grades are failing. His mother insists he is "trying his best" and becomes tearful describing the nightly homework battles.
- When asked about his favorite video game, he speaks rapidly and coherently for five minutes without pause, demonstrating clear capacity for sustained attention --- but only when the stimulus is intrinsically rewarding.
- His difficulties are present across all settings --- home, school, and even during leisure activities like sports practice. His biological father had nearly identical struggles as a child and never completed high school.

# Differential Diagnosis

## Oppositional Defiant Disorder

Why Consider: Rohan shows disruptive behavior, disciplinary referrals, and defiance of adult requests.

Why Rule Out: Rohan's behavioral difficulties are not primarily characterized by defiance, vindictiveness, or hostility toward authority figures. While he is disruptive, he is not deliberately annoying others or blaming others for his mistakes. His impulsivity and inattention are neurodevelopmental, not oppositional. He expresses distress about his difficulties and wants to do better, which is inconsistent with ODD.

---

## Autism Spectrum Disorder

Why Consider: Rohan shows social difficulties and behavioral problems.

Why Rule Out: Rohan does not show the core social communication deficits characteristic of autism. He has social motivation, reads basic social cues (though impulsively acts on them), and does not display restricted interests or repetitive behaviors. His eye contact is appropriate, and he engages in reciprocal conversation when interested.

---

## Specific Learning Disorder with Impairment in Mathematics

Why Consider: Rohan struggles with math and shows academic decline.

Why Rule Out: Rohan's math difficulties are due to inattention and careless errors, not a specific deficit in mathematical reasoning. He can solve complex problems when focused and supervised. His academic struggles are global across subjects, not isolated to mathematics, and are better explained by inattention than by a specific learning disability.

# Correct Diagnosis

## Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder, Combined Presentation

### DSM-5-TR Explanation

The correct diagnosis is Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder because Rohan demonstrates a persistent pattern of inattention and hyperactivity-impulsivity that interferes with functioning and development, present since before age 12 and observable across multiple settings. He meets criteria for the Combined Presentation, with clear evidence of both inattentive and hyperactive-impulsive symptoms.

### Diagnostic Reasoning

Rohan meets DSM-5-TR criteria for ADHD, Combined Presentation:

1. **Criterion A1: Six or more symptoms of inattention have persisted for at least 6 months:**
   - Fails to sustain attention in tasks (easily distracted, loses place in books)
   - Makes careless mistakes (math errors, messy handwriting)
   - Difficulty organizing tasks (loses items, cannot follow multi-step instructions)
   - Avoids tasks requiring sustained mental effort (abandons projects)
   - Loses things necessary for tasks (school bag, homework, water bottle)
   - Easily distracted by extraneous stimuli
   - Forgetful in daily activities

2. **Criterion A2: Six or more symptoms of hyperactivity-impulsivity have persisted for at least 6 months:**
   - Fidgets with hands or feet or squirms in seat
   - Leaves seat when remaining seated is expected
   - Talks excessively
   - Blurts out answers before questions completed
   - Difficulty waiting turn
   - Interrupts or intrudes on others

3. **Criterion B: Several inattentive or hyperactive-impulsive symptoms were present before age 12** (symptoms evident since toddlerhood)

4. **Criterion C: Several symptoms are present in two or more settings** (home, school, leisure activities)

5. **Criterion D: The symptoms interfere with social, academic, or occupational functioning** (failing grades, disciplinary referrals, social difficulties)

6. **Criterion E: The symptoms are not better explained by another mental disorder** (not autism, not ODD, not specific learning disorder)

### Required Criteria

- Criterion A1: Six or more symptoms of inattention
- Criterion A2: Six or more symptoms of hyperactivity-impulsivity
- Criterion B: Onset before age 12
- Criterion C: Symptoms in multiple settings
- Criterion D: Functional impairment
- Criterion E: Not better explained by another disorder

### Criteria Met

All criteria met. Rohan demonstrates at least six symptoms of inattention and at least six symptoms of hyperactivity-impulsivity, with onset before age 12, present across multiple settings, causing significant functional impairment.

### Criteria Not Met

None. All required criteria are met.

# Treatment Plan

## Psychotherapy

- Behavioral parent training (e.g., Parent-Child Interaction Therapy, Triple P)
- Classroom behavioral interventions
- Organizational skills training
- Cognitive-behavioral therapy for emotional regulation and self-esteem

## Medication

- Stimulant medications (methylphenidate or amphetamine-based) are first-line pharmacological treatment
- Non-stimulant atomoxetine or alpha-2 agonists (guanfacine, clonidine) as alternatives

## Lifestyle

- Consistent sleep schedule
- Regular physical exercise
- Structured environment with clear routines
- Breaking tasks into smaller chunks
- Limiting distractions during homework
- Using timers and visual organizers
- Reducing screen time before bed

## Prognosis

Generally good with multimodal treatment. Many children show significant improvement with medication and behavioral interventions. Symptoms often persist into adolescence and adulthood, though hyperactivity typically decreases while inattention remains. Risk of academic underachievement, low self-esteem, substance use, and mood disorders if untreated.

# Common Student Mistakes

- Confusing ADHD with "laziness" or "lack of discipline." Remember: ADHD is a neurodevelopmental disorder involving dysregulation of dopamine and norepinephrine circuits in the prefrontal cortex and striatum. These children are not choosing to be inattentive --- their brains are genuinely less responsive to delayed rewards and less efficient at inhibiting impulses.
- The fact that they can hyperfocus on video games does not disprove ADHD; it demonstrates that their attentional system works differently, not that it is broken.
- Overlooking ADHD in children with above-average intelligence. High IQ does not protect against ADHD; in fact, bright children with ADHD may be missed because they can compensate until academic demands exceed their coping capacity.

---

# Memory Tricks

- **The ADHD Brain**: Dopamine Dysregulation = Difficulty Delaying gratification, Hyperactivity, Attention problems, Forgetfulness, Distractibility
- **Hyperfocus is not a contradiction**: ADHD is not an absence of attention; it is dysregulated attention. The same brain that cannot focus on homework can hyperfocus on video games because the reward system is engaged differently.

# Exam Pearls

- ADHD is one of the most heritable psychiatric disorders, with heritability estimates around 70--80%. Always take a careful family history --- a parent or sibling with similar childhood difficulties strongly supports the diagnosis and helps distinguish ADHD from situational behavior problems.
- The first documented description of ADHD-like symptoms dates back to 1798, when Scottish physician Sir Alexander Crichton described "mental restlessness" in children. The modern understanding evolved through terms like "minimal brain dysfunction" and "hyperkinetic reaction of childhood" before arriving at the current ADHD nomenclature in DSM-III (1980).
- This case is rated Beginner because ADHD is one of the most commonly diagnosed childhood disorders and its presentation is relatively straightforward. The combination of inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity across multiple settings with onset in childhood is classic.

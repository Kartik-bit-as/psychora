# Overview

Ms. Ava Moreau, a 24-year-old part-time fashion model and social media influencer, presents with a 6-year preoccupation with perceived defects in her physical appearance that has consumed her life. Despite being conventionally attractive and receiving consistent external validation, she believes she is grotesquely deformed. She has undergone four rhinoplasty procedures with minimal objective change and minimal satisfaction. The case illustrates body dysmorphic disorder with poor insight, highlighting the distinction from social anxiety disorder, major depressive disorder, and narcissistic personality disorder.

---

# Learning Objectives

- Identify the core features of body dysmorphic disorder: preoccupation with perceived defects, repetitive behaviors, and functional impairment
- Distinguish BDD from social anxiety disorder, major depressive disorder, and narcissistic personality disorder
- Recognize the poor insight variant of BDD and its clinical implications
- Understand the role of cosmetic procedures in BDD and why they are contraindicated
- Identify the relationship between BDD and obsessive-compulsive and related disorders

# Presenting Complaint

Ms. Ava Moreau was referred to the clinic by her plastic surgeon, who became concerned after her fourth rhinoplasty request in two years. Ava arrived wearing oversized sunglasses, a wide-brimmed hat, and a surgical mask, despite it being an overcast day. She refused to remove her sunglasses during the initial portion of the session and positioned herself with her back to the window, explaining that she "can't stand the thought of anyone seeing me from the side."

Ava describes a 6-year preoccupation with her appearance that has consumed her life. She believes her nose is "grotesquely large and crooked," her skin is "covered in huge, disfiguring pores," and her jawline is "asymmetrical and masculine." She spends 4-6 hours daily examining her face in magnifying mirrors, taking selfies from multiple angles, and comparing her appearance to other models and influencers. She has developed a complex system of lighting requirements for any room she enters and will not allow photographs to be taken of her profile.

Despite being told repeatedly by photographers, agents, friends, and plastic surgeons that she is exceptionally attractive, Ava insists they are "just being nice" or "don't see what I see." She has undergone three rhinoplasties and one revision rhinoplasty, with minimal objective change between procedures. After each surgery, she initially felt relief for 2-3 weeks, then became preoccupied with a new perceived flaw or re-focused on the original concern.

Ava has stopped attending castings, canceled a major campaign, and now works only from home, heavily editing all photos before posting. She has not been on a date in 3 years because she "can't imagine anyone wanting to look at me." She describes her preoccupation as "torture" and has had passive suicidal thoughts, though no active plan.

# Diagnostic Clues

- Patient has undergone multiple cosmetic procedures for concerns that others do not share, and she experiences only transient relief before shifting focus to new or renewed perceived flaws
- Her preoccupation is not with body weight or shape, and it is not attributable to an eating disorder
- The focus is specifically on facial features that are objectively normal or only minimally different from typical
- The key diagnostic features are: (1) preoccupation with perceived physical defects that are not observable or appear slight to others, (2) repetitive behaviors (mirror-checking, comparing, camouflaging) in response to these concerns, and (3) significant distress and functional impairment
- The poor insight variant is common—she partially recognizes others see her differently but believes her own perception is more accurate
- She has been told repeatedly by photographers, agents, friends, and plastic surgeons that she is exceptionally attractive, yet she insists they are "just being nice" or "don't see what I see"

# Differential Diagnosis

## Social Anxiety Disorder

Why Consider: Ava avoids social situations, has difficulty with eye contact, and experiences significant anxiety in social contexts.

Why Rule Out: While Ava avoids social situations, her avoidance is specifically driven by appearance concerns and fear of being seen, not by fear of negative evaluation or social scrutiny in general. She does not fear conversation, public speaking, or social interaction per se—she fears that others will see her perceived deformities. In social anxiety disorder, the fear is of social situations and negative evaluation broadly, not specifically of being seen due to appearance flaws.

---

## Major Depressive Disorder

Why Consider: Ava has depressive symptoms, including passive suicidal ideation, hopelessness, and social withdrawal.

Why Rule Out: Ava does have depressive symptoms, including passive suicidal ideation, hopelessness, and social withdrawal. However, these symptoms are secondary to her appearance preoccupation and the functional impairment it causes. The primary clinical picture is the appearance-related obsessions and compulsions. Treating only the depression would miss the core disorder driving her impairment.

---

## Narcissistic Personality Disorder

Why Consider: Ava is preoccupied with her appearance and spends extensive time on self-focused activities.

Why Rule Out: Narcissistic personality disorder involves a grandiose sense of self-importance, need for admiration, and lack of empathy. Ava is preoccupied with her appearance but in a self-critical, shame-based manner, not a grandiose one. She does not seek admiration or believe she is superior; rather, she believes she is defective and unworthy. Her self-focus is driven by perceived flaws, not by grandiosity.

---

## Anorexia Nervosa

Why Consider: Ava is in the modeling industry and has body image concerns.

Why Rule Out: No concerns about body weight or fat; no restrictive eating or purging behaviors; BMI normal. Her concerns are specifically about facial features (nose, skin, jawline), not body shape or weight.

---

## Delusional Disorder (Somatic Type)

Why Consider: Ava holds beliefs about her appearance that contradict objective evidence and the opinions of multiple professionals.

Why Rule Out: Insight is poor but not absent; she partially recognizes others see her differently, and her beliefs are not held with full delusional conviction. She acknowledges that others see her differently but insists her perception is "more accurate" or that they "don't see the real me." This partial insight is characteristic of BDD with poor insight rather than delusional disorder.

# Correct Diagnosis

Body Dysmorphic Disorder

DSM-5-TR Explanation: Body dysmorphic disorder is characterized by a preoccupation with one or more perceived defects or flaws in physical appearance that are not observable or appear only slight to others. During the course of the disorder, the individual has performed repetitive behaviors (e.g., mirror checking, excessive grooming, skin picking, reassurance seeking) or mental acts (e.g., comparing his or her appearance with that of others) in response to the appearance concerns. The preoccupation causes clinically significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning. The appearance preoccupation is not better explained by concerns with body fat or weight in an individual whose symptoms meet diagnostic criteria for an eating disorder.

Diagnostic Reasoning: Ava presents with a preoccupation with multiple perceived defects in her physical appearance (nose, skin pores, jawline) that are not observable or appear only slight to others. She engages in repetitive behaviors in response to these concerns, including mirror-checking (4-6 hours daily), excessive grooming, comparing her appearance to others, and seeking cosmetic procedures. The preoccupation has caused clinically significant distress and marked impairment in social (isolation, no dating) and occupational (lost campaign, reduced work) functioning. The symptoms are not better explained by an eating disorder (her concerns are not about body fat or weight). Her poor insight (believing her perception is more accurate than others') is consistent with the "with poor insight" specifier.

Required Criteria:
A. Preoccupation with one or more perceived defects or flaws in physical appearance that are not observable or appear only slight to others.
B. At some point during the course of the disorder, the individual has performed repetitive behaviors (e.g., mirror checking, excessive grooming, skin picking, reassurance seeking) or mental acts (e.g., comparing his or her appearance with that of others) in response to the appearance concerns.
C. The preoccupation causes clinically significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning.
D. The appearance preoccupation is not better explained by concerns with body fat or weight in an individual whose symptoms meet diagnostic criteria for an eating disorder.

Criteria Met: A, B, C, D

Criteria Not Met: None

Specifier: With poor insight

# Treatment Plan

## Psychotherapy

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) specifically adapted for BDD, which includes cognitive restructuring of appearance-related beliefs, exposure to avoided situations (e.g., being seen without concealing clothing, having photographs taken), and response prevention (reducing mirror-checking, comparing, reassurance-seeking). Mirror retraining (using mirrors for brief, functional purposes only) and attentional retraining (shifting focus from appearance to other aspects of experience) are BDD-specific techniques.

## Medication

SSRIs (e.g., fluoxetine, escitalopram) at higher doses are first-line pharmacological treatment for BDD and may reduce obsessive thoughts and compulsive behaviors. In cases with delusional-level poor insight, augmentation with an atypical antipsychotic may be considered.

## Lifestyle

Limit social media use and unfollow appearance-focused accounts; establish structured daily activities outside the home; engage in hobbies unrelated to appearance; improve sleep hygiene; reduce alcohol use; consider support groups for BDD.

## Prognosis

Moderate with appropriate CBT and/or SSRI treatment. BDD is often chronic and can be treatment-resistant, especially in cases with poor insight and history of cosmetic procedures. Early intervention before multiple surgeries improves prognosis. Risk of suicide is elevated and requires ongoing monitoring.

# Common Student Mistakes

- Misdiagnosing BDD as social anxiety disorder or depression because of the social avoidance and mood symptoms. The critical distinguishing feature is that the avoidance, distress, and impairment are specifically driven by preoccupation with perceived physical defects and the repetitive behaviors aimed at checking, fixing, or hiding those defects.
- Missing the diagnosis by attributing symptoms to vanity or narcissism. BDD is a shame-based, self-critical preoccupation, not a grandiose one.
- Failing to recognize that cosmetic surgery almost never helps and typically worsens BDD by reinforcing the belief that the perceived defect is real and fixable through surgery.
- Confusing BDD with eating disorders because both involve body image concerns. The key distinction is that BDD concerns are not about body weight or shape.

---

# Memory Tricks

- **B-D-D**: **B**ody image is distorted, **D**efects are not observable to others, **D**istress and dysfunction are severe
- Think of a funhouse mirror—the reflection is distorted, but the person believes it's accurate. Ava looks in the mirror and sees a "monster" while everyone else sees a beautiful woman.
- BDD was originally considered a "somatoform disorder" in DSM-IV but was reclassified to the "Obsessive-Compulsive and Related Disorders" category in DSM-5 due to its significant overlap with OCD in terms of intrusive thoughts and repetitive behaviors.

---

# Exam Pearls

- Patients with BDD often seek cosmetic surgery rather than psychiatric treatment, and surgery almost never helps. In fact, cosmetic procedures typically worsen BDD by reinforcing the belief that the perceived defect is real and fixable through surgery. Clinicians should screen for BDD before referring for cosmetic procedures.
- The prevalence of BDD is estimated at 1-3% of the general population, but it is significantly underdiagnosed because patients are often ashamed and secretive about their concerns.
- BDD carries a significantly elevated risk of suicide, with rates comparable to or higher than major depressive disorder. Passive suicidal ideation related to appearance concerns should always be taken seriously.
- The "with poor insight" specifier is common in BDD and does not indicate psychosis. Partial recognition that others see her differently, while maintaining that her own perception is "more accurate," is characteristic of poor insight in BDD rather than delusional disorder.

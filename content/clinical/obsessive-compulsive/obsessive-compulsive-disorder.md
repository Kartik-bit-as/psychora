# Overview

Mr. David Chen, a 29-year-old certified public accountant, presents with a 7-year history of increasingly distressing obsessive thoughts and time-consuming compulsive behaviors that have recently reached a crisis point. His primary obsessions center on fears of causing fires or hitting pedestrians, with compulsions including excessive checking, counting in multiples of three, reassurance-seeking, and avoidance behaviors. The case illustrates a classic OCD presentation with multiple obsession and compulsion subtypes, excellent insight, and significant functional impairment.

# Presenting Complaint

Mr. David Chen presented to the anxiety disorders clinic at the insistence of his fiancée, Rachel, who accompanied him to the intake session. David appeared exhausted, with dark circles under his eyes, and admitted he had been awake since 4:00 AM performing his morning rituals. He described a 7-year history of increasingly distressing thoughts and time-consuming behaviors that have recently reached a crisis point.

David's primary obsession centers on the fear that he has left the stove on, the door unlocked, or an appliance plugged in, which will cause a fire that kills his fiancée or their neighbors. Every morning before leaving for work, he checks the stove knobs 12 times (always in multiples of 3), the door lock 9 times, and every electrical outlet 6 times. The checking ritual takes approximately 3 hours, causing him to be consistently late for work. He has received two written warnings from his employer.

In addition to checking, David experiences intrusive, unwanted thoughts of hitting pedestrians with his car. These thoughts are so distressing that he now drives 20 miles out of his way to avoid school zones and has stopped driving on highways entirely. He repeatedly asks Rachel for reassurance that he is a good person and would never hurt anyone. He also counts objects in his environment—ceiling tiles, steps, words on signs—and feels intense anxiety if the count does not end on a multiple of 3.

David recognizes that his thoughts are products of his own mind and that his behaviors are excessive, but he feels driven to perform them to neutralize the anxiety. He describes the experience as "having a gun to my head—if I don't check, something terrible will happen, and it will be my fault."

# Differential Diagnosis

## Generalized Anxiety Disorder

Why Consider: David is anxious and worried about multiple concerns including harm to others and work performance.

Why Rule Out: While David is anxious, GAD is characterized by excessive worry about multiple everyday concerns (work, health, finances, relationships) without the specific intrusive, ego-dystonic thought content and ritualistic behaviors seen in OCD. David's anxiety is specifically tied to obsessive fears and compulsive neutralization, not to generalized apprehension.

---

## Obsessive-Compulsive Personality Disorder

Why Consider: David reports perfectionism and orderliness, and his mother was described as "very particular" about cleanliness and order.

Why Rule Out: OCPD involves a pervasive pattern of preoccupation with orderliness, perfectionism, and control, but these traits are ego-syntonic (the person sees them as rational and desirable). David's symptoms are ego-dystonic—he finds them distressing, recognizes them as excessive, and wants to stop. OCPD does not involve true obsessions and compulsions in the OCD sense.

---

## Delusional Disorder

Why Consider: David has persistent beliefs about causing harm and engages in elaborate behaviors to prevent it.

Why Rule Out: David has excellent insight. He fully recognizes that his fears of causing fires or hitting pedestrians are irrational and that his checking behaviors are excessive. In delusional disorder, the beliefs would be held with delusional conviction (fixed, false beliefs despite contrary evidence). David's ability to label his thoughts as "irrational" and his desire to be free of them rule out a psychotic disorder.

---

## Tic Disorder

Why Consider: David engages in repetitive behaviors (checking, counting).

Why Rule Out: No motor or vocal tics reported. His repetitive behaviors are cognitive-driven rituals, not semi-involuntary tics. The behaviors are performed in response to obsessive thoughts and are aimed at reducing anxiety or preventing harm.

# Correct Diagnosis

Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder

DSM-5-TR Explanation: Obsessive-compulsive disorder is characterized by the presence of obsessions, compulsions, or both. Obsessions are recurrent and persistent thoughts, urges, or images that are experienced, at some time during the disturbance, as intrusive and unwanted, and that in most individuals cause marked anxiety or distress. The individual attempts to ignore or suppress such thoughts, urges, or images, or to neutralize them with some other thought or action (i.e., by performing a compulsion). Compulsions are repetitive behaviors (e.g., hand washing, ordering, checking) or mental acts (e.g., praying, counting, repeating words silently) that the individual feels driven to perform in response to an obsession or according to rules that must be applied rigidly. The behaviors or mental acts are aimed at preventing or reducing anxiety or distress, or preventing some dreaded event or situation; however, these behaviors or mental acts are not connected in a realistic way with what they are designed to neutralize or prevent, or are clearly excessive.

Diagnostic Reasoning: David presents with classic OCD features: recurrent, intrusive, unwanted thoughts (obsessions) about causing fires and hitting pedestrians, which he attempts to ignore, suppress, or neutralize. These obsessions cause significant anxiety and distress. He engages in repetitive behaviors (checking stove, locks, outlets; counting in multiples of 3; reassurance-seeking) and mental acts aimed at preventing the dreaded outcomes or reducing anxiety. The obsessions and compulsions are time-consuming (3+ hours daily) and cause significant impairment in occupational, social, and relational functioning. He has good insight, recognizing that his thoughts are products of his own mind and that his behaviors are excessive. The symptoms are not better explained by another disorder.

Required Criteria:
A. Presence of obsessions, compulsions, or both:
   Obsessions are defined by (1) and (2):
   1. Recurrent and persistent thoughts, urges, or images that are experienced, at some time during the disturbance, as intrusive and unwanted, and that in most individuals cause marked anxiety or distress.
   2. The individual attempts to ignore or suppress such thoughts, urges, or images, or to neutralize them with some other thought or action (i.e., by performing a compulsion).
   Compulsions are defined by (1) and (2):
   1. Repetitive behaviors (e.g., hand washing, ordering, checking) or mental acts (e.g., praying, counting, repeating words silently) that the individual feels driven to perform in response to an obsession or according to rules that must be applied rigidly.
   2. The behaviors or mental acts are aimed at preventing or reducing anxiety or distress, or preventing some dreaded event or situation; however, these behaviors or mental acts are not connected in a realistic way with what they are designed to neutralize or prevent, or are clearly excessive.
B. The obsessions or compulsions are time-consuming (e.g., take more than 1 hour per day) or cause clinically significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning.
C. The disturbance is not attributable to the physiological effects of a substance or another medical condition.
D. The disturbance is not better explained by the symptoms of another mental disorder.

Criteria Met: A (obsessions and compulsions present), B, C, D

Criteria Not Met: None

# Treatment Plan

## Psychotherapy

Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP), the gold-standard CBT for OCD. Treatment would involve exposing David to feared situations (e.g., leaving the stove on without checking, driving near schools) while preventing the compulsive response (checking, counting, reassurance-seeking). Hierarchy-based exposure starting with moderately anxiety-provoking situations and progressing to most feared scenarios. Cognitive restructuring would target inflated responsibility beliefs and thought-action fusion (the belief that thinking about harm increases the likelihood of harm occurring).

## Medication

SSRIs at higher doses than typically used for depression (e.g., fluoxetine 40-80 mg, sertraline 100-200 mg) are first-line pharmacological treatment. Clomipramine (a tricyclic antidepressant) is also effective but has more side effects. Augmentation with low-dose antipsychotics (e.g., risperidone, aripiprazole) may be considered for treatment-resistant cases.

## Lifestyle

Sleep hygiene improvement to address deprivation; stress management techniques (though not as primary treatment); reduction of caffeine and alcohol; structured daily schedule to accommodate ERP homework; couples therapy to address relationship strain and reduce accommodation of rituals by fiancée.

## Prognosis

Moderate to good with ERP and/or SSRI treatment. However, OCD is often chronic and requires ongoing management. Early intervention, good insight, and absence of comorbidities are positive prognostic factors. Relapse prevention and maintenance ERP are essential.

# Common Student Mistakes

- Confusing OCD with OCPD because both involve orderliness and rituals. The critical distinction is that OCD symptoms are ego-dystonic (distressing, unwanted, recognized as excessive), whereas OCPD traits are ego-syntonic (valued, part of self-identity, seen as rational). Additionally, OCD involves true obsessions and compulsions, while OCPD involves personality traits without the specific symptom profile.
- Missing the diagnosis by focusing on the anxiety without recognizing the specific obsessive-compulsive symptom pattern.
- Misdiagnosing as GAD because of the worry content without recognizing the intrusive, ego-dystonic nature of the thoughts and the compulsive neutralization behaviors.
- Assuming that good insight rules out OCD. In fact, most individuals with OCD recognize that their obsessions and compulsions are excessive or unreasonable. The degree of insight can vary (good, fair, poor, absent/delusional beliefs), but good insight is the most common presentation and is explicitly recognized in DSM-5-TR specifiers.

# Memory Tricks

- **O-C-D**: **O**bsessions are intrusive and unwanted, **C**ompulsions reduce anxiety temporarily, **D**istress and dysfunction are key
- Think of OCD as a broken alarm system—the brain's danger signal keeps firing even when there's no real threat, and compulsions are the repeated attempts to "turn off" the alarm
- The "Y-BOCS" (Yale-Brown Obsessive Compulsive Scale) is the gold-standard assessment tool for OCD severity. It was developed in 1989 and remains the most widely used instrument in both clinical practice and research trials for OCD worldwide.

---

# Exam Pearls

- The presence of insight does not rule out OCD. In fact, most individuals with OCD recognize that their obsessions and compulsions are excessive or unreasonable. The degree of insight can vary (good, fair, poor, absent/delusional beliefs), but good insight is the most common presentation and is explicitly recognized in DSM-5-TR specifiers.
- Thought-action fusion is a key cognitive distortion in OCD: the belief that thinking about something increases the likelihood of it happening, or that having a thought is morally equivalent to performing the action.
- ERP is the gold-standard psychological treatment for OCD. Medication alone is less effective than ERP alone, and the combination of ERP and SSRIs is often most effective.
- Family accommodation (e.g., Rachel providing reassurance, allowing David to check) maintains OCD symptoms and should be addressed in treatment.

# Overview

Dr. Harold Whitman, a 68-year-old retired university professor of History of Science, presents with a 40-year history of accumulating possessions that has accelerated dramatically following his wife's death 5 years ago. His 3-bedroom house is filled floor-to-ceiling with stacks of newspapers, books, academic journals, cardboard boxes, plastic bags, and miscellaneous items, creating significant safety hazards and functional impairment. This case illustrates hoarding disorder with poor insight, highlighting the distinction from OCD, major depressive disorder, and neurocognitive disorder.

# Presenting Complaint

Dr. Harold Whitman was referred to the geriatric psychiatry clinic by his primary care physician after the city fire marshal issued a citation for hazardous conditions in his home. Dr. Whitman arrived at the appointment alone, well-groomed, and articulate, expressing confusion and anger about the citation. He brought a folder containing the violation notice and photographs of his home, which he described as "a bit cluttered but perfectly livable."

The photographs, however, revealed a dramatically different picture: every room of the 3-bedroom house was filled floor-to-ceiling with stacks of newspapers, books, academic journals, cardboard boxes, plastic bags, and miscellaneous items. Narrow pathways approximately 12 inches wide wound through the clutter, allowing passage between rooms. The kitchen counters were entirely covered, and the stove was inaccessible. The bathroom was functional but surrounded by towering stacks of toiletries and cleaning supplies. One bedroom was completely filled and inaccessible.

Dr. Whitman explained that he has been collecting books and papers since graduate school, but the accumulation accelerated after his wife's death 5 years ago. He described intense distress at the thought of discarding any item, explaining that every newspaper might contain historically significant information, every book might be needed for future research, and every object has "potential value." He has not allowed anyone into his home for 3 years, including his adult children, claiming he is "too busy organizing" to have visitors.

He spends 6-8 hours daily sorting, organizing, and acquiring new items from thrift stores, library sales, and online auctions. Despite this extensive effort, he has made no meaningful progress in reducing the clutter. He becomes extremely anxious when his children suggest hiring a cleaning service or moving to a smaller apartment.

# Differential Diagnosis

## Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder

Why Consider: Dr. Whitman engages in repetitive behaviors (accumulating, organizing) and experiences anxiety related to his possessions.

Why Rule Out: While hoarding can occur as a symptom of OCD, in this case there are no classic OCD obsessions (intrusive, unwanted thoughts) or compulsions (repetitive behaviors aimed at neutralizing anxiety). Dr. Whitman does not report intrusive thoughts about contamination, harm, or symmetry that drive the hoarding. His difficulty discarding is driven by beliefs about the value and utility of items, not by obsessive fears that something terrible will happen if he discards them. Additionally, the acquisition behavior and the nature of the clutter (acquisition of new, unneeded items) are more characteristic of hoarding disorder than OCD-related hoarding.

---

## Major Depressive Disorder

Why Consider: Dr. Whitman experienced bereavement-related depression after his wife's death, and his hoarding accelerated during this period.

Why Rule Out: While Dr. Whitman experienced bereavement-related depression after his wife's death, his hoarding behavior predates this by decades and is not a symptom of depression. The hoarding is a long-standing pattern that worsened after a stressor, not a new behavior emerging in the context of a mood episode. His current mood symptoms (irritability, guardedness) are reactive to the citation and external pressure, not indicative of a current major depressive episode.

---

## Neurocognitive Disorder (Dementia)

Why Consider: Dr. Whitman is 68 years old and exhibits behavior that seems irrational. Hoarding can occur in the context of cognitive decline.

Why Rule Out: Dr. Whitman's cognition is intact. He is articulate, well-oriented, and intellectually engaged. He drives complex rationalizations about his hoarding and manages online purchases and delivery services. There is no evidence of memory impairment, executive dysfunction beyond the specific domain of discarding, or other cognitive decline. Hoarding in dementia typically involves a new onset in the context of clear cognitive decline, which is not present here.

---

## Normal Collecting

Why Consider: Dr. Whitman is a retired professor who has collected books and academic materials throughout his career.

Why Rule Out: The volume of items, the functional impairment, the safety hazards, and the distress/disturbance far exceed normal collecting behavior. Normal collecting does not result in inaccessible living spaces, fire hazards, social isolation, or legal consequences. Dr. Whitman's collecting has evolved into a pathological pattern that meets full criteria for hoarding disorder.

# Correct Diagnosis

Hoarding Disorder

DSM-5-TR Explanation: Hoarding disorder is characterized by persistent difficulty discarding or parting with possessions, regardless of their actual value. This difficulty is due to a perceived need to save the items and to distress associated with discarding them. The difficulty discarding possessions results in the accumulation of possessions that congest and clutter active living areas and substantially compromises their intended use. The hoarding causes clinically significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning (including maintaining a safe environment for self and others). The hoarding is not attributable to another medical condition and is not better explained by the symptoms of another mental disorder.

Diagnostic Reasoning: Dr. Whitman demonstrates persistent difficulty discarding possessions, regardless of their actual value, due to a perceived need to save them and intense distress associated with discarding. He has accumulated possessions to the point that they congest and clutter active living areas, rendering the stove inaccessible, one bathroom unusable, and pathways dangerously narrow. The hoarding causes significant distress (agitation when challenged) and impairment (social isolation, safety hazards, potential legal consequences, inability to prepare meals). The symptoms are not attributable to another medical condition or mental disorder: his cognition is intact, there are no obsessions in the OCD sense, and the behavior predates any depressive episode. His poor insight is consistent with the "with poor insight" specifier.

# Treatment Plan

## Psychotherapy

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) specifically adapted for hoarding disorder, which includes skills training (organization, decision-making), exposure to discarding items, and cognitive restructuring of beliefs about possessions (e.g., "Everything has potential value," "I might need this someday"). Motivational interviewing is essential given his poor insight and resistance to change. Home-based treatment is often necessary. Harm reduction (clearing pathways, ensuring functional bathroom and kitchen access) may be an initial goal before full decluttering.

## Medication

SSRIs (e.g., sertraline, venlafaxine) may be helpful, particularly if comorbid anxiety or depression is present, but pharmacotherapy alone is generally less effective than CBT for hoarding disorder. There is no FDA-approved medication specifically for hoarding disorder.

## Lifestyle

Structured daily routine with designated acquisition-free times; restriction of online shopping and thrift store visits; involvement of a professional organizer trained in hoarding disorder; gradual re-engagement with social activities outside the home; safety modifications to the home environment; regular medical monitoring given diabetes and hypertension.

## Prognosis

Guarded to moderate. Hoarding disorder is often chronic and treatment-resistant, particularly in older adults with long-standing patterns and poor insight. The presence of external pressure (city citation, family concern) may provide motivation for change. Relapse is common, and maintenance treatment and ongoing support are essential.

# Common Student Mistakes

- Diagnosing hoarding as OCD because of the repetitive nature of the behavior and the anxiety involved. However, hoarding disorder is now a separate diagnosis in DSM-5-TR, and the critical distinction is that the hoarding is not driven by classic OCD obsessions and compulsions. The beliefs about possessions (sentimental value, utility, identity) and the excessive acquisition are more characteristic of hoarding disorder.
- Attributing hoarding to normal collecting without recognizing the functional impairment, safety hazards, and distress that distinguish pathological hoarding.
- Missing the diagnosis by attributing symptoms to depression or cognitive decline without considering the long-standing pattern that predates any mood or cognitive symptoms.
- Failing to recognize that poor insight is extremely common in hoarding disorder and is a major barrier to treatment engagement.

# Memory Tricks

- **H-O-A-R-D**: **H**oarding is about difficulty **D**iscarding, not just acquiring; **O**rganizing without reducing clutter is typical; **A**ctual value of items is irrelevant to the patient; **R**ationalizations about item importance are common; **D**anger and dysfunction distinguish it from collecting
- Think of a dam that keeps filling with water but has no outlet—Dr. Whitman's house keeps filling with items, but nothing ever leaves. The water level (clutter) rises until it floods the functional spaces.
- Hoarding disorder was officially recognized as a distinct diagnosis in DSM-5 (2013), separating it from OCD for the first time. Research suggests that hoarding has a unique neurobiological profile, with different patterns of brain activation compared to OCD, particularly in regions involved in decision-making and emotional attachment to objects.

# Exam Pearls

- Hoarding disorder is distinct from normal collecting and from OCD-related hoarding. The key differentiator is that the accumulation results in active congestion of living areas and significant impairment, and the difficulty discarding is driven by beliefs about item value and identity, not by obsessive fears.
- Poor insight is extremely common in hoarding disorder and is a major barrier to treatment engagement. Patients often do not recognize the severity of the problem or the danger it creates.
- Home-based treatment is often necessary for hoarding disorder because the home environment is the primary site of the pathology. Harm reduction (ensuring safety and basic functioning) may be an appropriate initial goal before full decluttering.
- The excessive acquisition behavior (buying, collecting free items) is a core feature of hoarding disorder and distinguishes it from OCD-related hoarding, where acquisition is typically not prominent.

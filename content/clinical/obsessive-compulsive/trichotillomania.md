# Overview

Ms. Priya Sharma, a 31-year-old associate attorney at a corporate law firm, presents with a 19-year history of recurrent hair pulling resulting in visible hair loss, including multiple bald patches on her scalp, thinned eyebrows, and absent eyelashes. The case illustrates both "focused" and "automatic" pulling patterns, significant shame and concealment strategies, and the distinction from OCD, body dysmorphic disorder, and medical causes of hair loss.

# Presenting Complaint

Ms. Priya Sharma presented to the anxiety disorders clinic after her husband discovered a large collection of her pulled hair in a drawer and confronted her about it. She arrived at the appointment anxious and embarrassed, wearing a silk scarf covering her head despite the warm weather. She explained that she has been pulling out her hair since age 12, initially from her scalp but later expanding to her eyebrows and eyelashes.

Priya describes the hair-pulling as occurring in two distinct patterns. First, she engages in "focused" pulling during times of stress—before court appearances, while reviewing difficult case files, or after arguments with her husband. During these episodes, she is fully aware of the behavior and experiences a sense of tension before pulling and relief or gratification afterward. Second, she engages in "automatic" pulling while engaged in sedentary activities—reading, watching television, or driving—often without full awareness until she notices a pile of hair on her lap or desk.

She has developed elaborate camouflage strategies: she styles her remaining hair to cover bald patches, uses volumizing powders and hair fibers, wears hats and scarves in casual settings, and has considered wearing a wig. She has pulled approximately 40% of her scalp hair, leaving multiple irregular bald patches. Her eyebrows are significantly thinned, and she has no eyelashes on her left eye. She has tried repeatedly to stop or reduce the behavior, including wearing gloves, cutting her nails extremely short, and keeping her hands occupied with fidget toys, but these strategies have provided only temporary relief.

Priya reports significant shame about the behavior and its visible consequences. She avoids swimming, going to the gym, and attending social events where her hair might be seen. She has not told anyone except her husband about the behavior, and she fears that colleagues would judge her as "crazy" if they knew.

# Differential Diagnosis

## Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder

Why Consider: Priya engages in a repetitive behavior (hair pulling) that causes distress and impairment, and the behavior is classified under Obsessive-Compulsive and Related Disorders in DSM-5-TR.

Why Rule Out: While hair-pulling can occur in OCD, typically as a compulsive behavior aimed at neutralizing an obsession (e.g., pulling hair to relieve anxiety about contamination or symmetry), Priya's hair-pulling is not preceded by obsessions or performed according to rigid rules. The tension-relief pattern and the automatic pulling episodes are more characteristic of trichotillomania. Additionally, she has no other OCD symptoms (no obsessions, no other compulsions, no contamination fears or symmetry concerns).

---

## Body Dysmorphic Disorder

Why Consider: Priya is distressed about her appearance (bald patches, thin eyebrows) and engages in behaviors related to her appearance.

Why Rule Out: While Priya is distressed about her appearance (bald patches, thin eyebrows), her distress is a consequence of the hair-pulling behavior, not a preoccupation with perceived physical defects independent of the pulling. In BDD, the concern is with perceived flaws that are not observable or appear slight to others; Priya's hair loss is objectively visible and caused by her behavior. She does not examine her appearance excessively outside of the context of the pulling consequences, nor does she have appearance-related rituals beyond concealment of the damage.

---

## Alopecia Areata

Why Consider: Priya has patchy hair loss that could be consistent with an autoimmune condition.

Why Rule Out: Alopecia areata is an autoimmune condition causing patchy hair loss. Priya's hair loss has been thoroughly evaluated by dermatologists on two occasions, who ruled out medical causes. The pattern of hair loss (irregular patches corresponding to pulling sites, eyebrow and eyelash involvement) and her detailed report of the pulling behavior confirm a behavioral rather than medical etiology.

---

## Stereotypic Movement Disorder

Why Consider: Priya engages in a repetitive body-focused behavior.

Why Rule Out: Hair-pulling is not a stereotypy; it is a focused, goal-directed behavior with a tension-relief cycle, not a repetitive, seemingly purposeless movement. Stereotypic movement disorder involves repetitive, seemingly driven, and apparently purposeless motor behavior (e.g., hand waving, body rocking) that is not better explained by another condition.

# Treatment Plan

## Psychotherapy

Habit Reversal Training (HRT), a behavioral intervention specifically designed for body-focused repetitive behaviors. HRT involves three components: awareness training (identifying triggers and early warning signs of pulling), competing response training (learning to engage in an incompatible behavior when the urge arises, such as making a fist or touching a different body part), and motivation and compliance strategies. Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) and dialectical behavior therapy (DBT) skills may be added to address emotional regulation and distress tolerance.

## Medication

There is no FDA-approved medication specifically for trichotillomania. SSRIs (e.g., fluoxetine, sertraline) may help with comorbid anxiety or depression but have limited efficacy for the pulling behavior itself. N-acetylcysteine (NAC), an over-the-counter supplement, has shown some promise in reducing urges in trichotillomania. Clomipramine may be more effective than SSRIs but has more side effects.

## Lifestyle

Identify and modify environmental triggers (e.g., reducing sedentary time, changing study/work environment); use physical barriers (gloves, hats) during high-risk times; maintain a pulling log to increase awareness; engage in regular physical activity to reduce stress and boredom; improve sleep hygiene; consider support groups for body-focused repetitive behaviors.

## Prognosis

Moderate. Trichotillomania is often chronic with a waxing and waning course. Early intervention, good insight, and absence of severe comorbidities are positive prognostic factors. HRT can significantly reduce pulling frequency, but complete remission is less common. Relapse prevention and maintenance strategies are important.

# Common Student Mistakes

- Misdiagnosing trichotillomania as OCD because of the repetitive nature of the behavior and its classification in the same chapter. The critical distinction is the absence of obsessions and the presence of the tension-relief pattern. Additionally, students sometimes miss the diagnosis entirely by attributing the hair loss to a medical condition without considering a behavioral cause.
- Missing the diagnosis by attributing the hair loss to a medical condition (e.g., alopecia areata) without obtaining a thorough dermatological evaluation and patient history.
- Confusing trichotillomania with BDD by focusing on the appearance concerns without recognizing that the distress is about the consequences of pulling, not about perceived defects independent of the behavior.
- Failing to recognize the two distinct patterns of pulling (focused and automatic) and their different treatment implications.

# Memory Tricks

- **T-R-I-C-H**: **T**ension before pulling, **R**elief after pulling, **I**mpairment is significant, **C**oncealment is common, **H**air loss is the result
- Think of a tension spring—Priya's anxiety builds like a coiled spring (tension), and pulling the hair is the release (relief). This tension-relief cycle is the hallmark of trichotillomania.
- The term "trichotillomania" comes from the Greek *tricho-* (hair), *tillein* (to pull), and *mania* (madness or frenzy). It was first described by the French physician François Hallopeau in 1889, who coined the term after observing a patient who pulled out her hair.

# Exam Pearls

- Trichotillomania is classified under Obsessive-Compulsive and Related Disorders in DSM-5-TR, but it is distinct from OCD. The tension-relief pattern (increasing tension before pulling and relief/gratification after) is the hallmark feature that distinguishes it from OCD compulsions, which are typically performed to reduce anxiety from an obsession rather than to achieve gratification.
- There are two distinct patterns of hair pulling: "focused" pulling (intentional, in response to internal states like stress or anxiety, with awareness) and "automatic" pulling (unintentional, during sedentary activities, with reduced awareness). Most individuals exhibit both patterns.
- The "tension-relief" pattern is the key diagnostic feature that distinguishes trichotillomania from OCD and other conditions. If the patient does not report this pattern, reconsider the diagnosis.
- N-acetylcysteine (NAC) is an over-the-counter supplement that has shown some promise in reducing urges in trichotillomania, though evidence is mixed.

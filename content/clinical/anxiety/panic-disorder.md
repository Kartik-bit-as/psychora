# Overview

Mr. Daniel Park is a 32-year-old software engineer who presents with recurrent, unexpected panic attacks that have intensified over the past six months. He describes discrete episodes of intense fear with heart palpitations, shortness of breath, chest pain, dizziness, and a terrifying sense that he is dying or going crazy. The first attack occurred in a grocery store checkout line; he drove himself to the ER convinced he was having a heart attack. He has had approximately 15--20 panic attacks since then, occurring in various settings --- at work, while driving, at restaurants, during a movie, and twice while sleeping. The worst part, he says, is "the fear of the next one." He has developed extensive avoidance behaviors, transitioned to fully remote work, ended his relationship, and barely leaves his apartment. He carries a water bottle, paper bag, and phone with 911 on speed dial everywhere he goes.

---

# Learning Objectives

- Identify the diagnostic criteria for Panic Disorder, including the requirement for recurrent unexpected panic attacks followed by persistent worry or behavioral change
- Differentiate Panic Disorder from Generalized Anxiety Disorder, Specific Phobia, and Somatic Symptom Disorder
- Recognize that panic attacks can occur in any anxiety disorder but Panic Disorder requires unexpected attacks and persistent fear of future attacks
- Understand the role of medical workups in panic disorder presentation and why extensive negative workups do not rule out the diagnosis
- Appreciate the concept of "fear of fear" as the defining feature of panic disorder

# Presenting Complaint

Mr. Daniel Park arrived looking gaunt and exhausted, with dark circles under his eyes. He accepted water with a trembling hand and immediately positioned himself near the door.

"The first one happened six months ago," he said, his voice tight. "I was at the grocery store, in the checkout line. Suddenly my heart started pounding --- I mean, really pounding, like it was going to explode. I couldn't catch my breath. My hands went numb. I thought I was having a heart attack. I dropped my groceries and ran out of the store. I drove myself to the ER."

He paused, looking down at his hands. "They did an EKG, blood work, chest X-ray. Everything was normal. The doctor said it was a panic attack. I didn't believe him. I thought they missed something."

Daniel described a pattern that has intensified since that first episode. He has had approximately 15--20 panic attacks in the past six months, occurring in various settings --- at work, while driving, at restaurants, during a movie, and twice while sleeping. Each attack follows a similar trajectory: sudden onset of intense fear, heart palpitations, shortness of breath, chest pain, dizziness, tingling in his extremities, and a terrifying sense that he is dying or going crazy.

"The worst part isn't the attack itself," he said. "It's the fear of the next one. I used to love going out. Now I barely leave my apartment. I had to break up with my girlfriend because I couldn't go to restaurants or concerts or even her parents' house. I work from home now. I order groceries online. I'm 32 years old and I'm becoming a hermit because I'm afraid of my own body."

He reported that he now carries a bottle of water, a paper bag, and his phone (with 911 on speed dial) everywhere he goes. He has visited the ER three times in six months, each time convinced he was having a heart attack or stroke. All workups were normal.

"I know it sounds crazy," he said, his voice breaking. "I know the doctors said my heart is fine. But when it's happening --- when I'm gasping for air and my chest is crushing me --- I don't care what they said. I'm dying. And no one believes me."

# History of Present Illness

Daniel's panic attacks began abruptly and have become increasingly frequent and disabling.

**Duration:** 6 months of recurrent panic attacks. Attacks occur 2--4 times per month, with increasing frequency in past 2 months.

**Progression:** Worsening. Initially isolated attacks with full recovery between episodes. Now, attacks are more frequent and are accompanied by persistent worry about future attacks and significant behavioral changes (avoidance).

# Differential Diagnosis

## Generalized Anxiety Disorder

Why Consider: Daniel worries excessively and has anxiety symptoms.

Why Rule Out: While Daniel worries excessively, his worry is not generalized across multiple domains. It is specifically focused on panic attacks and their physical sensations. GAD requires worry about multiple areas (work, health, relationships, finances, future) that is pervasive and chronic. Daniel's anxiety is episodic and panic-focused. He does not worry about his job performance, his relationships (except as they are affected by panic), or global concerns. His worry is secondary to the panic attacks, not primary.

## Specific Phobia (Cardiac)

Why Consider: Daniel fears physical sensations and has health anxiety.

Why Rule Out: Daniel fears physical sensations and panic attacks, not a specific external object or situation. While he avoids certain situations (crowds, highways, restaurants), this avoidance is not driven by fear of the situation itself but by fear of having a panic attack in that situation. This is situational avoidance secondary to panic disorder, not a specific phobia. In specific phobia, the fear is of the object/situation itself (e.g., fear of spiders, fear of heights). Daniel would be perfectly comfortable in a crowded restaurant if he were guaranteed not to have a panic attack.

## Somatic Symptom Disorder

Why Consider: Daniel has somatic symptoms and health anxiety, with multiple medical visits.

Why Rule Out: While Daniel has somatic symptoms and health anxiety, his presentation is not consistent with SSD. SSD requires one or more distressing somatic symptoms with excessive thoughts, feelings, or behaviors related to those symptoms (disproportionate thoughts, high anxiety, excessive time/energy devoted to symptoms). Daniel's somatic symptoms are time-limited and episodic (panic attacks), not persistent somatic complaints. His primary fear is not of having an illness but of dying during a panic attack. Between attacks, he does not ruminate about illness or seek excessive medical care --- he avoids situations to prevent attacks. The medical visits are reactive to acute episodes, not part of a pattern of illness preoccupation.

## Agoraphobia

Why Consider: Daniel has significant agoraphobic avoidance (fear of situations where escape might be difficult).

Why Rule Out: Daniel has significant agoraphobic avoidance, but in DSM-5, agoraphobia is coded separately only if fears extend beyond panic-like symptoms. His avoidance is specifically panic-related, making panic disorder the primary diagnosis. Agoraphobia can be specified if criteria are met.

## Postural Orthostatic Tachycardia Syndrome (POTS)

Why Consider: Can cause palpitations and dizziness.

Why Rule Out: Symptoms are orthostatic and persistent, not episodic with fear. Workups negative.

## Pheochromocytoma

Why Consider: Can cause episodic hypertension, palpitations, sweating.

Why Rule Out: Ruled out by negative workup.

## Cardiac Arrhythmia

Why Consider: Can cause palpitations and chest pain.

Why Rule Out: EKG, echo, stress test all normal. Panic attacks not exercise-induced.

## Hyperthyroidism

Why Consider: Can cause anxiety and palpitations.

Why Rule Out: TSH normal.

# Correct Diagnosis

**Panic Disorder**

## DSM-5-TR Explanation

Daniel meets full criteria for Panic Disorder:

## Diagnostic Reasoning

1. **Recurrent unexpected panic attacks** --- discrete periods of intense fear with 4+ somatic/cognitive symptoms. He has had 15--20 attacks with 11 symptoms each.

2. **At least one attack followed by ≥1 month of persistent concern about future attacks** --- he has had 6 months of persistent worry.

3. **Significant maladaptive behavior change** --- extensive avoidance (remote work, social withdrawal, driving restrictions, online ordering, relationship termination).

4. **Not attributable to substance or medical condition** --- comprehensive cardiac, neurological, and endocrine workups are negative.

5. **Not better explained by another mental disorder** --- panic attacks are not restricted to phobic objects (specific phobia), social evaluation (social anxiety), or reminders of trauma (PTSD).

## Required Criteria

- Criterion A: Recurrent unexpected panic attacks. A panic attack is an abrupt surge of intense fear or intense discomfort that reaches a peak within minutes, and during which time four (or more) of the following symptoms occur: palpitations, sweating, trembling, shortness of breath, feelings of choking, chest pain, nausea, dizziness, derealization/depersonalization, fear of losing control, fear of dying
- Criterion B: At least one of the attacks has been followed by 1 month (or more) of one or both of the following: persistent concern or worry about additional panic attacks or their consequences, a significant maladaptive change in behavior related to the attacks
- Criterion C: The disturbance is not attributable to the physiological effects of a substance or another medical condition
- Criterion D: The disturbance is not better explained by another mental disorder

## Criteria Met

- Criterion A: 15--20 recurrent unexpected panic attacks with 11/13 symptoms (minimum 4 required)
- Criterion B: 6 months of persistent worry about future attacks + significant maladaptive behavior change (avoidance, remote work, relationship termination, social withdrawal)
- Criterion C: Not attributable to substance or medical condition (comprehensive negative workups)
- Criterion D: Not better explained by another mental disorder

## Criteria Not Met

- None. All criteria for Panic Disorder are met.

# Treatment Plan

## Psychotherapy

- **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) with panic control treatment:** Gold standard. Includes psychoeducation about panic physiology, cognitive restructuring of catastrophic interpretations ("heart palpitations = heart attack"), interoceptive exposure (deliberately inducing panic sensations in safe contexts), and in-vivo exposure to avoided situations.

- **Panic-Focused Psychodynamic Psychotherapy:** Alternative for those who prefer depth-oriented work.

- **Breathing retraining:** To prevent hyperventilation during attacks (but not as sole treatment, as it can become safety behavior).

## Medication

- **SSRIs** (e.g., sertraline, paroxetine, fluoxetine) --- first-line pharmacotherapy. High-dose SSRIs often needed for panic disorder.

- **SNRIs** (e.g., venlafaxine) --- equally effective.

- **Benzodiazepines** (e.g., clonazepam, alprazolam) --- short-term only for severe cases; risk of dependence and interference with exposure therapy.

- **Beta-blockers** --- not first-line for panic (don't target anticipatory anxiety), but may help with performance anxiety.

- *Individual medication selection by prescribing clinician only.*

## Lifestyle

- Gradual, systematic exposure to avoided situations (with therapist guidance)
- Elimination of safety behaviors (water bottle, paper bag, constant pulse-checking)
- Regular aerobic exercise (cardiac reassurance, anxiety reduction)
- Sleep hygiene
- Caffeine elimination (already done)
- Alcohol reduction
- Support group for panic disorder

## Prognosis

Excellent with appropriate treatment. Panic disorder is one of the most treatable anxiety disorders. CBT has remission rates of 70--80%. However, Daniel's 6-month delay in seeking psychiatric care has allowed significant avoidance and secondary depression to develop. The longer avoidance persists, the harder it is to reverse. Early intensive intervention is critical. His insight is good, and he has no comorbid depression --- strong prognostic indicators.

# Common Student Mistakes

- Missing panic disorder because the patient has extensive medical workups. Students see multiple ER visits, cardiology consultations, and negative workups and assume the patient has somatic symptom disorder or is "just anxious." However, panic disorder frequently presents with medical help-seeking because the physical symptoms are so severe and realistic.
- Confusing panic disorder with specific phobia because of avoidance behaviors. The avoidance in panic disorder is driven by fear of panic attacks, not fear of the situations themselves.
- Failing to recognize that panic attacks can occur in sleep (nocturnal panic attacks), which strongly supports panic disorder over other explanations.
- Prescribing benzodiazepines as first-line treatment without considering SSRIs and CBT.

# Memory Tricks

- **"Fear of fear"**: The defining feature of panic disorder is not the panic attack itself but the fear of having another one.
- **"Unexpected + Unwanted"**: Panic attacks in panic disorder are unexpected (uncued) and unwanted --- unlike phobic panic attacks which are expected when encountering the phobic stimulus.
- **"The body lies"**: Panic disorder patients experience terrifying physical sensations that are actually harmless --- the brain misinterprets normal arousal as danger.
- Think of a smoke alarm: In panic disorder, the smoke alarm goes off when there's no fire. The treatment isn't to remove the alarm (suppress symptoms) but to recalibrate it (restructure catastrophic beliefs).

---

# Exam Pearls

- The "fear of fear" is the defining feature of panic disorder. Patients like Daniel are not afraid of grocery stores, crowds, or driving --- they are afraid of the internal sensations that might occur in those settings.
- This is why panic disorder is so disabling: the feared stimulus is internal and unavoidable. You can avoid spiders; you cannot avoid your own heartbeat.
- Treatment must target interoceptive exposure --- deliberately experiencing the physical sensations of panic in safe contexts to learn that they are harmless.
- Breathing into a paper bag or carrying water bottles are safety behaviors that maintain the disorder by preventing disconfirmation of catastrophic beliefs.
- Panic disorder has one of the highest rates of medical misdiagnosis in psychiatry. Studies show that patients with panic disorder visit emergency departments an average of 10 times before receiving a correct psychiatric diagnosis, and many undergo unnecessary cardiac catheterizations.
- The term "panic attack" was coined in the 1960s by psychiatrist Donald Klein, who noticed that patients with these episodes responded to imipramine (a tricyclic antidepressant) but not to benzodiazepines --- a pharmacological fingerprint that helped distinguish panic from generalized anxiety.

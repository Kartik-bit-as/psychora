# Overview

Mrs. Elena Voss, a 34-year-old married mother of two, presents with an 18-month history of overwhelming fear of being away from her husband, Marcus. What began as mild discomfort when running errands alone has escalated to full-blown panic attacks, complete avoidance of independent activities, and significant impairment in occupational, social, and family functioning. The onset followed a minor car accident involving her husband. This case illustrates adult-onset separation anxiety disorder, a frequently underdiagnosed condition.

# Presenting Complaint

Mrs. Elena Voss presented to the outpatient psychology clinic accompanied by her husband, Marcus. She appeared visibly distressed, clutching her husband's arm as she entered the waiting room. When the clinician invited her in for an individual session, Elena hesitated and asked if Marcus could join her. After reassurance that he could wait nearby, she reluctantly entered the office but left the door slightly ajar.

Elena explained that over the past 18 months, she has developed an overwhelming fear of being away from her husband. What started as mild discomfort when running errands alone has escalated to full-blown panic attacks. She described episodes of heart palpitations, sweating, trembling, and a terrifying sense that something catastrophic would happen to Marcus or that she would never see him again. These attacks occur predictably whenever she attempts to leave the house without him.

She has stopped driving, quit her in-person job to work remotely, and now relies entirely on Marcus for grocery shopping, school drop-offs, and even walking the dog. She feels intense guilt about the burden she places on her family but describes her fear as "bigger than my logic." She worries constantly that Marcus will die in a car accident, develop a sudden illness, or simply decide not to return home. At night, she checks his breathing repeatedly and cannot fall asleep unless she is touching him.

# Differential Diagnosis

## Panic Disorder

Why Consider: Elena experiences panic attacks with physical symptoms (heart palpitations, sweating, trembling).

Why Rule Out: In panic disorder, panic attacks are unexpected and not tied to a specific situational trigger. Elena's attacks are consistently and exclusively triggered by separation from her husband or anticipation thereof. She does not report unexpected panic attacks in other contexts, nor does she fear the panic attacks themselves as the primary concern.

---

## Agoraphobia

Why Consider: Elena avoids many situations (driving, grocery shopping, social activities) and experiences panic-like symptoms.

Why Rule Out: While Elena avoids many situations, her avoidance is not driven by fear of panic-like symptoms, fear of incapacitation, or fear of being unable to escape. Instead, it is specifically about being away from her husband. If she could take her husband with her, she would readily engage in previously avoided activities. In agoraphobia, the fear persists even with a trusted companion in many cases, and the core fear is of the situation itself or of having panic-like symptoms in public.

---

## Dependent Personality Disorder

Why Consider: Elena shows dependency on her husband and has difficulty functioning independently.

Why Rule Out: While Elena shows dependency on her husband, dependent personality disorder involves a pervasive pattern of submissive and clinging behavior across multiple relationships and domains, not specifically fear of separation. Elena's presentation is characterized by acute anxiety and panic in response to separation, which is more consistent with an anxiety disorder pattern than a personality disorder. Additionally, her symptoms have a clear onset and course more typical of an anxiety disorder.

---

## Generalized Anxiety Disorder

Why Consider: Elena reports excessive worry and anxiety.

Why Rule Out: Her worries are not generalized across multiple domains but are specifically focused on separation from her husband and his safety. The anxiety is not about everyday concerns but is attachment-specific.

---

# Correct Diagnosis

Separation Anxiety Disorder

DSM-5-TR Explanation: Separation anxiety disorder involves excessive fear or anxiety concerning separation from those to whom the individual is attached. The fear, anxiety, or avoidance is persistent, lasting at least 4 weeks in children and adolescents and typically 6 months or more in adults. The disturbance causes clinically significant distress or impairment in social, academic, occupational, or other important areas of functioning.

Diagnostic Reasoning: Elena exhibits multiple core symptoms of separation anxiety disorder, including recurrent excessive distress when anticipating or experiencing separation from her husband, persistent worry about losing him or harm befalling him, and persistent reluctance to go out or be alone without him. The fear is clearly excessive, developmentally inappropriate for her age, and has persisted for more than 4 weeks (in fact, 18 months). The avoidance has caused significant impairment in occupational, social, and family functioning. Importantly, her symptoms are not better explained by another disorder: her panic attacks are situationally bound to separation rather than unexpected, and her worries are specifically about attachment-related harm rather than generalized or social concerns.

Required Criteria:
A. Developmentally inappropriate and excessive fear or anxiety concerning separation from those to whom the individual is attached, as evidenced by at least three of the following:
   1. Recurrent excessive distress when anticipating or experiencing separation from home or from major attachment figures
   2. Persistent and excessive worry about losing major attachment figures or about possible harm to them
   3. Persistent and excessive worry that an untoward event will lead to separation from a major attachment figure
   4. Persistent reluctance or refusal to go out, away from home, to school, to work, or elsewhere because of fear of separation
   5. Persistent and excessive fear of or reluctance about being alone or without major attachment figures at home or in other settings
   6. Persistent reluctance or refusal to sleep away from home or to go to sleep without being near a major attachment figure
   7. Repeated nightmares involving the theme of separation
   8. Repeated complaints of physical symptoms when separation from major attachment figures occurs or is anticipated
B. The fear, anxiety, or avoidance is persistent, lasting at least 4 weeks in children and adolescents and typically 6 months or more in adults.
C. The disturbance causes clinically significant distress or impairment in social, academic, occupational, or other important areas of functioning.
D. The disturbance is not better explained by another mental disorder.

Criteria Met: A (1, 2, 4, 6, 7, 8), B, C, D

Criteria Not Met: None

# Treatment Plan

## Psychotherapy

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) with a focus on graduated exposure to separation. Treatment would involve creating a hierarchy of separation scenarios (e.g., Marcus going to another room, then to the backyard, then to the store for 15 minutes, etc.), paired with cognitive restructuring of catastrophic beliefs about harm befalling her husband. Psychoeducation about the nature of anxiety and the role of avoidance in maintaining symptoms would be essential early components.

## Medication

SSRIs (e.g., sertraline, escitalopram) may be considered if symptoms are severe enough to impair engagement in psychotherapy, though CBT alone is often effective for separation anxiety disorder. Short-term use of a benzodiazepine is generally not recommended due to risk of dependence and interference with exposure work.

## Lifestyle

Gradual re-engagement in independent activities; structured daily routine to reduce anticipatory anxiety; mindfulness or relaxation techniques; regular physical activity as tolerated; sleep hygiene improvement; couples communication strategies to reduce marital strain while supporting independence.

## Prognosis

Good with appropriate CBT intervention. Adult-onset cases may require longer treatment duration than childhood cases but generally respond well to exposure-based approaches. Relapse prevention focusing on maintaining gains and addressing life stressors is important.

# Common Student Mistakes

- Misdiagnosing adult separation anxiety as dependent personality disorder because both involve reliance on others. The critical distinction is that separation anxiety disorder features acute, distressing anxiety and panic in response to separation, whereas dependent personality disorder involves a pervasive pattern of submissiveness and neediness without necessarily involving panic-level anxiety about separation.
- Assuming separation anxiety disorder only occurs in children and failing to consider it in adults.
- Confusing the diagnosis with panic disorder by focusing on the panic attacks without recognizing they are exclusively triggered by separation.
- Missing the diagnosis by attributing symptoms to agoraphobia without recognizing that the fear is specifically about separation, not about the situations themselves.

# Exam Pearls

- Separation anxiety disorder in adults is often underdiagnosed because clinicians assume it is exclusively a childhood disorder. Adult presentations frequently involve romantic partners rather than parents, and the disorder may first appear in adulthood following a stressor or loss that activates attachment fears.
- The prevalence of adult separation anxiety disorder is estimated at 6-7% of the general population—higher than many clinicians realize—yet it remains one of the most under-recognized anxiety disorders in adult psychiatric practice.
- Always assess whether panic attacks are unexpected (panic disorder) or situationally bound (separation anxiety, specific phobia, social anxiety).
- Adult separation anxiety can present with panic attacks, but the key is that the panic is exclusively triggered by separation from an attachment figure.

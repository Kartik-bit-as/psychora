# Overview

Lily Park, a 7-year-old first-grade student, presents with a consistent pattern of failure to speak in specific social situations (school, with unfamiliar adults) despite speaking normally in other situations (home, with family). The disturbance has lasted well over one month, is not limited to the first month of school, and significantly interferes with her educational achievement and social communication. This case illustrates a classic, prototypical presentation of selective mutism in a young child.

---

# Presenting Complaint

Ms. Jennifer Park brought her daughter, Lily, to the child psychology clinic at the urging of Lily's first-grade teacher. Lily walked into the waiting room holding her mother's hand, eyes cast downward, and did not respond when the receptionist greeted her. In the therapy room, Lily sat quietly on a small chair, clutching a stuffed rabbit, and did not make eye contact with the clinician. When the clinician asked Lily her name, Lily looked at her mother expectantly. Ms. Park gently encouraged Lily to answer, but Lily remained silent, eventually shaking her head "no" while maintaining a frozen, anxious posture.

Ms. Park explained that Lily is a "completely different child" at home. She speaks in full, complex sentences, tells elaborate stories, sings songs, and argues with her brother. She has a large vocabulary and advanced language skills for her age. However, since starting first grade three months ago, Lily has not spoken a single word at school—not to her teacher, not to classmates, not even to ask to use the bathroom. She uses nonverbal communication (nodding, pointing, writing notes) to meet basic needs.

Lily's kindergarten teacher had noted that Lily was "very quiet" and rarely volunteered to speak, but she did respond when called upon and spoke to a few close friends. The transition to first grade with a new teacher and larger class appears to have extinguished even that minimal verbal participation.

# History of Present Illness

**Onset:** First noticed in preschool at age 4, when Lily would not speak to teachers but spoke to select peers. Worsened significantly at the start of first grade (age 7).

**Duration:** Present for approximately 3 years, with significant worsening over the past 3 months.

**Progression:** Preschool: minimal speech with teachers, some speech with peers. Kindergarten: rare speech with teacher, speech with 2-3 close friends. First grade: complete absence of speech in school setting. Home environment remains unaffected throughout.

**Triggers:** New school environments, new teachers, unfamiliar adults, being the center of attention, group settings.

**Impact on Life:** Academic performance is difficult to assess because Lily cannot verbally participate in class discussions or ask questions. Teacher reports she completes written work adequately but may be falling behind in oral reading assessments. Socially, she has no friends at school because peers find her silence "weird" and have stopped trying to engage her. She eats lunch alone and plays alone at recess. She has developed somatic complaints (stomachaches) on school mornings. Parents report she cries before school and asks to stay home.

# Diagnostic Clues

- Normal speech and language abilities in at least one setting (home), ruling out primary communication disorders
- Mutism is specifically tied to certain social environments and people, not global
- Clear signs of social anxiety in the affected settings (freezing, avoidance of eye contact, somatic complaints)
- Child uses nonverbal communication to compensate for verbal mutism
- Stark contrast between verbal behavior in comfortable settings versus uncomfortable settings
- Child wants to speak but appears physically unable to do so due to anxiety
- Progressive worsening with transitions to new environments

---

# Differential Diagnosis

## Autism Spectrum Disorder

Why Consider: Lily shows social withdrawal, limited eye contact, and communication difficulties in certain settings.

Why Rule Out: Lily shows no restricted interests, repetitive behaviors, sensory sensitivities, or social communication deficits outside of the mutism. At home, she engages in reciprocal social interaction, imaginative play, and complex conversation. Her eye contact and social engagement are normal in comfortable settings. The selective nature of her symptoms and the presence of normal social behavior in familiar contexts argue against autism.

---

## Language Disorder

Why Consider: Lily has significant communication difficulties in the school setting.

Why Rule Out: Lily's speech and language evaluation at age 6 confirmed age-appropriate receptive and expressive language skills. She speaks in complex sentences at home, demonstrating intact language capacity. A language disorder would present consistently across all settings, not selectively in social situations.

---

## Social Anxiety Disorder

Why Consider: Lily clearly has social anxiety, with avoidance of social interaction, fear of unfamiliar people, and physical symptoms of anxiety.

Why Rule Out: While Lily clearly has social anxiety, social anxiety disorder does not specifically involve failure to speak. Many children with social anxiety disorder speak, albeit minimally or with great discomfort. Selective mutism is the more precise diagnosis when the primary manifestation is a complete failure to speak in specific social situations despite normal speech elsewhere. The two conditions are highly comorbid, but selective mutism captures the specific symptom of mutism that requires targeted intervention.

---

## Reactive Attachment Disorder

Why Consider: Lily shows limited social engagement with unfamiliar adults.

Why Rule Out: No history of neglect, institutional care, or attachment disruptions. Lily has a secure attachment to her parents and speaks freely at home.

# Correct Diagnosis

Selective Mutism

DSM-5-TR Explanation: Selective mutism is characterized by a consistent failure to speak in specific social situations in which there is an expectation for speaking (e.g., at school) despite speaking in other situations (e.g., at home). The disturbance interferes with educational or occupational achievement or with social communication. The duration of the disturbance is at least 1 month (not limited to the first month of school). The failure to speak is not attributable to a lack of knowledge of, or comfort with, the spoken language required in the social situation. The disturbance is not better explained by a communication disorder and does not occur exclusively during the course of autism spectrum disorder, schizophrenia, or another psychotic disorder.

Diagnostic Reasoning: Lily demonstrates a consistent pattern of failure to speak in specific social situations (school, with unfamiliar adults) despite speaking normally in other situations (home, with family). The disturbance has lasted well over one month, is not limited to the first month of school, and significantly interferes with her educational achievement and social communication. Her mutism is clearly associated with marked anxiety in social settings (freezing, avoidance of eye contact, somatic complaints, crying). Importantly, her speech and language abilities are intact, and the mutism is not better explained by a communication disorder, autism, or another mental disorder.

Required Criteria:
A. Consistent failure to speak in specific social situations in which there is an expectation for speaking (e.g., at school) despite speaking in other situations.
B. The disturbance interferes with educational or occupational achievement or with social communication.
C. The duration of the disturbance is at least 1 month (not limited to the first month of school).
D. The failure to speak is not attributable to a lack of knowledge of, or comfort with, the spoken language required in the social situation.
E. The disturbance is not better explained by a communication disorder and does not occur exclusively during the course of autism spectrum disorder, schizophrenia, or another psychotic disorder.

Criteria Met: A, B, C, D, E

# Treatment Plan

## Psychotherapy

Behavioral intervention using stimulus fading and shaping techniques. Treatment would begin with Lily speaking to her mother in the school setting, then gradually introducing the teacher while mother remains present, then fading mother's presence. Contingency management (positive reinforcement for verbalization) and self-modeling (video recordings of Lily speaking at home played in the school setting) are evidence-based approaches. Cognitive-behavioral techniques appropriate for her developmental level may be added as she progresses.

## Medication

SSRIs (e.g., fluoxetine, sertraline) may be considered if symptoms are severe and do not respond to behavioral intervention alone, or if comorbid social anxiety disorder is present and impairing. Medication should always be paired with behavioral intervention in children.

## Lifestyle

Maintain predictable routines to reduce anticipatory anxiety; provide safe, non-pressured opportunities for social interaction outside of school (e.g., small playdates with one familiar peer); educate teachers about not pressuring Lily to speak and accepting nonverbal communication initially; involve parents in treatment to ensure consistency between home and school approaches.

## Prognosis

Generally good with early intervention, though some children may continue to experience social anxiety into adolescence and adulthood. Children who receive treatment before age 10 tend to have better outcomes. Without intervention, selective mutism can lead to chronic social anxiety, academic underachievement, and peer rejection.

# Common Student Mistakes

- Diagnosing selective mutism as autism spectrum disorder because of the apparent social withdrawal. The critical distinguishing feature is that children with selective mutism show normal social reciprocity and communication in comfortable settings, whereas autism involves pervasive social communication deficits across all contexts.
- Attributing the mutism to shyness or stubbornness rather than recognizing it as an anxiety disorder characterized by a physiological inability to speak in specific settings.
- Pushing the child to speak or punishing non-speech, which worsens symptoms. The goal of treatment is to reduce anxiety first, which allows speech to emerge naturally.
- Missing the diagnosis by attributing symptoms to a language disorder without considering the setting-specific nature of the mutism.

# Exam Pearls

- Selective mutism was originally called "elective mutism" in earlier DSM editions, implying that children were choosing not to speak. The name was changed to "selective mutism" in DSM-IV to reflect that the behavior is driven by anxiety, not willful defiance or opposition.
- Selective mutism is not "just shyness" or stubbornness—it is an anxiety disorder characterized by a physiological inability to speak in specific settings.
- The comorbidity between selective mutism and social anxiety disorder is very high (estimated 80-90%), but selective mutism is diagnosed when the mutism is the primary and most impairing symptom.
- Early intervention before age 10 is associated with significantly better outcomes.

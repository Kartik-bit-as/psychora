# Overview

Dr. Samuel Okonkwo, a 42-year-old associate professor of history, presents with an 8-month history of progressive avoidance of public spaces and situations where escape might be difficult or help unavailable if panic-like symptoms develop. What began as a panic attack during a university lecture has generalized to near-complete home confinement, causing severe occupational, social, and functional impairment. This case illustrates the development and progression of agoraphobia following an initial panic attack.

---

# Learning Objectives

- Distinguish agoraphobia from panic disorder based on the nature of the feared situations
- Recognize the progressive generalization of avoidance in agoraphobia
- Identify the core fear in agoraphobia: difficulty escaping or obtaining help if panic-like symptoms develop
- Differentiate agoraphobia from social anxiety disorder and specific phobia
- Understand the occupational and social consequences of severe agoraphobia

# Presenting Complaint

Dr. Samuel Okonkwo was referred to the university counseling center by his department chair after a prolonged absence from teaching duties. He arrived at the clinic appearing disheveled and anxious, requesting to sit near the door. He explained that approximately 8 months ago, while lecturing to 120 students in a large auditorium, he experienced a sudden, overwhelming panic attack. He described intense heart palpitations, dizziness, sweating, and a terrifying fear that he was having a heart attack or would lose consciousness in front of his students. He managed to finish the lecture but was shaken for days.

Over the following weeks, Dr. Okonkwo developed a profound fear of having similar attacks in public or situations where escape would be difficult or embarrassing. He began avoiding large lecture halls, then smaller classrooms, then any teaching environment. Six months ago, he stopped attending campus entirely and requested medical leave. The avoidance generalized rapidly: he stopped going to grocery stores (fear of long checkout lines), shopping malls, movie theaters, public transportation, and restaurants. He now orders all groceries for delivery and has not left his apartment building in over two months except for brief walks directly outside his building, where he remains within sight of his front door.

He reports that even thinking about entering a crowded space triggers intense anxiety. He has had three additional panic attacks in the past 6 months, all occurring when he attempted to push his boundaries (e.g., walking two blocks to a pharmacy). He describes his world as "shrinking" and expresses profound shame about his professional failure and isolation.

# Differential Diagnosis

## Panic Disorder

Why Consider: Dr. Okonkwo experiences panic attacks with physical symptoms (heart palpitations, dizziness, sweating).

Why Rule Out: In panic disorder, panic attacks are unexpected and recurrent, and the central fear is of having additional panic attacks or their consequences. Dr. Okonkwo's panic attacks are consistently triggered by agoraphobic situations (crowds, lecture halls, being far from home). His primary fear is not of panic attacks themselves but of being in situations where escape would be difficult if symptoms occurred. Panic disorder can co-occur with agoraphobia, but the agoraphobic avoidance is the dominant clinical feature here.

---

## Social Anxiety Disorder

Why Consider: Dr. Okonkwo's first panic attack occurred while teaching in front of students, and he avoids social and performance situations.

Why Rule Out: While Dr. Okonkwo's first panic attack occurred while teaching, his fear is not primarily about negative evaluation or social scrutiny. He does not fear embarrassing himself socially; he fears having panic-like symptoms in situations where escape is difficult. His avoidance has generalized to non-social situations (grocery stores, public transportation, walking outside alone) where social evaluation is not a factor.

---

## Specific Phobia (Situational Type)

Why Consider: Dr. Okonkwo has marked fear about specific situations (lecture halls, grocery stores, public transportation).

Why Rule Out: Specific phobia involves marked fear about a specific object or situation. Dr. Okonkwo fears multiple distinct types of situations (public transport, crowds, open spaces, being outside home alone), which is characteristic of agoraphobia rather than a specific phobia. The breadth of feared situations across multiple agoraphobic categories points to agoraphobia.

---

## Major Depressive Disorder

Why Consider: Dr. Okonkwo has depressive symptoms including feelings of worthlessness, hopelessness, and social isolation.

Why Rule Out: Depressive symptoms are secondary to the agoraphobic impairment and isolation; the primary clinical picture is anxiety-driven avoidance. The depression emerged after the agoraphobia had already significantly impaired his functioning.

# Correct Diagnosis

Agoraphobia

DSM-5-TR Explanation: Agoraphobia is characterized by marked fear or anxiety about two (or more) of the following five situations: (1) using public transportation, (2) being in open spaces, (3) being in enclosed spaces, (4) standing in line or being in a crowd, (5) being outside of the home alone. The individual fears or avoids these situations because of thoughts that escape might be difficult or help might not be available in the event of developing panic-like symptoms or other incapacitating or embarrassing symptoms. The agoraphobic situations almost always provoke fear or anxiety. The agoraphobic situations are actively avoided, require the presence of a companion, or are endured with intense fear or anxiety. The fear, anxiety, or avoidance is persistent, typically lasting for 6 months or more. The fear, anxiety, or avoidance causes clinically significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning.

Diagnostic Reasoning: Dr. Okonkwo exhibits marked fear or anxiety about multiple agoraphobic situations, including public transportation, open spaces, enclosed spaces (auditoriums, classrooms), standing in line, and being outside his home alone. His fear centers on the belief that escape from these situations might be difficult or that help would be unavailable if he developed panic-like symptoms or other incapacitating symptoms. He actively avoids these situations, and the avoidance has caused significant distress and impairment in occupational and social functioning. The symptoms have persisted for over 6 months. Importantly, his symptoms are not better explained by another disorder: while he has panic attacks, they are not unexpected (they occur in agoraphobic situations), and his primary fear is of the situations themselves rather than of the panic attacks per se.

# Treatment Plan

## Psychotherapy

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) with graduated exposure to agoraphobic situations. Treatment would begin with constructing a hierarchy of feared situations (e.g., standing in apartment hallway, walking to mailbox, walking one block, entering small store, etc.) and systematically exposing Dr. Okonkwo to each level while preventing avoidance. Cognitive restructuring would target catastrophic beliefs about panic symptoms (e.g., "I will die," "I will lose control"). Interoceptive exposure (inducing physical sensations similar to panic in safe settings) would reduce fear of bodily sensations.

## Medication

SSRIs (e.g., sertraline, paroxetine) are first-line pharmacological treatment for agoraphobia, often used in combination with CBT. Benzodiazepines may provide short-term relief for acute situations but are not recommended as monotherapy due to dependence risk and interference with exposure work.

## Lifestyle

Gradual re-engagement in physical activity to address deconditioning; structured daily schedule to reduce avoidance; reduction of alcohol use (currently used as anxiolytic); sleep hygiene improvement; social reconnection through low-pressure activities; vocational counseling regarding return to teaching.

## Prognosis

Good with intensive CBT and possibly SSRI treatment. However, severe, chronic agoraphobia with significant avoidance can be challenging to treat and may require longer-term intervention. Relapse prevention is crucial, particularly around life stressors that might trigger renewed avoidance.

# Common Student Mistakes

- Diagnosing agoraphobia as panic disorder with agoraphobia (an older DSM-IV formulation) or missing agoraphobia entirely by focusing only on the panic attacks. In DSM-5-TR, agoraphobia is a separate diagnosis, and the critical feature is fear of multiple agoraphobic situations due to concern about escape difficulty or unavailability of help.
- Confusing agoraphobia with social anxiety disorder by focusing on the teaching/performance context of the first panic attack without recognizing the generalized avoidance of non-social situations.
- Missing the diagnosis by attributing symptoms to specific phobia without recognizing the multiple categories of feared situations.
- Failing to recognize that agoraphobia often develops as a complication of panic attacks, but the diagnosis requires that the fear encompasses the agoraphobic situations themselves, not just fear of panic.

# Memory Tricks

- **AGORA**: **A**voidance of multiple situations, **G**eneralized fear of being trapped, **O**utside the home alone is feared, **R**equires escape to feel safe, **A**goraphobic situations provoke panic
- Think of the ancient Greek agora (marketplace)—a bustling public space where one might feel trapped. Dr. Okonkwo fears all such public gathering places.
- The word "agoraphobia" comes from the Greek *agora* (marketplace or gathering place) and *phobos* (fear). In ancient Greece, the agora was the central public space, making the etymology particularly apt for a disorder characterized by fear of public spaces and crowds.

# Exam Pearls

- Agoraphobia often develops as a complication of panic attacks, but the diagnosis requires that the fear encompasses the agoraphobic situations themselves, not just fear of panic.
- In clinical practice, many patients with agoraphobia also have panic disorder, but the agoraphobia diagnosis captures the extensive avoidance that drives impairment.
- The critical diagnostic question is: What is the patient afraid will happen in these situations? In agoraphobia, the fear is that escape will be difficult or help unavailable if panic-like symptoms develop—not social judgment or specific phobic stimuli.
- The breadth of feared situations across multiple agoraphobic categories distinguishes agoraphobia from specific phobia.

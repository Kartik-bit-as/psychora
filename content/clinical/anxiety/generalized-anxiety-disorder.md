# Overview

Ms. Sophie Brennan is a 26-year-old junior accountant who describes a lifetime of excessive worry that has intensified since her promotion six months ago. She worries about work deadlines, her fiancé's safety, her parents' health, wedding details, climate change, her car breaking down, saying something stupid in meetings, the economy, her future children's college funds (she is not pregnant), and whether she left the stove on (she checks it three times before leaving). She describes her brain as "a hamster wheel that never stops." She has never experienced panic attacks, and her anxiety is not confined to specific phobic situations or social evaluation --- it is generalized and pervasive. She has started avoiding social events because "they're too unpredictable" and has declined a destination bachelorette party because "what if the plane crashes?"

- Identify the diagnostic criteria for Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD), including the requirement for excessive worry about multiple domains

- Appreciate the distinction between worry-driven checking behaviors (GAD) and obsession-driven compulsions (OCD)

# Presenting Complaint

Ms. Sophie Brennan arrived 20 minutes early for her appointment, explaining that she had left work "just in case there was traffic" despite the clinic being a 10-minute drive away. She sat with her shoulders hunched, a leather planner clutched in her lap, and checked her phone repeatedly.

"I know it sounds ridiculous," she began, "but I worry about things that haven't happened yet, might never happen, and even things that already happened that I can't change. My brain is like a hamster wheel that never stops."

Sophie described a lifetime of excessive worry that has intensified since her promotion to junior accountant six months ago. She worries about work deadlines, her fiancé's safety, her parents' health, wedding details, climate change, her car breaking down, saying something stupid in meetings, the economy, her future children's college funds (she is not pregnant), and whether she left the stove on (she checks it three times before leaving).

"The worst part is that I know my worries are irrational," she said, her voice tight. "I know the stove is off. I know my fiancé is safe at work. But if I don't check, if I don't plan for every contingency, something terrible will happen. And it will be my fault."

She reported physical symptoms accompanying her worry: chronic muscle tension in her neck and shoulders (she sees a massage therapist weekly), difficulty falling asleep ("my brain won't shut up"), fatigue, and irritability. She has started avoiding social events because "they're too unpredictable" and has declined a destination bachelorette party because "what if the plane crashes?"

"My fiancé says I'm 'too much,'" she admitted, tears forming. "He loves me, but he's tired of reassuring me. And I'm tired of needing reassurance. I just want to feel... calm. For one day."

# Differential Diagnosis

## Panic Disorder

Why Consider: Sophie has anxiety symptoms and physical complaints.

Why Rule Out: Panic Disorder requires recurrent unexpected panic attacks --- sudden surges of intense fear with physical symptoms (palpitations, sweating, trembling, shortness of breath, chest pain, nausea, dizziness, derealization, fear of dying/going crazy). Sophie has never experienced a panic attack. Her anxiety is chronic and pervasive, not episodic and acute. While she worries about negative outcomes, she does not fear the panic attacks themselves, which is central to panic disorder.

## Social Anxiety Disorder

Why Consider: Sophie avoids some social events.

Why Rule Out: While Sophie avoids some social events, her avoidance is not driven by fear of social evaluation or embarrassment. She avoids social events because they are unpredictable and might trigger her generalized worry (travel, safety, logistics). She is not afraid of being judged, humiliated, or rejected in social situations --- she is afraid of uncertainty and negative outcomes broadly. Her anxiety is generalized across domains, not social-specific.

## Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder

Why Consider: Sophie does have some compulsive behaviors (checking the stove, triple-checking work).

Why Rule Out: Sophie does have some compulsive behaviors, but these are driven by generalized worry rather than obsessions. In OCD, compulsions are performed in response to specific intrusive thoughts, images, or urges (obsessions) that cause anxiety, and the compulsions are aimed at neutralizing or preventing the obsession-related feared outcome. Sophie's checking is generalized anxiety management, not tied to specific obsessional content. She does not have intrusive thoughts about contamination, harm, or order that she tries to neutralize. Her worry is about real-life concerns (albeit exaggerated), not ego-dystonic intrusive thoughts.

## Major Depressive Disorder

Why Consider: Sophie has distress and some avoidance behaviors.

Why Rule Out: Sophie has no depressed mood, anhedonia, or other core depressive symptoms. Her distress is anxiety-based.

## Adjustment Disorder with Anxiety

Why Consider: Sophie has symptoms that could be related to life stressors.

Why Rule Out: Symptoms are chronic (since childhood) and not tied to a specific stressor.

## ADHD

Why Consider: Sophie has concentration problems.

Why Rule Out: Concentration problems present but secondary to anxiety, not primary attention deficit. No childhood ADHD history.

## Hypothyroidism

Why Consider: Can cause anxiety and fatigue.

Why Rule Out: TSH is normal.

# Correct Diagnosis

**Generalized Anxiety Disorder**

## DSM-5-TR Explanation

Sophie meets full criteria for Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD):

## Diagnostic Reasoning

1. **Excessive anxiety and worry about multiple domains** --- work, relationships, health, safety, future, past events. The worry is not confined to specific situations.

2. **Individual finds it difficult to control the worry** --- she describes worry as "uncontrollable" and "like a hamster wheel."

3. **Anxiety and worry associated with 3+ symptoms** --- she has 6 associated symptoms: restlessness, fatigue, concentration difficulty, irritability, muscle tension, and sleep disturbance.

4. **Anxiety, worry, or physical symptoms cause clinically significant distress and impairment** --- occupational inefficiency, social avoidance, relationship strain, chronic physical symptoms.

5. **Disturbance not attributable to substance or medical condition** --- no substance use; medical conditions (IBS, tension headaches) are likely secondary to anxiety.

6. **Not better explained by another mental disorder** --- worry is not restricted to panic attacks (panic disorder), social evaluation (social anxiety), contamination/order (OCD), separation from attachment figures (separation anxiety), or reminders of trauma (PTSD).

## Required Criteria

- Criterion A: Excessive anxiety and worry (apprehensive expectation), occurring more days than not for at least 6 months, about a number of events or activities
- Criterion B: The individual finds it difficult to control the worry
- Criterion C: The anxiety and worry are associated with three (or more) of the following six symptoms (with at least some symptoms having been present for more days than not for the past 6 months): (1) restlessness or feeling keyed up or on edge, (2) being easily fatigued, (3) difficulty concentrating or mind going blank, (4) irritability, (5) muscle tension, (6) sleep disturbance
- Criterion D: The anxiety, worry, or physical symptoms cause clinically significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning
- Criterion E: The disturbance is not attributable to the physiological effects of a substance or another medical condition
- Criterion F: The disturbance is not better explained by another mental disorder

## Criteria Met

- Criterion A: Excessive worry about multiple domains (work, relationships, health, safety, future, past) for more days than not, 6+ months
- Criterion B: Difficulty controlling worry (describes as "uncontrollable")
- Criterion C: 6/6 associated symptoms met (restlessness, fatigue, concentration difficulty, irritability, muscle tension, sleep disturbance) --- minimum 3 required
- Criterion D: Significant distress and impairment (occupational inefficiency, social avoidance, relationship strain, chronic physical symptoms)
- Criterion E: Not attributable to substance or medical condition
- Criterion F: Not better explained by another mental disorder

## Criteria Not Met

# Treatment Plan

## Psychotherapy

- **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT):** Gold standard for GAD. Targets catastrophic thinking, worry beliefs ("worry keeps me safe"), and intolerance of uncertainty. Includes worry time scheduling, cognitive restructuring, and behavioral experiments.

- **Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT):** Helps reduce struggle against anxiety and increase valued action despite uncertainty.

- **Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR):** Reduces rumination and increases present-moment awareness.

## Medication

- **SSRIs** (e.g., escitalopram, sertraline) --- first-line pharmacotherapy for GAD.

- **SNRIs** (e.g., venlafaxine, duloxetine) --- equally effective; may be preferred if comorbid pain symptoms.

- **Buspirone** --- non-benzodiazepine anxiolytic; useful as adjunct or monotherapy.

- **Benzodiazepines** --- short-term only; risk of dependence and cognitive side effects.

- *Individual medication selection by prescribing clinician only.*

## Lifestyle

- Caffeine reduction (major anxiety trigger)
- Regular aerobic exercise
- Sleep hygiene
- Relaxation training (progressive muscle relaxation, diaphragmatic breathing)
- Worry journaling with scheduled "worry time"
- Gradual exposure to avoided situations (travel, social events)

## Prognosis

Good. GAD is highly responsive to CBT and SSRIs. Sophie has strong insight, no comorbid depression, and a supportive relationship. However, GAD tends to be chronic and relapsing; long-term management strategies are important. Her perfectionism and need for control may require ongoing work. Wedding planning is a current stressor that will resolve, but underlying worry pattern will persist without treatment.

# Common Student Mistakes

- Confusing GAD with OCD because of checking behaviors. Students see stove-checking and work triple-checking and immediately think OCD. However, the function of the behavior determines the diagnosis. In OCD, checking neutralizes a specific obsession. In GAD, checking reduces generalized uncertainty and worry.
- Missing GAD because the patient has no panic attacks. GAD does not require panic attacks; it requires chronic, pervasive worry.
- Attributing symptoms to "just stress" or "perfectionism" without recognizing a clinical syndrome.
- Failing to assess for physical symptoms (muscle tension, sleep disturbance, fatigue) that are required for diagnosis.

---

# Memory Tricks

- **"The disorder of what if"**: GAD patients are not worried about specific, realistic threats --- they are worried about infinite possible threats.
- **"SIG E CAPS for anxiety"**: Sleep, Irritability, Gastrointestinal (IBS), Energy (fatigue), Concentration, Anxiety (excessive), Physical (muscle tension), Social (avoidance) --- Sophie has all of these.
- **"Worry is the hamster wheel"**: The more you run, the faster it spins. Treatment doesn't stop the wheel; it helps you step off.
- **Think of an umbrella**: GAD is the umbrella that covers worry about everything --- unlike specific phobias (one raindrop) or social anxiety (one cloud).

# Exam Pearls

- GAD is often called the "disorder of what if." Patients like Sophie are not worried about specific, realistic threats --- they are worried about infinite possible threats.
- The cognitive hallmark is intolerance of uncertainty: the belief that uncertainty is dangerous and must be eliminated through worry and planning.
- Treatment does not aim to eliminate worry (impossible) but to change the relationship to worry and increase tolerance for uncertainty.
- The paradox of GAD is that worry feels productive and protective, but it actually maintains anxiety by preventing disconfirmation of catastrophic beliefs.
- The ancient Stoic philosopher Seneca wrote a treatise called "On Tranquility of Mind" that reads remarkably like a CBT manual for GAD. He described worry as "a state of slavery" where the mind is "tossed about by hope and fear," and recommended focusing on the present moment, accepting what cannot be controlled, and recognizing that anticipated suffering is often worse than actual suffering.

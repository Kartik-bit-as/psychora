# Overview

Ms. Clara Nguyen is a 23-year-old graduate student in an MFA Creative Writing program who presents with a lifetime of paralyzing fear in social situations. She describes feeling like "everyone is judging me, all the time" and has structured her entire life around avoidance of social evaluation. She avoids speaking in class, eats alone in her room rather than the cafeteria, has never attended a party or social gathering at her university, and has never been on a date. She froze completely during a required poetry presentation in workshop. She is considering dropping out of her MFA program because the workshop format --- reading work aloud and receiving critique --- is "torture." She has never experienced panic attacks outside social situations, and her anxiety is specifically tied to social evaluation, not generalized.

# Presenting Complaint

Ms. Clara Nguyen spoke so quietly that the clinician had to lean forward to hear her. She sat with her body angled away from the window, as if avoiding something outside, though the blinds were drawn.

"I know it doesn't make sense," she said, her voice barely above a whisper. "But I feel like everyone is judging me, all the time. When I walk into a room, I can feel their eyes. When I speak in class, my heart pounds so hard I think everyone can hear it. Last week, I had to present a poem in workshop, and I..." She paused, her face flushing crimson. "I froze. Completely. Couldn't speak. The professor was nice about it, but I wanted to die. I mean, literally. I wanted the floor to open up and swallow me."

Clara described a lifetime of paralyzing fear in social situations. She avoids speaking in class unless absolutely required, eats alone in her room rather than the cafeteria, and has never attended a party or social gathering at her university. She has never been on a date --- "the idea of someone looking at me, evaluating me, is unbearable."

"The worst part is that I want connection," she said, tears forming. "I want friends. I want to share my writing. I want to be seen. But the fear is stronger than the want. Every time I try, my body betrays me --- sweating, shaking, my mind goes blank. And then I replay the moment for weeks, analyzing everything I did wrong."

She reported that she dropped a required public speaking course last semester because she could not face the final presentation. She is considering dropping out of her MFA program entirely because the workshop format --- reading work aloud and receiving critique --- is "torture."

Clara's social anxiety has been present since childhood and has intensified during young adulthood.

**Onset:** First noticed around age 7--8. She remembers refusing to read aloud in class, hiding in bathroom during group activities, and being described by teachers as "painfully shy." Parents attributed it to temperament.

# Differential Diagnosis

## Generalized Anxiety Disorder

Why Consider: Clara worries excessively and has anxiety symptoms.

Why Rule Out: While Clara worries excessively, her worry is not generalized across multiple domains. She does not worry about finances, health, safety, or future catastrophes. Her anxiety is specifically and exclusively focused on social evaluation and scrutiny. GAD requires worry about multiple unrelated areas that is pervasive and not tied to specific situations. Clara is perfectly calm when alone or in non-evaluative contexts. Her anxiety is situation-specific, not generalized.

## Avoidant Personality Disorder

Why Consider: Avoidant Personality Disorder shares many features with social anxiety disorder --- social inhibition, feelings of inadequacy, hypersensitivity to negative evaluation, and avoidance of social situations.

Why Rule Out: Avoidant Personality Disorder shares many features with social anxiety disorder. In fact, there is significant diagnostic overlap, and some researchers view AvPD as a severe variant of social anxiety. However, social anxiety disorder is the more appropriate primary diagnosis when the social fear is the dominant and defining feature. Clara's presentation is better explained by social anxiety disorder --- her symptoms map cleanly onto the criteria without requiring the additional personality dysfunction (pervasive pattern across cognition, affectivity, interpersonal functioning, and impulse control) required for personality disorders. Additionally, personality disorders should not be diagnosed when symptoms are better explained by another mental disorder. Social anxiety is treatable; labeling a young person with a personality disorder can be stigmatizing and premature.

## Schizoid Personality Disorder

Why Consider: Clara avoids social situations and has limited social connections.

Why Rule Out: Schizoid Personality Disorder is characterized by detachment from social relationships and restricted emotional expression, with no desire for social connection. Clara wants social connection --- she wants friends, wants to share her writing, wants a romantic relationship. Her avoidance is driven by fear, not by lack of interest. This is the critical distinction: social anxiety = fear of social situations despite desire for connection; schizoid = indifference to social situations with no desire for connection. Clara's tears when describing her loneliness and her statement "I want to be seen" rule out schizoid personality disorder.

## Selective Mutism

Why Consider: Clara has difficulty speaking in social situations.

Why Rule Out: Occurs in children, typically limited to specific settings (school). Clara speaks in all settings but with extreme anxiety.

## Autism Spectrum Disorder

Why Consider: Clara has social difficulties and avoids social situations.

Why Rule Out: No social communication deficits, restricted interests, or repetitive behaviors characteristic of ASD. Clara understands social nuances but fears them.

## Body Dysmorphic Disorder

Why Consider: Clara is concerned about how she appears to others.

Why Rule Out: No preoccupation with perceived defects in appearance.

## Major Depressive Disorder

Why Consider: Clara has distress and tears during the interview.

Why Rule Out: No depressed mood, anhedonia, or other core depressive symptoms. Distress is anxiety-based.

## Performance-Only Social Anxiety

Why Consider: Clara's anxiety is prominent during performance situations.

Why Rule Out: Clara's anxiety extends beyond performance to general social interaction, so the "performance only" specifier does not apply.

# Correct Diagnosis

**Social Anxiety Disorder (Social Phobia)**

## DSM-5-TR Explanation

Clara meets full criteria for Social Anxiety Disorder (Social Phobia):

## Diagnostic Reasoning

1. **Marked fear or anxiety about one or more social situations** in which the individual is exposed to possible scrutiny by others --- speaking in class, reading work aloud, eating in public, meeting new people, attending social events.

2. **Fears that she will act in a way or show anxiety symptoms that will be negatively evaluated** --- fears of being seen as "boring," "weird," or inadequate.

3. **Social situations almost always provoke fear or anxiety** --- consistent across all social evaluation contexts.

4. **Social situations are avoided or endured with intense fear** --- extensive avoidance is her primary coping strategy.

5. **Fear or anxiety is out of proportion to the actual threat** --- she recognizes it is excessive but cannot control it.

6. **Persistent, typically ≥6 months** --- present for 15+ years.

7. **Causes clinically significant distress and impairment** --- threatens education, prevents relationships, restricts occupational options.

8. **Not attributable to substance or medical condition** --- no relevant medical conditions.

9. **Not better explained by another mental disorder** --- fear is not of panic attacks (panic disorder), social contamination (OCD), or body defects (body dysmorphic disorder).

## Required Criteria

- Criterion A: Marked fear or anxiety about one or more social situations in which the individual is exposed to possible scrutiny by others. The individual fears that he or she will act in a way or show anxiety symptoms that will be negatively evaluated

- Criterion C: Social situations are avoided or endured with intense fear or anxiety
- Criterion D: The fear or anxiety is out of proportion to the actual threat posed by the social situation and to the sociocultural context

- Criterion F: The fear, anxiety, or avoidance causes clinically significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning

# Treatment Plan

## Psychotherapy

- **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) with exposure:** Gold standard. Includes cognitive restructuring of negative self-beliefs and catastrophic predictions, and graduated exposure to feared social situations (starting with less threatening scenarios and progressing to more challenging ones).

- **Social Skills Training:** Often needed because extensive avoidance has prevented normal social skill development.

- **Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT):** Helps reduce struggle against anxiety and increase willingness to experience discomfort in service of valued goals (sharing writing, forming relationships).

- **Group CBT:** Paradoxically effective --- provides both exposure and peer support in a therapeutic context.

## Medication

- **SSRIs** (e.g., paroxetine, sertraline, escitalopram) --- first-line pharmacotherapy for social anxiety disorder.

- **SNRIs** (e.g., venlafaxine) --- equally effective.

- **Beta-blockers** (e.g., propranolol) --- useful for performance anxiety (public speaking, musical performance) but less effective for generalized social anxiety.

- **Benzodiazepines** --- not first-line due to dependence risk and interference with exposure therapy.

- *Individual medication selection by prescribing clinician only.*

## Lifestyle

- Graduated exposure hierarchy with therapist guidance
- Joining low-stakes social groups (book club, writing group) with structured interaction
- Practicing social skills in safe contexts
- Reducing safety behaviors (avoiding eye contact, rehearsing conversations excessively, wearing bland clothing to avoid attention)
- Regular exercise (reduces physiological anxiety symptoms)
- Limiting caffeine

## Prognosis

Good with appropriate treatment. Social anxiety disorder is highly responsive to CBT and SSRIs. However, Clara's 15-year history of extensive avoidance means she has missed critical developmental experiences (dating, close friendships, professional networking) that will require remedial skill-building. The longer avoidance persists, the more entrenched the beliefs become. Early intensive intervention is essential. Her insight and motivation are strong protective factors. The MFA workshop format, while currently terrifying, could become a powerful therapeutic context if she engages in exposure-based treatment.

# Common Student Mistakes

- Confusing social anxiety with introversion or shyness. Students see someone who avoids social situations and assumes they "just don't like people." But social anxiety is fear-driven, not preference-driven. The critical question is: "If you could wave a magic wand and eliminate your anxiety, would you want to go to the party/give the presentation/go on the date?" If the answer is yes, it's social anxiety. If the answer is no, it's introversion.
- Missing social anxiety because the patient is academically successful. Clara has a high GPA and was accepted to an MFA program --- but she achieved this entirely through written work and avoidance of participation.
- Diagnosing avoidant personality disorder prematurely in young adults. Social anxiety disorder should be the primary diagnosis when symptoms map cleanly onto the criteria.
- Overlooking the "performance only" specifier when it doesn't apply. Clara's anxiety extends to all social evaluation, not just performance situations.

# Memory Tricks

- **"Fear of the spotlight"**: Social anxiety is specifically about being seen and evaluated --- not about general danger or uncertainty.
- **"Want but can't"**: The hallmark of social anxiety is wanting connection but being too afraid to pursue it. Introverts don't want the party; social anxiety patients do.
- **"The 7% rule"**: Social anxiety affects approximately 7% of the population --- making it one of the most common psychiatric conditions worldwide, yet the average person waits 10 years before seeking treatment.
- Think of a turtle: Social anxiety patients want to come out of their shell (connection) but are too afraid of what might happen if they do.

# Exam Pearls

- Social anxiety disorder is the most common anxiety disorder but one of the least treated. Patients like Clara often suffer in silence for years, labeled as "shy" or "introverted," while their lives shrink around avoidance.
- The diagnostic key is not the presence of anxiety in social situations (everyone experiences this) but the severity, persistence, and functional impact.
- Ask: "Does this person want social connection but fear it?" If yes, think social anxiety.
- Remember: introversion is a preference for solitude; social anxiety is a fear of social evaluation. Clara is not an introvert --- she is a terrified extrovert.
- The term "social phobia" was replaced with "social anxiety disorder" in DSM-IV to reduce stigma and better capture the pervasive nature of the condition. However, some researchers argue "phobia" was more accurate because it conveyed the intense, irrational fear component.

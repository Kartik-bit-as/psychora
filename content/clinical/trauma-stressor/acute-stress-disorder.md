# Overview

Ms. Danielle "Dani" Williams, a 26-year-old bank teller, presents with acute trauma-related symptoms following an armed robbery at her bank branch 5 days ago. The case illustrates the temporal boundary between acute stress disorder (symptoms lasting 3 days to 1 month) and posttraumatic stress disorder (symptoms lasting more than 1 month), highlighting the importance of early identification and intervention during the acute window.

---

# Learning Objectives

- Identify the symptom criteria for acute stress disorder and distinguish them from PTSD based on duration

- Understand the prognostic significance of acute stress disorder as a risk factor for PTSD
- Apply the 3-day to 1-month duration criterion for acute stress disorder
- Identify the protective factors and early interventions that may prevent progression to PTSD

# Presenting Complaint

Ms. Danielle "Dani" Williams presented to the employee assistance counselor 5 days after experiencing an armed robbery at her bank branch. She arrived at the appointment trembling, with visible dark circles under her eyes, and clutching a cup of coffee with both hands. She had difficulty sitting still and jumped visibly when a car horn sounded outside the building.

Dani explained that 5 days ago, a masked man entered the bank where she works, brandished a handgun, and demanded money from her drawer. She described the event in fragmented, vivid detail: the black ski mask with eye holes that seemed "too big and empty," the smell of cigarette smoke on his jacket, the click of the gun's hammer as he racked it, the way her hands shook so badly she couldn't open the cash drawer at first, and the feeling that "I was going to die right there on the floor." The robbery lasted approximately 4 minutes, after which the man fled. No one was physically injured.

Since that day, Dani has been unable to return to work. She has recurrent, intrusive memories of the robbery that intrude dozens of times per day—while eating, showering, trying to read, or walking down the street. She has nightmares about the gunman every night, waking up screaming and drenched in sweat. She feels detached from her surroundings, as if she is "watching my life on a screen." She has difficulty concentrating and finds herself staring at walls for long periods.

Dani is avoiding the neighborhood where the bank is located and has changed her daily commute to avoid passing any bank branches. She refuses to watch television news because crime reports trigger intense anxiety. She has called in sick to work every day since the robbery and is terrified of returning. She describes feeling "on edge" constantly, startling at sudden noises, and being unable to relax even in her own apartment. She has not been eating well and has lost 5 pounds in 5 days.

# Differential Diagnosis

## Posttraumatic Stress Disorder

Why Consider: Dani's symptom picture is consistent with what may develop into PTSD, including re-experiencing, avoidance, negative mood, dissociation, and hyperarousal.

Why Rule Out: PTSD requires symptoms to persist for more than one month after trauma exposure. Dani's symptoms have only been present for 5 days. While her symptom picture is consistent with what may develop into PTSD, the current duration falls within the acute stress disorder window. ASD is essentially a time-limited diagnosis that may evolve into PTSD if symptoms persist beyond one month.

---

## Panic Disorder

Why Consider: Dani experienced acute panic during the robbery and has ongoing anxiety with physical symptoms.

Why Rule Out: While Dani experienced acute panic during the robbery and has ongoing anxiety, her symptoms are specifically trauma-related and include the full spectrum of intrusion, avoidance, negative mood, dissociation, and hyperarousal. Panic disorder involves unexpected panic attacks and persistent concern about having additional attacks, not a specific trauma-related symptom cluster. Dani's anxiety is clearly tied to the robbery and its reminders, not unexpected or spontaneous.

---

## Adjustment Disorder

Why Consider: Dani is experiencing significant emotional and behavioral symptoms in response to a stressful event.

Why Rule Out: Adjustment disorder involves emotional or behavioral symptoms in response to an identifiable stressor that are out of proportion to the severity of the stressor. An armed robbery with a gun is an extreme trauma that exceeds the scope of stressors typically associated with adjustment disorder. Additionally, Dani's symptoms meet the specific criteria for a trauma-related disorder (intrusion, avoidance, dissociation, hyperarousal) rather than the more general distress seen in adjustment disorder.

---

## Brief Psychotic Disorder

Why Consider: Dani has experienced a traumatic event and has some unusual experiences (feeling detached from surroundings).

Why Rule Out: No psychotic symptoms (hallucinations, delusions, disorganized speech/behavior); dissociative symptoms are non-psychotic. Dani's insight is good, and her symptoms are clearly trauma-related rather than psychotic in nature.

# Correct Diagnosis

Acute Stress Disorder

DSM-5-TR Explanation: Acute stress disorder is characterized by the development of characteristic symptoms lasting from 3 days to 1 month after exposure to one or more traumatic events. The traumatic event involves actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence. The individual experiences intrusion symptoms, negative mood, dissociative symptoms, avoidance symptoms, and arousal symptoms. The disturbance causes clinically significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning. The disturbance is not attributable to the physiological effects of a substance or another medical condition and is not better explained by brief psychotic disorder.

Diagnostic Reasoning: Dani was exposed to actual threatened serious injury (armed robbery with a gun) and developed a characteristic symptom picture that began immediately after the trauma and has persisted for 5 days. She exhibits 9 symptoms from any of the five categories: intrusive memories (Category B), negative mood (Category C), dissociative symptoms (derealization—Category D), avoidance symptoms (Category E), and arousal symptoms (hypervigilance, startle, sleep disturbance—Category F). The duration is between 3 days and one month after trauma exposure, which is the specific duration criterion for acute stress disorder. The symptoms cause significant distress and impairment in occupational and social functioning.

Required Criteria:
A. Exposure to actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence.
B. Presence of nine (or more) of the following symptoms from any of the five categories:
   Intrusion:
   1. Recurrent, involuntary, and intrusive distressing memories of the traumatic event(s)
   2. Recurrent distressing dreams in which the content and/or affect of the dream are related to the traumatic event(s)
   3. Dissociative reactions (e.g., flashbacks) in which the individual feels or acts as if the traumatic event(s) were recurring
   4. Intense or prolonged psychological distress or marked physiological reactions in response to internal or external cues that symbolize or resemble an aspect of the traumatic event(s)
   Negative Mood:
   5. Persistent inability to experience positive emotions
   Dissociative:
   6. An altered sense of the reality of one's surroundings or oneself (e.g., seeing oneself from another's perspective, being in a daze, time slowing)
   7. Inability to remember an important aspect of the traumatic event(s) (typically due to dissociative amnesia)
   Avoidance:
   8. Efforts to avoid distressing memories, thoughts, or feelings about or closely associated with the traumatic event(s)
   9. Efforts to avoid external reminders (people, places, conversations, activities, objects, situations) that arouse distressing memories, thoughts, or feelings about or closely associated with the traumatic event(s)
   Arousal:
   10. Sleep disturbance
   11. Irritable behavior and angry outbursts
   12. Hypervigilance
   13. Problems with concentration
   14. Exaggerated startle response
C. Duration of the disturbance (symptoms in Criterion B) is 3 days to 1 month after trauma exposure.
D. The disturbance causes clinically significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning.
E. The disturbance is not attributable to the physiological effects of a substance or another medical condition and is not better explained by brief psychotic disorder.

Criteria Met: A, B (symptoms 1, 2, 4, 5, 6, 8, 9, 10, 12), C, D, E

# Treatment Plan

## Psychotherapy

Brief trauma-focused intervention, including psychoeducation about normal trauma reactions, cognitive processing of the event, and early exposure to avoided situations (e.g., returning to the bank neighborhood, discussing the event). Trauma-Focused CBT adapted for acute settings is effective in preventing progression to PTSD. Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR) may also be used in the acute phase.

## Medication

Short-term use of benzodiazepines may be considered for severe acute anxiety and sleep disruption, though caution is warranted due to dependence risk. SSRIs are generally not initiated in the acute phase unless symptoms are severe and expected to persist. Beta-blockers (e.g., propranolol) have been studied for acute trauma but evidence is mixed.

## Lifestyle

Maintain regular sleep schedule; resume normal daily routines as soon as possible; avoid alcohol and caffeine; engage in physical activity; maintain social connections; return to work with appropriate support and accommodations; limit media exposure to crime-related content.

## Prognosis

Generally good with early intervention. Many individuals with acute stress disorder recover without developing PTSD. Risk factors for progression to PTSD include severe dissociation during or after the trauma, prior trauma history, lack of social support, and ongoing life stressors. Early return to normal functioning and processing of the trauma are protective factors.

# Common Student Mistakes

- Diagnosing acute trauma reactions as PTSD without considering the duration criterion. The critical distinction is that ASD lasts between 3 days and one month, while PTSD requires symptoms to persist beyond one month.
- Missing ASD entirely by attributing symptoms to "normal stress" or adjustment disorder, failing to recognize the specific trauma-related symptom cluster.
- Confusing dissociative symptoms with psychosis. Dissociation in ASD is a non-psychotic symptom characterized by feeling detached from oneself or one's surroundings, not by hallucinations or delusions.
- Failing to recognize that early intervention during the ASD window can significantly reduce the risk of progression to PTSD.

---

# Memory Tricks

- **A-S-D**: **A**cute onset (within days), **S**hort duration (3 days to 1 month), **D**issociative symptoms are characteristic
- Think of a storm warning—ASD is the warning period (3 days to 1 month) after a traumatic "storm." If the storm clouds don't clear after a month, the warning upgrades to PTSD.
- Research suggests that approximately 50% of individuals who meet criteria for acute stress disorder will go on to develop PTSD if not treated. However, early psychological intervention during the ASD window can reduce this risk significantly, making early identification and treatment critical.

---

# Exam Pearls

- Acute stress disorder is not simply "normal shock"—it is a clinical diagnosis that identifies individuals at high risk for developing PTSD. Early intervention during the ASD window can prevent progression to chronic PTSD.
- The presence of dissociative symptoms during or immediately after trauma is a particularly strong predictor of PTSD development. Dani's derealization ("watching my life on a screen") is a significant prognostic indicator.
- The 3-day minimum duration is important to distinguish ASD from normal acute stress reactions, which typically resolve within the first 48-72 hours.
- Early return to normal functioning, including work, is a protective factor against PTSD development. However, this should be done with appropriate support and accommodations, not by forcing the individual to "tough it out."

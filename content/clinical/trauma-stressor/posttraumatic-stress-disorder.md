# Overview

Mr. James "Jimmy" O'Brien, a 38-year-old firefighter/paramedic with 15 years of service, presents with a 10-month history of severe trauma-related symptoms following a fatal residential fire in which he discovered a deceased 6-year-old girl. The case illustrates the full four-cluster symptom picture of PTSD (re-experiencing, avoidance, negative alterations in cognition and mood, and alterations in arousal and reactivity) in a first responder, highlighting the distinction from acute stress disorder, major depressive disorder, and adjustment disorder.

---

# Learning Objectives

- Identify the four symptom clusters of PTSD: re-experiencing, avoidance, negative alterations in cognition and mood, and alterations in arousal and reactivity
- Distinguish PTSD from acute stress disorder based on duration criteria
- Differentiate PTSD from major depressive disorder and adjustment disorder
- Recognize the occupational and cultural barriers to seeking help among first responders
- Understand the role of comorbid substance use in trauma-related presentations

# Presenting Complaint

Mr. James "Jimmy" O'Brien was referred to the trauma clinic by his fire department's employee assistance program after his captain noticed he was "not himself" and had made two critical errors during recent calls. Jimmy arrived at the appointment reluctantly, at his wife's insistence, and sat with his back to the wall, scanning the room repeatedly. He appeared exhausted, with bloodshot eyes and visible tremors in his hands.

Jimmy explained that 10 months ago, he responded to a residential fire in a low-income apartment building. He was the first firefighter to enter the unit and discovered a 6-year-old girl trapped under a collapsed beam. He managed to free her and began CPR, but she died at the scene despite his efforts. He describes the event in vivid, fragmented detail: the smell of burning fabric, the child's blue-tinged skin, her mother's screams from the street below, the feeling of her small ribs cracking under his chest compressions.

Since that night, Jimmy has been a different person. He has recurrent, intrusive memories of the event that burst into his consciousness without warning—while driving, during meals, in the shower. He has nightmares about the fire almost every night, often waking up screaming and sweating. He refuses to talk about the event with anyone, including his wife, and becomes enraged when she asks if he is okay. He has stopped attending his children's school events because fire drills trigger intense anxiety. He no longer watches television because emergency scenes on shows cause flashbacks.

Jimmy describes feeling emotionally numb, as if he is "walking through a dream." He has lost interest in activities he previously enjoyed—fishing, coaching his son's baseball team, playing guitar. He feels detached from his wife and children, unable to experience love or joy. He is hypervigilant constantly: he checks locks repeatedly, sits with his back to walls in restaurants, startles violently at sudden noises, and drives aggressively, convinced that other drivers are going to cause accidents. He has begun drinking 4-6 beers every night to "shut my brain off" and has started taking overtime shifts to avoid being home, where he feels "trapped with my thoughts."

# Differential Diagnosis

## Acute Stress Disorder

Why Consider: Jimmy experienced a traumatic event and developed symptoms immediately afterward, including intrusion, avoidance, negative mood, and hyperarousal.

Why Rule Out: Acute stress disorder requires symptoms to last between 3 days and one month after trauma exposure. Jimmy's symptoms have persisted for 10 months, far exceeding the one-month duration criterion for acute stress disorder. ASD is a precursor diagnosis that may evolve into PTSD, but the duration here clearly supports PTSD.

---

## Major Depressive Disorder

Why Consider: Jimmy has depressive symptoms including emotional numbing, anhedonia, hopelessness, and passive suicidal thoughts.

Why Rule Out: While Jimmy has depressive symptoms (emotional numbing, anhedonia, hopelessness, passive suicidal thoughts), these are embedded within a broader trauma-related symptom picture. The presence of re-experiencing, avoidance, and hyperarousal symptoms, along with the clear trauma exposure, points to PTSD as the primary diagnosis. The depressive symptoms are secondary to the trauma and better explained by PTSD's negative alterations in cognition and mood cluster.

---

## Adjustment Disorder

Why Consider: Jimmy is experiencing significant emotional and behavioral symptoms in response to a stressful event.

Why Rule Out: Adjustment disorder involves emotional or behavioral symptoms in response to an identifiable stressor, but the symptoms do not meet criteria for another specific disorder and are not of the severity or specificity seen here. Jimmy's symptoms are severe, pervasive, and meet full criteria for PTSD. The trauma (witnessing a child's death) is far beyond the scope of "normal" stressors that typically trigger adjustment disorder, and the symptom profile is distinctly trauma-related rather than a general stress reaction.

---

## Substance-Induced Disorder

Why Consider: Jimmy has significantly increased his alcohol consumption, and substance use can cause or exacerbate psychiatric symptoms.

Why Rule Out: Alcohol use is secondary to PTSD symptoms (self-medication); symptoms preceded alcohol escalation and persist beyond periods of intoxication. The trauma-related symptom picture is clearly primary, and the alcohol use is a maladaptive coping mechanism that emerged after the trauma symptoms.

# Correct Diagnosis

Posttraumatic Stress Disorder

DSM-5-TR Explanation: Posttraumatic stress disorder is characterized by exposure to actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence, followed by the development of characteristic symptoms including intrusive symptoms associated with the traumatic event, persistent avoidance of stimuli associated with the traumatic event, negative alterations in cognitions and mood associated with the traumatic event, and marked alterations in arousal and reactivity associated with the traumatic event. The duration of the disturbance is more than 1 month. The disturbance causes clinically significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning.

Diagnostic Reasoning: Jimmy meets full criteria for PTSD. He was exposed to actual death (the child's death in the fire) through direct exposure as a first responder. He exhibits persistent re-experiencing symptoms (recurrent intrusive memories, nightmares, flashbacks, intense distress at trauma reminders). He demonstrates persistent avoidance of both internal reminders (memories, thoughts, feelings about the event) and external reminders (fire drills, TV emergency scenes, firehouse events). He shows negative alterations in cognition and mood (emotional numbing, detachment from family, inability to experience positive emotions, persistent negative emotional state). He exhibits marked alterations in arousal and reactivity (hypervigilance, exaggerated startle response, irritability, reckless behavior through overtime and alcohol use). The duration exceeds one month (10 months), and the symptoms cause clinically significant distress and impairment in occupational, social, and relational functioning.

Required Criteria:
A. Exposure to actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence:
   1. Direct exposure
   2. Witnessing, in person, the event(s) as it occurred to others
   3. Learning that the traumatic event(s) occurred to a close family member or close friend
   4. Experiencing repeated or extreme exposure to aversive details of the traumatic event(s)
B. Presence of one (or more) of the following intrusion symptoms associated with the traumatic event(s):
   1. Recurrent, involuntary, and intrusive distressing memories of the traumatic event(s)
   2. Recurrent distressing dreams in which the content and/or affect of the dream are related to the traumatic event(s)
   3. Dissociative reactions (e.g., flashbacks) in which the individual feels or acts as if the traumatic event(s) were recurring
   4. Intense or prolonged psychological distress at exposure to internal or external cues that symbolize or resemble an aspect of the traumatic event(s)
   5. Marked physiological reactions to internal or external cues that symbolize or resemble an aspect of the traumatic event(s)
C. Persistent avoidance of stimuli associated with the traumatic event(s):
   1. Avoidance of or efforts to avoid distressing memories, thoughts, or feelings about or closely associated with the traumatic event(s)
   2. Avoidance of or efforts to avoid external reminders (people, places, conversations, activities, objects, situations) that arouse distressing memories, thoughts, or feelings about or closely associated with the traumatic event(s)
D. Negative alterations in cognitions and mood associated with the traumatic event(s):
   1. Inability to remember an important aspect of the traumatic event(s)
   2. Persistent and exaggerated negative beliefs or expectations about oneself, others, or the world
   3. Persistent, distorted cognitions about the cause or consequences of the traumatic event(s)
   4. Persistent negative emotional state
   5. Markedly diminished interest or participation in significant activities
   6. Feelings of detachment or estrangement from others
   7. Persistent inability to experience positive emotions
E. Marked alterations in arousal and reactivity associated with the traumatic event(s):
   1. Irritable behavior and angry outbursts
   2. Reckless or self-destructive behavior
   3. Hypervigilance
   4. Exaggerated startle response
   5. Problems with concentration
   6. Sleep disturbance
F. Duration of the disturbance (Criteria B, C, D, and E) is more than 1 month.
G. The disturbance causes clinically significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning.
H. The disturbance is not attributable to the physiological effects of a substance or another medical condition.

Criteria Met: A (direct exposure as first responder), B (1, 2, 4, 5), C (1, 2), D (4, 5, 6, 7), E (1, 2, 3, 4, 6), F, G, H

Criteria Not Met: D (1, 2, 3)

# Treatment Plan

## Psychotherapy

Trauma-Focused Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (TF-CBT) or Prolonged Exposure (PE) therapy. PE involves imaginal exposure (revisiting the trauma memory in a safe therapeutic setting) and in vivo exposure (gradually approaching avoided situations). Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT) is another evidence-based approach that targets trauma-related cognitions (e.g., Jimmy's self-blame: "I should have saved her"). Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR) is also effective. Treatment should address the emerging alcohol use disorder concurrently.

## Medication

SSRIs (sertraline and paroxetine are FDA-approved for PTSD) are first-line pharmacological treatment. Prazosin may be added specifically for trauma-related nightmares and sleep disruption. Avoid benzodiazepines due to interaction with alcohol and potential for dependence.

## Lifestyle

Sleep hygiene improvement and treatment of nightmares; gradual re-engagement with family activities; reduction of alcohol use (may require substance use treatment); physical exercise (with caution given hypertension); peer support through fire department or first responder support groups; mindfulness and grounding techniques for hyperarousal; vocational counseling regarding return to full duty.

## Prognosis

Moderate to good with appropriate trauma-focused psychotherapy and possibly SSRI treatment. First responders often have complex PTSD presentations with occupational implications. Comorbid alcohol use disorder may complicate treatment. Early intervention improves outcomes, but chronic PTSD can develop if untreated. Risk of suicide requires ongoing monitoring.

# Common Student Mistakes

- Diagnosing PTSD as depression or adjustment disorder, focusing on the mood symptoms while missing the full trauma-related symptom picture. The critical diagnostic requirement is the presence of all four symptom clusters (re-experiencing, avoidance, negative alterations in cognition/mood, and alterations in arousal/reactivity) following exposure to actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence.
- Missing the diagnosis by attributing symptoms to acute stress disorder without considering the duration criterion (symptoms have persisted for 10 months, far exceeding the 1-month ASD window).
- Attributing symptoms to a substance-induced disorder without recognizing that the alcohol use is secondary to the trauma symptoms (self-medication).
- Failing to recognize the occupational and cultural barriers to seeking help among first responders, which can delay diagnosis and treatment.

# Memory Tricks

- **P-T-S-D**: **P**ersistent re-experiencing, **T**rauma exposure required, **S**ymptom clusters are four, **D**uration exceeds one month
- Think of a broken record that keeps skipping back to the same traumatic moment—Jimmy's mind keeps replaying the fire, and he can't move the needle forward.
- PTSD was officially recognized as a diagnosis in DSM-III (1980), largely due to advocacy by Vietnam veterans and their clinicians. Prior to this, trauma-related symptoms were variously described as "shell shock," "combat fatigue," or "war neurosis," but were not systematically categorized.

# Exam Pearls

- PTSD in first responders is often underdiagnosed and undertreated due to cultural stigma around seeking mental health help. The "tough it out" mentality in firefighting and similar professions leads many to suffer in silence until symptoms cause critical errors or relationship breakdown. Screening for PTSD should be routine after traumatic calls.
- The four symptom clusters of PTSD must all be present for a diagnosis: (1) intrusion, (2) avoidance, (3) negative alterations in cognition and mood, and (4) alterations in arousal and reactivity. Missing any cluster may lead to underdiagnosis.
- Comorbid substance use is extremely common in PTSD and should be addressed concurrently, not sequentially. Alcohol use as self-medication can worsen PTSD symptoms and interfere with treatment.
- First responders may experience moral injury (distress from actions taken or not taken during the trauma) in addition to PTSD symptoms. Jimmy's self-blame ("I should have saved her") is a form of moral injury that requires targeted intervention.

# Overview

Mr. Tyler Brennan, a 19-year-old college sophomore, presents with a 4-month history of emotional and behavioral symptoms following a clear, identifiable psychosocial stressor: the loss of his athletic scholarship due to a severe sports injury. The case illustrates the concept of subthreshold, stressor-linked distress that causes significant impairment but does not meet criteria for a more specific disorder, highlighting the distinction from major depressive disorder, PTSD, and normal stress reactions.

---

# Learning Objectives

- Identify the core features of adjustment disorder: emotional or behavioral symptoms in response to an identifiable stressor
- Distinguish adjustment disorder from major depressive disorder, PTSD, and normal stress reactions
- Apply the temporal criterion (symptoms within 3 months of stressor onset)
- Recognize the significance of adjustment disorder as a common but often overlooked diagnosis
- Understand the excellent prognosis of adjustment disorder with brief, focused intervention

# Presenting Complaint

Mr. Tyler Brennan presented to the university counseling center after his academic advisor referred him due to failing grades and a concerning email he sent stating he "might not be cut out for college." Tyler arrived at the appointment wearing wrinkled clothes, with unkempt hair, and appeared lethargic. He slouched in his chair and spoke in a monotone, frequently shrugging and saying "I don't know" in response to questions.

Tyler explained that he entered college on a full athletic scholarship for soccer, having been a star player in high school with dreams of playing professionally. During his freshman year, he balanced academics and athletics successfully, maintaining a 3.2 GPA while starting on the varsity team. However, 4 months ago, during a preseason scrimmage, he suffered a severe ACL tear and meniscus damage requiring surgical repair. He was told he would miss the entire season and that his scholarship was in jeopardy if he could not return to play.

Since the injury, Tyler's life has unraveled. He was cut from the team roster due to the injury, and his scholarship was revoked 2 months ago because he could no longer fulfill the athletic requirement. He now faces $35,000 in annual tuition he cannot afford, and his parents have told him they cannot help financially. He failed two classes last semester (his first academic failures ever) and is on academic probation. He stopped attending soccer team social events and has distanced himself from his former teammates, saying he "can't stand to be around them anymore."

Tyler reports feeling sad "most of the time" and has lost interest in activities he previously enjoyed—playing video games with friends, going to the gym, attending parties. He has difficulty concentrating on schoolwork and finds himself ruminating about his injury and lost future. His sleep has become irregular—he stays up until 3 AM scrolling on his phone and then sleeps through morning classes. His appetite has decreased, and he has lost 10 pounds in 2 months. He denies suicidal thoughts but says he "doesn't see the point in anything right now."

# Differential Diagnosis

## Major Depressive Disorder

Why Consider: Tyler has depressive symptoms including depressed mood, loss of interest, sleep disturbance, appetite change, difficulty concentrating, and feelings of hopelessness.

Why Rule Out: While Tyler has depressive symptoms, they are directly tied to the stressor and have not persisted long enough (4 months) or with sufficient severity to meet criteria for major depressive disorder. In MDD, symptoms would be more pervasive, persistent, and not so clearly linked to a specific stressor. Additionally, Tyler's symptoms do not meet the full criteria for a major depressive episode (e.g., no psychomotor agitation or retardation, no feelings of excessive guilt or worthlessness beyond what is contextually appropriate, no suicidal ideation).

---

## Posttraumatic Stress Disorder

Why Consider: Tyler experienced a significant injury and has developed emotional and behavioral symptoms in response.

Why Rule Out: The stressor (sports injury and loss of scholarship) does not involve actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence. While the injury was significant, it was not life-threatening, and the primary distress is about the psychosocial consequences (loss of identity, financial crisis, academic failure), not about a traumatic event. Tyler does not exhibit the full PTSD symptom cluster (no re-experiencing, no avoidance of trauma reminders, no hyperarousal in the PTSD sense).

---

## Normal Stress Reaction

Why Consider: Tyler is experiencing a normal reaction to a significant life stressor.

Why Rule Out: While stress reactions are normal, Tyler's symptoms have caused significant impairment in academic and social functioning (failing classes, academic probation, social isolation, weight loss, sleep disruption). A "normal" stress reaction would not typically result in such marked functional decline. The diagnosis of adjustment disorder acknowledges that the reaction is clinically significant and warrants intervention, even if it does not meet criteria for a more severe disorder.

---

## Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia)

Why Consider: Tyler has persistent depressive symptoms lasting several months.

Why Rule Out: Duration is only 4 months; symptoms are acute and stressor-linked, not chronic. Persistent depressive disorder requires symptoms to persist for at least 2 years in adults, which is not the case here.

# Correct Diagnosis

Adjustment Disorder with Depressed Mood

DSM-5-TR Explanation: Adjustment disorders are characterized by the development of emotional or behavioral symptoms in response to an identifiable stressor(s) occurring within 3 months of the onset of the stressor(s). These symptoms or behaviors are clinically significant, as evidenced by one or both of the following: (1) marked distress that is out of proportion to the severity or intensity of the stressor, and (2) significant impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning. The stress-related disturbance does not meet the criteria for another mental disorder and is not merely an exacerbation of a preexisting mental disorder. Once the stressor or its consequences have terminated, the symptoms do not persist for more than an additional 6 months.

Diagnostic Reasoning: Tyler developed emotional and behavioral symptoms (depressed mood, loss of interest, social withdrawal, academic failure, sleep and appetite disturbance) within 3 months of an identifiable psychosocial stressor (the sports injury, loss of scholarship, and resulting financial and identity crisis). The distress is out of proportion to the severity of the stressor (given his resilience and prior functioning) and has caused significant impairment in social (withdrawal from friends) and occupational/academic (failing classes, academic probation) functioning. The symptoms do not meet criteria for another specific disorder: they are not severe enough, persistent enough, or independent enough from the stressor to qualify as major depressive disorder, and the stressor is not a life-threatening trauma that would support PTSD or acute stress disorder.

Required Criteria:
A. The development of emotional or behavioral symptoms in response to an identifiable stressor(s) occurring within 3 months of the onset of the stressor(s).
B. These symptoms or behaviors are clinically significant, as evidenced by one or both of the following:
   1. Marked distress that is out of proportion to the severity or intensity of the stressor, taking into account the external context and the cultural factors that might influence symptom severity and presentation.
   2. Significant impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning.
C. The stress-related disturbance does not meet the criteria for another mental disorder and is not merely an exacerbation of a preexisting mental disorder.
D. The symptoms do not represent normal bereavement.
E. Once the stressor or its consequences have terminated, the symptoms do not persist for more than an additional 6 months.

Criteria Met: A, B (1 and 2), C, E

Criteria Not Met: D (not applicable—stressor is not bereavement)

Specifier: With depressed mood

# Treatment Plan

## Psychotherapy

Brief, problem-focused psychotherapy is the treatment of choice for adjustment disorders. Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) can help Tyler reframe catastrophic thoughts about his future, develop coping strategies for the loss of his athletic identity, and address academic and financial planning. Solution-Focused Brief Therapy (SFBT) may help him identify strengths and resources to navigate the current crisis. Supportive counseling and psychoeducation about normal reactions to loss and transition are also beneficial.

## Medication

Medication is generally not first-line for adjustment disorders. Short-term use of low-dose SSRIs or benzodiazepines may be considered if anxiety or sleep disturbance is severe and impairing functioning, but psychotherapy is preferred. If symptoms persist and evolve into major depressive disorder, antidepressant medication would be indicated.

## Lifestyle

Re-engagement in physical activity appropriate for his injury recovery; establishment of a regular sleep schedule; academic support services (tutoring, disability accommodations if applicable); financial aid counseling; exploration of alternative career paths and identities outside of athletics; reconnection with social support network; structured daily routine.

## Prognosis

Excellent with appropriate brief intervention. Adjustment disorders are typically time-limited and resolve when the stressor is removed, the individual adapts to the stressor, or effective coping strategies are developed. Tyler's prior resilience, good premorbid functioning, and absence of comorbidities are positive prognostic factors. Risk of progression to major depressive disorder exists if the stressor persists and coping remains maladaptive.

# Common Student Mistakes

- Overdiagnosing adjustment disorder as major depressive disorder because of the presence of depressive symptoms. The critical distinction is that adjustment disorder symptoms are specifically linked to an identifiable stressor, do not meet full criteria for another disorder, and occur within 3 months of the stressor.
- Underdiagnosing adjustment disorder by dismissing significant distress as "normal stress," missing the opportunity for early intervention that can prevent progression to more severe disorders.
- Confusing adjustment disorder with PTSD by not carefully evaluating whether the stressor meets the trauma criterion (actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence). A sports injury, while significant, does not meet this criterion.
- Failing to recognize that adjustment disorders carry a significant risk of suicide and can progress to more severe disorders if untreated.

# Memory Tricks

- **A-D-J-U-S-T**: **A**cute stressor identified, **D**istress is out of proportion, **J**ust within 3 months of onset, **U**sual functioning impaired, **S**ubthreshold for other disorders, **T**ime-limited and treatable
- Think of a tree branch bending under the weight of snow—adjustment disorder is when the branch bends significantly but hasn't broken (major depressive disorder) and the snow isn't an avalanche (PTSD). With support, the branch can spring back.
- Adjustment disorders are sometimes called "the common cold of psychiatry" because they are so prevalent in clinical practice. Studies suggest they may account for up to 20% of outpatient mental health visits and are particularly common in medical settings, where patients struggle to adapt to illness, injury, or disability.

---

# Exam Pearls

- Adjustment disorders are among the most common diagnoses in clinical practice but are often overlooked because they are seen as "less serious" than other disorders. However, they carry a significant risk of suicide and can progress to more severe disorders if untreated. They represent a critical intervention window where brief, focused treatment can prevent chronicity.
- The temporal criterion is essential: symptoms must develop within 3 months of the stressor onset. If symptoms begin more than 3 months after the stressor, consider other diagnoses.
- The "out of proportion" criterion requires considering the individual's context, cultural factors, and premorbid functioning. What might be a normal reaction for one person may be pathological for another.
- Adjustment disorder symptoms should not meet full criteria for another disorder. If they do, the other disorder takes precedence. However, adjustment disorder can coexist with other disorders if the stressor exacerbates a preexisting condition.
